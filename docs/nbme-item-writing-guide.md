The purpose of this manual is to help faculty members across the health professions improve the quality of the multiple-choice items they write for their examinations, and to help them better understand the nature of item development, item analysis, and some basics of psychometrics and measurement. ## Chapter 1: Introduction

### Assessment: An Important Component of Instruction

Assessment (testing) is a critical component of instruction. When properly used, it can aid in accomplishing key curricular goals:

- **Communicates importance:** Tests are a powerful motivator; students will learn the educational concepts they believe you value.
- **Fills instructional gaps:** Encourages students to read broadly on their own and participate more as educational opportunities are available.
- **Provides feedback:** As students progress toward mastery or even excellence, they are aided by ongoing feedback from their instructors, and tests are often an important component of that feedback.

Because tests have such a powerful influence on student learning, it is important to develop tests that will properly align with educational goals. This manual focuses on writing high-quality multiple-choice questions (MCQs), aka items, that can assess a wide variety of clinical knowledge and skills.

Two issues are of particular concern when developing quality MCQ-based exams: **content sampling** and **psychometric performance**.

### Issues of Content Sampling

The purpose of any assessment is to permit inferences to be drawn concerning the skills of test-takers. Inferences are decisions, judgments, or conclusions that extend beyond the particular set of items included in the exam into the larger domain from which the items were sampled.

Key principles:

- The first decision involves the content to be sampled; content decisions will drive the number and topic areas of the MCQs.
- The amount of attention given to a content area should reflect its relative importance.
- If test questions are not adequately representative of the broader domain, results may be biased.
- If overall test length is too short, scores may not be sufficiently precise or reliable.
- To generate a reproducible score, the item writer needs to sample content broadly (typically 100 or more MCQs for text-based exams).

### Issues of Psychometric Performance

The process of evaluating psychometric characteristics is determined by the purpose of the test and the decisions that will be made based on results:

- **Higher-stakes tests** (promotion, graduation, course grades): Scores must be reasonably reproducible (high reliability) and evidence should demonstrate accuracy (validity).
- **Lower-stakes tests** (one element of decision-making): The required psychometric evidence is less, but attention should still be paid to reliability and validity.

### Purposes of Testing

- Communicate to students material that is important
- Motivate students to study
- Identify areas of deficiency, in need of remediation, or further learning
- Determine final grades or make promotion decisions
- Identify areas where instruction can be improved

### What Material Should Be Tested?

- Exam content should align with course or clinical experience objectives
- Important topics should be weighted more heavily than less important topics
- The testing time devoted to each topic should reflect the relative importance of the topic
- The sample of items should be representative of the instructional goals

## Chapter 2: Multiple-Choice Item Formats

One of the most crucial aspects of a multiple-choice test item (MCQ) is its type or structure. Different item types can be used for different topic areas, and each carries advantages and disadvantages. A critical aspect to keep in mind when choosing an item type is not only the ease of writing, but also the potential flaws that might benefit the savvy test-taker or introduce irrelevant difficulty.

### True-False vs. One-Best-Answer Items

The universe of multiple-choice items can be divided into two families:

**True-false item formats** (test-takers select all options that are true):
- C-type (A/B/Both/Neither response items)
- K-type (complex true-false items)
- X-type (simple true-false items)

**One-best-answer item formats** (test-takers select the single best response):
- A-type (4 or more options, single items or sets)
- B-type (4- or 5-option matching items, in sets of 2 to 5 items)
- F-type (items grouped into sets around specific content, where test-takers cannot return to previously seen items)
- G-type (items grouped into sets around specific content, where test-takers can return to previously seen items)
- R-type (extended-matching items, in sets of 5 to 20 items)

The letters used to label item formats hold no intrinsic meaning; they were assigned sequentially as new formats were developed.

### The True-False Family

True-false items require test-takers to select all options that are "true," which could be anywhere from one to all of the listed options. The test-taker must decide where to make the cutoff and determine to what extent a response must be "true" in order to be keyed as "true." This additional judgment may be unrelated to clinical expertise or knowledge.

**Example of a well-structured true-false item:**

> Which of the following are X-linked recessive conditions?
> 1. Cystic fibrosis
> 2. Duchenne muscular dystrophy
> 3. Hemophilia A (classic hemophilia)
> 4. Tay-Sachs disease

The stem is clear and the options are absolutely true or false with no ambiguity. Options are homogeneous (all are conditions), clearly worded, and of similar length, and the question is closed and focused.

**Example of a flawed true-false item:**

> True statements about cystic fibrosis (CF) include:
> 1. CF is an autosomal recessive disease
> 2. Children with CF usually die in their teens
> 3. Males with CF are sterile
> 4. The incidence of CF is 1:2000

Options 2, 3, and 4 cannot be judged as absolutely true or false, because a group of content experts would not necessarily agree on the answers. For example, for option 4, experts would demand more information (Is this in the United States? Is this among all ethnic groups?). Revision would include editing options 2, 3, and 4 to be clear statements of fact.

### General Rules for True-False Items

Because test-takers must select all "true" options, true-false items must satisfy these rules:

- Item and option text must be clear and unambiguous. Avoid imprecise phrases such as "is associated with" or "is useful for" or "is important"; words that provide cueing such as "may" or "could be"; and vague terms such as "usually" or "frequently."
- The lead-in should be closed and focused.
- Options must be absolutely true or false; no shades of gray are permissible.
- Options should be homogeneous so that they can be judged as entirely true or entirely false on a single dimension.

### Recommendations for Using True-False Items

**We recommend avoiding true-false questions if possible.** Although many item writers believe true-false items are easier to write, this type can be more problematic:

- The distinction between "true" and "false" is often unclear.
- Subsequent reviewers frequently alter the answer key.
- Reviewers tend to rewrite or discard true-false items far more frequently than other formats.
- To avoid ambiguity, item writers often lean toward assessing recall of isolated facts.
- Application of knowledge, integration, synthesis, and judgment can be better assessed by one-best-answer questions.

**The NBME has completely stopped using true-false formats on its examinations.**

### The One-Best-Answer Family

One-best-answer questions make explicit that only one option is to be selected. They are the most widely used multiple-choice item format. They consist of a **stem** (e.g., a clinical case presentation) and a **lead-in question**, followed by a series of choices, with one correct answer and anywhere from three to five distractors.

**Example:**

> A 32-year-old man has a 4-day history of progressive weakness in his extremities. He has been healthy except for an upper respiratory tract infection 10 days ago. His temperature is 37.8 C (100.0 F), pulse is 94/min, respirations are 42/min and shallow, and blood pressure is 130/80 mm Hg. He has symmetric weakness of both sides of the face and the proximal and distal muscles of the extremities. Sensation is intact. No deep tendon reflexes can be elicited. Babinski sign is present.
>
> **Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?**
>
> A. Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis
> B. Guillain-Barre syndrome *
> C. Myasthenia gravis
> D. Poliomyelitis
> E. Polymyositis

Even though the incorrect answers are not completely wrong, they are less correct than the keyed answer. Experts would all agree that the most likely diagnosis is B. As long as options can be laid out on a single continuum (from "Least Likely Diagnosis" to "Most Likely Diagnosis"), distractors do not have to be totally wrong.

### The "Cover-the-Options" Rule

If a lead-in is properly focused, a test-taker should be able to read the stem and lead-in, cover the options, and guess the correct answer without seeing the option set. When writing items, covering the options and attempting to answer is a good check for whether this rule has been followed.

**Example of a flawed item (options not on a single dimension):**

> Which of the following is true about pseudogout?
> A. It is clearly hereditary in most cases
> B. It is seldom associated with acute pain in a joint
> C. It may be associated with a finding of chondrocalcinosis
> D. It occurs frequently in women
> E. It responds well to treatment with allopurinol

The options are heterogeneous and deal with miscellaneous facts; they cannot be rank-ordered from least to most true along a single dimension. The question by itself is not clear and cannot be answered without looking at the options.

### General Rules for One-Best-Answer Items

- Item and option text must be clear and unambiguous. Avoid imprecise phrases such as "is associated with" or "is useful for" or "is important"; words that provide cueing such as "may" or "could be"; and vague terms such as "usually" or "frequently."
- The lead-in should be closed and focused, and ideally worded so that the test-taker can cover the options and guess the correct answer (the "cover-the-options" rule).
- All options should be homogeneous so that they can be judged as entirely true or entirely false on a single dimension.
- Incorrect options can be partially or wholly incorrect.

### Recommendations for Using One-Best-Answer Items

**We recommend using one-best-answer questions whenever possible.** This format:

- Prevents confusion from having to guess the author's intent
- Can be easier and more efficient to write (wrong options do not have to be entirely incorrect)
- Allows different lead-ins to be paired with the same stems to create item sets
- Is the only format currently used by the NBME on exams

## Chapter 3: Technical Item Flaws

The purpose of this chapter is to describe two kinds of technical item flaws:

1. **Flaws related to irrelevant difficulty** -- artificially increase difficulty, confuse all test-takers, add construct-irrelevant variance to the final test score.
2. **Flaws related to testwiseness** -- cue savvy/confident test-takers, aid them in guessing the right answer based on test-taking skills alone without necessarily knowing the content.

The item writer's goal is to eliminate both types of flaws to create a level playing field. A test-taker's probability of answering correctly should be determined by expertise on the topic being assessed.

### Flaws Related to Irrelevant Difficulty

#### Options Are Overly Long or Complicated

The number of words in each option increases the reading load, which can shift the construct being measured from content knowledge to reading speed. Note: this flaw relates only to **options**. Long stems are acceptable when the purpose is to assess interpretation and synthesis of information.

#### Numeric Data Are Not Presented Consistently

When numeric options are used:
- Options should be listed in numeric order
- Options should be in a single format (all as ranges or all as specific values)
- No subset of options should include all possible outcomes (which allows elimination of other options)

#### Vague Frequency Terms in Options

Vague frequency terms (such as "often" or "usually") are not consistently defined or interpreted by readers, and sometimes not even by experts. This can lead to multiple correct answers or options that cannot be rank ordered in terms of correctness.

#### "None of the Above" Is Used as an Option

"None of the above" is problematic in items where judgment is involved and options are not absolutely true or false:
- Knowledgeable students face a dilemma between the intended correct option and one that encompasses everything not listed
- It essentially turns the item into a true-false item
- If the correct answer is to take no action, replace "None of the above" with a specific option (e.g., "No management is indicated at this time")

#### Options Are Not Homogeneous or Parallel

When the structure of each option differs, it adds to reading time. This flaw can be corrected by careful editing to ensure all options have the same format and structure. Change the lead-in to generate logical, parallel answers.

#### Stems Are Unnecessarily Complicated

Avoid requiring test-takers to perform tasks irrelevant to the testing point (e.g., rank-ordering Roman numerals). The testing point itself should be the challenge, not the format.

#### Stems Are Negatively Phrased

Negative phrasing asks test-takers to find the most false or least accurate option. If most items on a test are positively phrased, a negatively phrased stem carries the risk that students will miss the word "except," even when bolded or capitalized.

### Flaws Related to Testwiseness

#### Presence of Grammatical Cues

This flaw exists when an option does not follow grammatically from the stem or lead-in. Testwise students can eliminate options that don't fit grammatically. To avoid this: read each option following the stem to ensure grammatical fit, and always use closed lead-ins.

#### Presence of Grouped or Collectively Exhaustive Options

When a savvy student can identify a subset of options that cover all possible outcomes (are collectively exhaustive) and rule out the options not in that subset. The item writer should be able to rank order each option on the same dimension, and no subset of options should include all possible outcomes.

#### Use of Absolute Terms

Options containing terms like "always" or "never" are less likely to be true than options stated less absolutely. Testwise students will eliminate these. This flaw tends to arise when verbs are included in the options rather than in the lead-in.

#### Presence of Word Repetition ("Clang Clues")

Language used in the lead-in is repeated in the options. For example, the word "unreal" in the stem can clue test-takers that "derealization" is the correct answer. This flaw can appear even metaphorically (e.g., bone pain in the stem and "osteo-" prefix in the answer).

#### The Correct Option Is Longer, More Specific, or More Complete

Item writers often pay more attention to the correct answer than distractors, creating correct answers that are longer or include additional instructional material, parenthetical information, and caveats. This flaw can be avoided by reviewing the entire item set for length.

#### Presence of Convergence

The correct answer is the option that has the most in common with the other options. Testwise students can converge on the right answer by counting how frequently certain terms appear. This flaw occurs when item writers start with the correct answer and write distractors as permutations of it.

### Summary of Technical Item Flaws

**Flaws Related to Irrelevant Difficulty:**
- Options are overly long or complicated
- Numeric data are not stated consistently
- Terms in the options or the stem are vague
- Language or structure of the options is not homogeneous
- Options are not in a logical order
- "None of the above" is used as an option
- Stems are unnecessarily complicated
- Stems contain negative phrasing

**Flaws Related to Testwiseness:**
- Grammatical cues exist because one or more distractors don't follow grammatically from the stem
- Options are cued by being paired or exhaustive, where some options can be eliminated because other options cover all possible outcomes
- Absolute terms such as "always" or "never" are in some options
- The correct answer is longer, more specific, or more complete than the other options
- A word or phrase is included both in the stem and in the correct answer
- Convergence (the correct answer includes the most elements in common with the other options) is present

## Chapter 4: Item Analysis and Interpretation of Results

Item analysis provides useful information about item performance for a given group of test-takers. It should be done before final test scores are calculated and before grades are provided. Common types of item analysis:

- Analysis of item difficulty
- Analysis of item discrimination
- Analysis of item options
- Comparative analysis of test-taker groups

### Analysis of Item Difficulty

The most common classical test theory index of difficulty is the **P-value** (percent-correct value): the percent of overall test-takers who got an item correct.

- Lower P-values = more difficult items
- Higher P-values = easier items
- Items that are very easy (P-value > .95) or very hard (P-value < .30) do not provide much information about the population
- Unusually high or low P-values on certain topics can result from test-takers who have completely mastered or not learned the material
- A high-quality assessment should contain items representing a range of difficulties

### Analysis of Item Discrimination

A good item discriminates between test-takers who know the material and those who do not. The index of discrimination is computed as the correlation of performance on the item with performance on the test as a whole (item-total correlation).

- **Biserial** and **point-biserial** correlation coefficients range from -1.0 to +1.0
- **Large positive values:** test-takers who get the item correct tend to do well overall (desirable)
- **Near zero:** little relationship between item and test performance; item does not provide additional information
- **Negative values:** test-takers who did worse overall have a higher chance of getting the item right (indicates a problem -- possibly measuring something different, an obvious flaw, or incorrect keying)

### Analysis of Item Options

The item writer should always review performance of incorrect options (option analysis):

- Were any options not selected? (sign of implausible distractors)
- Was any wrong option chosen more often than expected, or more than the key? (may indicate more than one correct answer, or miskeying)
- If an option expected to be plausible or expected to be easily excluded performs contrary to expectations, review the item for structural soundness and content

### Comparative Analysis of Test-Taker Groups

Two categories:

**1. Within-item analysis:** Classify students by overall test performance into groups.
- High/Low grouping: top 50% vs. bottom 50%, or top 25% vs. bottom 25%
- For very large numbers: quartiles or quintiles
- Option analysis is most informative when conducted on subgroups

**2. Cross-group analysis:** Group students by a variable expected to impact performance.
- Compare P-values and option analysis across groups
- Track item statistics over time to detect exposure, outdated content, or changes in instruction

### Example Items and Analysis

**Item #1** -- Miskeyed item:

```
GROUP    A    B*    C     D    E    F
HIGH     1    1     91    4    1    2
LOW      20   6     51    14   6    3
TOTAL    9    2     76    8    3    2
P-value: 2    discrimination index: -.21
```

Only 2% answered the keyed answer (B) correctly. The correct answer is almost certainly C. If rekeyed to C, P-value becomes 76% with positive discrimination -- excellent values.

**Item #2** -- Good item:

```
GROUP    A    B    C*    D     E    F
HIGH     0    1    90    3     3    3
LOW      0    1    60    25    8    6
TOTAL    0    1    74    12    7    6
P-value: 74   discrimination index: +.33
```

Appropriate difficulty, good discrimination. Options A and B may need rewriting as they are not very plausible distractors.

**Item #3** -- Difficult item, possible second correct answer:

```
GROUP    A     B    C*    D     E     F
HIGH     44    1    50    2     1     2
LOW      20    15   21    22    20    2
TOTAL    32    7    34    14    11    2
P-value: 34   discrimination index: +.30
```

50% of the High group selected C, but 44% selected A. Content expert should review whether A is a second correct answer.

**Item #4** -- Difficult but sound item:

```
GROUP    A     B     C*    D     E    F
HIGH     18    10    51    17    2    2
LOW      24    24    21    25    4    2
TOTAL    22    17    34    22    3    2
P-value: 34   discrimination index: +.30
```

Same difficulty as Item #3, but test-takers who don't know the answer are more evenly spread across distractors. More distractors in the Low group than the High group -- a healthy pattern.

**Item #5** -- Two correct answers:

```
GROUP    A     B     C    D*    E
HIGH     10    43    5    40    2
LOW      23    36    12   26    3
TOTAL    17    43    7    31    2
P-value: 31   discrimination index: -.09
```

Both High and Low groups more likely to select B than the keyed D. Should be reviewed by a content expert and not scored until reviewed.

## Chapter 5: Basic Rules for Writing One-Best-Answer Items

### Rule 1: Each item should focus on an important concept or testing point.

The topic usually results from the **blueprint** -- the outline of the major topics to be covered on the examination. A clear and comprehensive blueprint or other set of test specifications should always be available so item writers can stay focused on the important topics and write sufficient numbers of items for each topic.

### Rule 2: Each item should assess application of knowledge, not recall of an isolated fact.

The first step is to develop an appropriate stimulus (clinical or experimental vignette) to provide context. Without such a stimulus, the resulting item will generally be assessing knowledge recall, making it difficult to assess higher levels within Bloom's taxonomy such as "application of knowledge."

Tips:
- Use actual cases as a source of ideas, but avoid adhering too closely (actual cases often have atypical features)
- Consider the underlying cause of conditions -- patient demographics, history, and other factors will differ depending on etiology
- Adjust vignette details to the level of the test-taker (more typical features for novices; some atypical features for advanced test-takers)

### Rule 3: The item lead-in should be focused, closed, and clear; the test-taker should be able to answer based on the stem and lead-in alone.

- Use focused lead-ins such as "Which of the following is the next step in the management of this patient?" or "Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?"
- Avoid open-ended lead-ins such as "The diagnosis in the patient is:"
- The lead-in should be a single, closed, clear question
- Using closed lead-ins helps avoid flaws such as grammatical cueing

### Rule 4: All options should be homogeneous and plausible, to avoid cueing to the correct option.

- Generate the correct or keyed answer for the lead-in
- Generate plausible and parallel yet incorrect distractors (often more challenging than writing the correct answer)

### Rule 5: Always review items to identify and remove technical flaws.

Review checklist:
- The bulk of the text (vignette/case) should precede the lead-in
- The clinical or experimental vignette should follow a logical sequence: demographics, history, physical exam, lab data, initial treatment, subsequent findings
- The lead-in should be closed
- The wording of the lead-in should logically generate a homogeneous option set
- Use a template to ensure all sections are in place and correctly structured

Ask yourself:
- If options were removed, could a knowledgeable test-taker answer correctly?
- Is there anything that would confuse the knowledgeable test-taker?
- Are there any clues to help a testwise student guess correctly?
- Have a colleague review for content, clarity, and appropriateness

## Chapter 6: Testing Application of Foundational (Basic) and Clinical Knowledge

### Choosing the Topics to Test

The content of an exam should be driven by:
- The purpose of the exam
- The test-taker population
- How the scores will be used

Considerations:
- An exam intended for formative feedback will have different focus and content than an end-of-clerkship exam
- Topics should be important for the level of practice being assessed

### Writing Clinical Vignettes

Test questions can be classified as requiring:
- **Recall:** Knowledge of definitions or facts
- **Interpretation:** Reviewing information and reaching a conclusion (e.g., diagnosis)
- **Problem-Solving:** Taking action in a presented situation (e.g., deciding next step in management)

An alternate, more practical approach divides items into two categories:
- **Application of knowledge:** Requires reaching a conclusion, making a prediction, or selecting a course of action
- **Recall of a fact:** Assesses only rote memory without requiring application

### Figure 1: Recall vs. Vignette Items in Assessment

| Item Type | Formative Assessment | Summative Assessment |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Recall** | Useful for assessing efficiency of classroom instruction; provides "rapid fire" stimulation of learning; attention-keeping strategy | Allows for large quantity of items; best for single-step questions and single concepts/facts |
| **Vignette/Experimental** | Item format familiarization; problem-based learning; team-based learning; clinical or experimental correlations during instruction | Good for assessing higher-order thinking skills; provides better approximation of real-life practice; allows for integration and differentiation; amenable to multi-step question formats |

### Benefits of Clinical Vignettes

- Authenticity is enhanced by questions that require test-takers to "solve" clinical problems
- Questions are more likely to focus on important information rather than trivia
- They help identify test-takers who have memorized facts but cannot use them effectively

Recall items often begin by citing a disease and asking what findings are expected -- clinically backwards (patients rarely tell physicians what disease they have).

### Guidelines for Clinical Vignette Content

- Test application of knowledge using clinical vignettes to pose medical decisions in patient care situations
- Focus items on common or potentially catastrophic problems; avoid "zebras" and esoterica
- Pose clinical decision-making tasks that would be expected of a successful test-taker
- Avoid clinical situations that would be handled by a specialist if not writing for a specialty exam
- Focus on specific tasks that the successful test-taker must be able to undertake at the next stage of training
- Focus on areas in which clinical reasoning mistakes are often made

### Clinical Vignette Template

The following components should appear in this order (not all are necessary):

1. Age, gender (e.g., 45-year-old man)
2. Site of care (e.g., the emergency department)
3. Presenting complaint (e.g., headache)
4. Duration of complaint (e.g., 2 days)
5. Patient history (past medical, family, psychosocial, review of systems if important and plausible)
6. Physical findings
7. Results of diagnostic studies
8. Initial treatment, subsequent findings

### Item Stem Rules

- Focuses on important concepts rather than trivial facts
- Can be answered without looking at the options
- Includes all relevant facts; no additional data should be provided in the options
- Is not "tricky" or overly complex
- Is not negatively phrased (avoid using "except" or "not" in the lead-in)

### Writing Item Lead-ins

The vast majority of items should be written with a clinical or experimental vignette. Information should be provided in a consistent order across items. The lead-in must pose a clear question so the test-taker can answer without looking at the options (the "cover-the-options" rule).

### Item Example: With and Without Vignette

Performance comparison across three formats of the same item (percentage selecting each option):

**No Vignette:**

```
         A    B    C*   D    E
HIGH     1    0    99   0    0
LOW      8    1    90   1    0
```

**Short Vignette:**

```
         A    B    C*   D    E
HIGH     0    0    98   2    0
LOW      6    2    82   9    1
```

**Long Vignette:**

```
         A    B    C*   D    E
HIGH     0    1    98   1    0
LOW      10   9    66   10   5
```

The vignette format did not substantially affect the High group but made the item more difficult for the Low group. Clinical vignettes require test-takers to demonstrate proficiency in sorting through patient information, synthesizing important findings, and reaching a conclusion.

### Additional Points on Vignettes

**Verbosity, Window Dressing, and Red Herrings:**
- Avoid excessive verbosity, extraneous material ("window dressing"), and misleading information ("red herrings")
- Good-quality clinical vignettes can avoid these traps while testing application of knowledge
- Vignettes should follow a standard structure and pose clinically natural questions
- Templates help develop high-quality vignettes with lower risk of unnecessary verbiage

**Use of Real Patients:**
- Be careful when basing vignettes on real patients, particularly for student-level tests
- Real patients are complicated, and the complicated elements are not always those important for assessment
- Incidental findings are fine, but real patients often have "red herrings" among their findings

**Patients Who Lie:**
- Patients in vignettes should tell the truth, or the physician's interpretation should be provided
- Do not write items requiring interpretation of veracity (e.g., avoid "The patient 'claims' to drink only one bottle of beer each night")

### Structuring Items to Fit Task Competencies

Each competency requires a slightly different approach to item writing.

#### Foundational (Basic) Science

Items that require understanding and application of basic science, along with clinical knowledge. Sample lead-ins:

- Which of the following is the most likely cause/mechanism of this effect?
- Which of the following is the most likely causal infectious agent?
- This patient most likely has a defect in which of the following?
- This patient most likely has a deficiency in which of the following enzymes?

#### Management

**Health Maintenance and Disease Prevention:**

Items assess the ability to evaluate risk factors, understand epidemiologic data, and apply preventive measures. Categories include: screening tests, constructive interference, immunizations/travel medicine, and emergency intervention.

- Questions should NOT focus on the direct assessment of isolated facts
- In asking about immunizations or screening tests, consider providing a chart of customary practices to avoid memorization of conflicting recommendations

Sample lead-ins:
- Which of the following immunizations should be administered at this time?

- Which of the following tests would have predicted these findings?
- For which of the following conditions is this patient at greatest risk?
- Which of the following is most likely to have prevented this condition?

**Pharmacotherapy/Clinical Interventions and Treatments:**

Items assess principles of chronic and acute care. Focus on aspects of care relevant to the level of practice of the test-taker. Sample lead-ins:
- Which of the following is the most appropriate initial or next step in patient care?
- Which of the following is the most effective management?
- Which of the following is the most appropriate pharmacotherapy?
- Which of the following is the first priority in caring for this patient?

#### Mechanisms of Disease

Items evaluate knowledge of pathophysiology in its broadest sense: etiology, pathogenesis, natural history, clinical course, associated findings, complications, severity of illness, and effects of therapeutic interventions. Items should be framed in a clinical context. Sample lead-ins:

- Which of the following is the most likely explanation for these findings?
- Which of the following is the most likely location of this patient's lesion?
- Which of the following is the most likely pathogen?
- Which of the following findings is most likely to be increased/decreased?
- A biopsy specimen is most likely to show which of the following?

### Writing Items on Difficult Topics

When working with difficult topics:

1. Review sources of test material and select any acceptable questions on the topic
2. Identify the key features of these items and develop a template for similar items
3. For topics with no sample items, outline tasks related to the topic that are essential to know
4. Focus on application rather than definitions

**Example -- Ethics and Jurisprudence:**

The NBME decided it was not important to assess whether students know definitions; the goal became assessing whether test-takers could **apply ethical principles** in patient care decisions. All questions involved a patient vignette and required the student to indicate what the physician should do or evaluate the appropriateness of the physician's actions. No questions focused just on definition of terms.

## Chapter 7: Using Media as Part of Clinical Vignettes

Computer-based exam administration makes it straightforward to add media. Advantages include:

- Opportunity for adding authenticity to assessment of knowledge and skills
- Ability to assess skills that text-based items cannot measure well
- Avoiding textual cues that benefit savvy test-takers

### Considerations for Using Media

- **Content area and skills being assessed:** The media should be a natural fit for the skill (e.g., ECG for cardiac auscultation findings)
- **Novelty:** Very novel media may require a learning curve or additional tutorial information; simplicity in accessing media is desirable
- **Memorability:** Media may be more easily remembered by students; use multiple items per piece of media to reduce the memorability problem
- **Richness of patient description:** A long vignette combined with media provides rich, authentic descriptions but requires more time for exploration

### Types of Media

- **Images (static):** Radiographic studies (x-rays, CT scans, MRIs); students interpret studies and decide on diagnosis or management
- **Patient photographs:** Add authenticity and convey information accurately and succinctly
- **Videos:** Neurologic examinations and physical exam findings are better shown than described; also useful for patient-doctor interactions
- **Interactive media (e.g., avatars):** Require students to select different areas to see and/or hear examination findings similar to actual examinations

### Selecting Media

Media should be purposefully selected to help the student answer the question, not included without a good reason. Do not describe with text that which can be easily demonstrated in the media.

### Content Areas Conducive to Media

- Dermatologic and musculoskeletal examination findings
- Cardiology (such as heart sounds)
- Neurologic examination findings
- Ethical and communication scenarios

**Dermatologic and musculoskeletal findings** particularly benefit from media: showing findings rather than describing them simulates real clinical practice, and research shows faster response times with media compared to text for dermatologic findings.

**Ethical and communication scenarios** are often easy to answer in text form because intonation and body language cannot be demonstrated well through text. Video-based items add important complexity.

### Acquiring and Creating Media

A subject matter expert group can oversee media acquisition:
- Develop a list of diseases, conditions, and tasks best illustrated with media
- Develop exemplars to distribute for item-writing assignments
- A good image is one to which multiple test items can be written

**Patient confidentiality** and **metadata** are key considerations:
- Ensure neither the patient nor institution can be identified
- Follow institutional confidentiality policy and HIPAA guidelines

### Metadata for Media

Record as much metadata as possible during acquisition:
- Administrative details
- Age of the patient
- Diagnosis
- Keywords
- Description of the test being performed
- Normal or abnormal results
- Descriptive file name
- Patient ID/Name
- Indication of signed consent form
- In/out cut points for individual clips
- Whether the clip contains important audio

> **Remember:** Your media are only as good as their metadata! Media have little value in item writing if they cannot be retrieved easily in search results.

### Sources of Media

1. **Content experts' personal libraries/patients:** Ready option but limited by patient confidentiality, memorability, and available patient population
2. **Purchase from vendors:** More expensive but allows requesting specific content and types with instructions to decrease memorability
3. **Create new media:** In-house or with a vendor (e.g., recording videos with actors for communication skills items)

### Tips for Creating Videos

**DO:**
- Use a plain background
- Avoid visual cues (e.g., office equipment, paintings)
- Record in a well-lit room
- Have the patient wear plain clothes or a hospital gown with no logos
- Keep background and clothing consistent across multiple patients or exams
- Have the provider talk to the patient as they would in a normal exam
- Have the provider avoid using names when addressing the patient
- Leave the videos as recorded and provide instructions for editing
- Limit the video length to 30 seconds
- Provide a signed patient consent form

**DON'T:**
- Pick a brightly colored room, room with identifiable pictures, or room with unique non-clinical furniture
- Allow the patient or provider to wear brightly colored clothes, clothes with logos, or jewelry
- Show the faces of the provider(s) or patient(s) if it is not essential
- Explain everything in detail or add narration
- Add transitions (e.g., fade-in, fade-out) to video files
- Resize or change the dimensions of the video

## Appendix A: NBME Retired Item Formats

To move forward with innovations in assessment, it is necessary to look back on previously used item types and understand their flaws. Multiple item types were used on NBME exams beginning in the mid-1980s to provide sufficient variety for lengthy examinations, with four predominant types: A-type, B-type, C-type, and K-type.

**Currently used item types:**
- **A-type** -- Standard one-best-answer item type
- **R-type** -- Single option sets associated with multiple items/vignettes
- **F-type** -- Items grouped into sets, test-takers cannot return to previously seen items
- **G-type** -- Items grouped into sets, test-takers can return to previously seen items

Limiting the number of acceptable item types provides advantages:
1. Allows item authors to spend less time on format and more time on quality content
2. Reinforces standardized nature of items, supporting validity evidence
3. Allows more efficient production, editing, and approval

### B-Type Items (Retired)

**Format:** Matching sets that tested related subjects in a series of items. Each response could be used more than once or not at all.

**Noted Flaws:** Did not typically include a lead-in, making the question unclear.

**Sample directions:** "For each numbered item, select the ONE lettered option that is most closely associated with it."

### C-Type Items (Retired)

**Format:** Similar to B-type in appearance but actually multiple true-false. Options are A, B, Both, or Neither.

**Noted Flaws:** Test-takers had to conclude to what extent something needed to be "true" to be selected. Judgments were less about medical knowledge and more about guessing the item writer's intent.

### D-Type Items (Retired)

**Format:** Complex matching sets where each item consists of three functional disturbances and five situations. Four of five numbered items belong to one category; test-takers identify the category and the item that does not belong.

**Noted Flaws:** Difficult to write; confusing directions; poor discrimination among test-takers.

### H-Type Items (Retired)

**Format:** Paired statements comparing two entities quantitatively. Select A if A > B, B if B > A, or C if approximately equal.

**Noted Flaws:** Challenging for test-takers to decide how great the difference needed to be to be relevant.

### I-Type Items (Retired)

**Format:** Similar to H-type. Pairs of phrases describing conditions or quantities that might vary in relation to each other.

**Noted Flaws:**
- Fewer options increased chance of guessing correctly (affected difficulty and discrimination)
- Items tended to focus on minor details rather than relevant concepts

### K-Type Items (Retired)

**Format:** Multiple true-false sets with a fixed answer code:

```
A           B          C          D         E
1,2,3 only  1,3 only   2,4 only   4 only    All are correct
```

**Noted Flaws:**
- Could only include absolutely true or false facts
- Could not assess clinical judgment except in comparisons
- Appeared too complicated; required test-takers to constantly remember the answer code
- Response combinations introduced a cueing effect that decreased discrimination
