# SECTION 1 — Issues Related to Format and Structure of Test Items

## Chapter 1: Introduction

### Assessment: An Important Component of Instruction

Assessment, also known as testing, is a critical component of health professions education. A primary purpose of testing is to communicate what you, as the instructor or preceptor and item writer, view as important. Tests are a powerful motivator; students will learn the educational concepts they believe you value. Assessment also helps to fill instructional gaps by motivating students to seek educational resources and opportunities beyond course work.

Two issues of concern when developing and constructing quality MCQ-based exams:

**Content sampling:**
- Content decisions drive the number and topic areas of the MCQs to be developed.
- Attention given to evaluating a content area should reflect its relative importance.
- Tests are point-in-time measurements with limited time; covering one area deeply means less time for others.
- If test questions are not adequately representative of the broader domain of interest, results may be biased.
- If overall test length is too short, scores may not be sufficiently precise or reliable.
- To generate a reproducible score, item writers should sample content broadly — typically 100+ MCQs for written assessments.

**Psychometric performance:**
- Higher-stakes tests (promotion, graduation, course grades, decisions in isolation) require reasonably reproducible scores (high reliability) and evidence of accuracy (validity).
- Lower-stakes tests require less evidence but attention to reliability and validity is still warranted.

### Purposes of Testing

- Inform students about material that is important
- Motivate students to study
- Identify areas of deficiency, in need of remediation, or further learning
- Determine final grades or make promotion decisions
- Identify areas in which instruction can be improved

### What Material Should Be Tested?

- Exam content should align with course or clinical experience objectives
- Predetermined important topics should be weighted more heavily than less important topics
- Testing time devoted to each topic should reflect the relative (predetermined) importance of the topic
- Breadth of items should be representative of the instructional goals and objectives (curricular alignment)

## Chapter 2: Multiple-Choice Item Formats

One of the most crucial aspects of an MCQ is its type or structure. Different item types can be used for different topic areas; each carries advantages and disadvantages. A critical consideration is the inclusion of potential flaws that might benefit the savvy test-taker or introduce irrelevant difficulty.

### One-Best-Answer vs. True-False Items

The universe of multiple-choice items divides into two families:

**One-best-answer items** (test-takers select the single best response):
- **A-type** — 4 or more options, single items or sets
- **F-type** — 2–3 items grouped into a set around specific content or case scenario where test-takers **cannot return** to previously seen items in the set
- **G-type** — 2 or 3 items grouped into a set around specific content where test-takers **can return** to previously seen items in the set

**True-false items** (test-takers select all options that are true):
- **C-type** — A/B/Both/Neither response items
- **K-type** — complex true-false items
- **X-type** — simple true-false items

The letters hold no intrinsic meaning; they were assigned sequentially as new formats were developed.

### The One-Best-Answer Family

One-best-answer items are the most widely used MCQ format. They consist of a stem (typically a vignette + lead-in) followed by options — one correct answer and 3 to 7 distractors. Incorrect options should be directly related to the lead-in and be homogeneous with the correct answer.

**Sample A-type item:**

> *Vignette:*
> A 32-year-old man comes to the office because of a 4-day history of progressive weakness in his extremities. He has been healthy except for an upper respiratory tract infection 10 days ago. His temperature is 37.8°C (100.0°F), pulse is 94/min, respirations are 42/min and shallow, and blood pressure is 130/80 mm Hg. He has symmetric weakness of both sides of the face and the proximal and distal muscles of the extremities. Sensation is intact. No deep tendon reflexes can be elicited. Babinski sign is absent.
>
> *Lead-in:* Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
>
> *Options:*
> A. Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis
> B. Guillain-Barré syndrome *
> C. Myasthenia gravis
> D. Poliomyelitis
> E. Polymyositis

Incorrect options are not wholly wrong — they can be laid out on a single continuum (e.g., least-likely to most-likely diagnosis) with the keyed option at one end.

### "Cover-the-Options" Rule

If a lead-in is properly focused, a test-taker should be able to read the vignette and lead-in, **cover the options**, and guess the correct answer without seeing the option set. This is a fundamental quality check for one-best-answer items.

### Homogeneous Options

A good item has a keyed answer and distractors that are homogeneous — all directly addressing the lead-in in the same manner, rank-orderable along a single dimension. When options are not on a single dimension (e.g., inheritance vs. treatment response vs. gender — all mixed), the item cannot be answered from the lead-in alone.

### General Rules for One-Best-Answer Items

- Item and option text must be clear and unambiguous. Avoid imprecise phrases ("is associated with," "is useful for," "is important"); avoid cueing words ("may," "could be"); avoid vague terms ("usually," "frequently").
- The lead-in should be closed and focused so the test-taker can cover the options and guess the correct answer.
- All options should be homogeneous — judgeable as entirely true or entirely false on a single dimension.
- Incorrect options can be partially or wholly incorrect.

**NBME currently uses only one-best-answer format items on exams.**

### The True-False Family (Use Sparingly)

True-false items require selecting all options that are "true," and demand the test-taker decide where the cutoff of "true enough" is — often unrelated to clinical expertise. Many are problematic because options are not absolutely true or false, leading to reviewer disagreement. **Recommendation: avoid true-false questions if possible.**

## Chapter 3: Technical Item Flaws

Two kinds of technical item flaws:
1. **Irrelevant difficulty** — makes the item challenging for reasons unrelated to the testing objective, adding construct-irrelevant variance.
2. **Testwise cues** — aids savvy test-takers in guessing correctly based on test-taking skills alone, without content knowledge.

### Flaws Related to Irrelevant Difficulty

**Long or Complex Options:**
Options with too many words increase reading load, shifting measurement from content knowledge to reading speed. Solution: put common text in the stem, use parallel construction, shorten options.

**Numeric Data Presented Inconsistently:**
Numeric options must be in numeric order and in a single format (all ranges OR all specific values — never mixed). Overlapping ranges (e.g., "greater than 50%" alongside "75%") create impossible choices.

**Vague Terms:**
Frequency terms ("often," "usually") are not consistently defined, leading to multiple correct answers. Avoid frequency terms and use focused lead-ins with short homogeneous options.

**"None of the Above":**
Problematic when judgment is involved. Replace with a specific action (e.g., "No pharmacotherapy is indicated at this time").

**Nonparallel Options:**
Options with different grammatical structures increase reading time. Edit options to parallel grammatical form and structure.

**Complicated Stems:**
Stems requiring interpretation of Roman numerals, complex orderings, or multi-part conditions add irrelevant difficulty. Focus on a single karyotype / single condition; move complexity to options.

**Negatively Phrased Lead-ins:**
Items using "EXCEPT" or "NOT" risk test-takers missing the negative word. Revise to positive structure.

### Flaws That Cue the Testwise Examinee

**Grammatical Cues:**
When an option does not follow grammatically from the lead-in, testwise students eliminate it. Use closed lead-ins; make options all singular or all plural.

**Grouped or Collectively Exhaustive Options:**
When a subset of options covers all possible outcomes (e.g., "increase / decrease / no change"), the correct answer must be in that subset. Replace at least one option in the subset; avoid creating option pairs.

**Absolute Terms ("always," "never"):**
Options with absolute terms ("is never seen") are less likely to be correct than hedged options. Eliminate absolute terms; use a focused lead-in with short options.

**Correct Option Stands Out:**
The correct answer should not be longer, more qualified, or more specific than distractors. Remove teaching-statement language; equalize length and specificity.

**Word Repetition ("Clang Clues"):**
Language repeated between the stem and the correct answer signals the key. Replace the repeated word in either stem or option, OR use the repeated word in all options.

**Convergence:**
The correct answer has the most elements in common with the other options. Testwise examinees count overlapping terms and converge on the answer. Review all options; balance use of terms so no single option shares the most elements.

### Summary Tables

**Irrelevant-difficulty flaws / solutions:**

| Flaw | Solution |
|---|---|
| Long, complex options | Put common text in stem; use parallel construction; shorten options |
| Tricky, unnecessarily complicated stems | Include only necessary content; avoid teaching statements |
| Inconsistent numeric data | Avoid overlapping options; ask for minimum or maximum value |
| Vague terms | Avoid frequency terms like "usually" and "often" |
| "None of the above" | Replace with specific action (e.g., "No intervention needed") |
| Nonparallel options | Edit for parallel grammatical form and structure |
| Negatively structured stem | Revise lead-in to positive structure |

**Testwise-cue flaws / solutions:**

| Flaw | Solution |
|---|---|
| Collectively exhaustive options | Replace at least one option in subset; avoid option pairs |
| Absolute terms in options | Eliminate absolute terms; focused lead-in + short homogeneous options |
| Grammatical cues | All options singular or all plural; use closed lead-ins |
| Correct answer stands out | Equalize option length; remove teaching-point language |
| Word repeats (clang clue) | Replace repeated word, or use it in all options |
| Convergence | Revise options to balance use of terms |

## Chapter 4: Item Analysis and Interpretation of Results

### Analysis of Item Difficulty

**P-value (percent-correct value):** the percent of overall test-takers who got an item correct.
- Lower p-values → harder items; higher → easier.
- Items with p-value >0.95 or <0.30 provide little discrimination information.
- A quality assessment contains a range of difficulties.

### Analysis of Item Discrimination

**Discrimination index:** correlation of performance on the item with performance on the test as a whole. Biserial / point-biserial correlations range −1.0 to +1.0.
- Large positive → item discriminates well; test-takers who got it correct did well overall.
- Zero → no relation between item and test performance.
- Negative → test-takers who did worse overall were more likely to get the item correct — indicates miskeying, a structural flaw, or measurement of a different construct.

### Analysis of Item Options

Review performance of incorrect options:
- Were any options not selected? → Implausible distractor or structural flaw.
- Was any incorrect option chosen more often than the key? → Possible miskeying or second correct answer.
- Evaluate each distractor against expectations (should be an easy exclusion? A plausible challenge?).

### Comparative Analyses

**Within-item (High/Low):** Group test-takers by overall test performance; analyze item difficulty and option patterns separately. Common splits: top 50% vs. bottom 50%, or top 27% vs. bottom 27% (often rounded to 25%/25%), or quartiles/quintiles.

**Cross-group:** Group by a variable expected to impact performance (e.g., year of training); compare p-values and option patterns across groups. Track item statistics over time to detect exposure, outdated content, or instructional changes.

### Example Analyses (5 patterns)

1. **Miskeyed** — High group picks a non-keyed option at high rates; negative discrimination. Rekey and rescore.
2. **Good** — Both groups pick the key; 90%/60% split; +0.33 discrimination. Implausible distractors may be worth rewriting.
3. **Difficult, possible second correct** — 50%/21% on the key; 44% in High group picks another option. Expert review needed.
4. **Difficult but sound** — 51%/21% on the key; wrong options spread evenly across Low group (healthy pattern).
5. **Two correct answers** — Both groups more likely to pick a non-keyed option than the key; negative discrimination. Do not score until reviewed.

# SECTION 2 — Writing One-Best-Answer Items for the Foundational (Basic) and Clinical Sciences

## Chapter 5: Basic Rules for Writing One-Best-Answer Items

**Rule 1 — Each item should focus on an important concept or testing point.**
The topic usually results from the examination blueprint. Blueprints often have two dimensions: disease-based (hypertension, ischemic heart disease, etc.) and task-based (assessment of foundational science, diagnosis, prognosis, etc.). A clear blueprint keeps item writers focused on important topics and ensures sufficient items per topic.

**Rule 2 — Each item should assess application of knowledge, not recall of an isolated fact.**
Develop a clinical or experimental vignette to provide context. Without a stimulus, the item assesses recall only. Use actual patient scenarios as idea sources, but avoid adhering too closely because real cases often have atypical features. Tailor vignette complexity to the level of the test-taker — more classic/typical presentations for newer learners, more atypical features for advanced learners.

**Rule 3 — The item lead-in should be focused, closed, and clear; the test-taker should be able to answer based on the vignette and lead-in alone.**
Use closed lead-ins such as "Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in management?" or "Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?" Avoid open-ended lead-ins ("The diagnosis is:"). Closed lead-ins also prevent grammatical-cueing flaws.

**Rule 4 — All options should be homogeneous and plausible to avoid cueing to the correct option.**

*Homogeneity:* A consistent focus and parallel format across answer options means the test-taker can weigh each without construct-irrelevant distractions. Example: for "most likely cause," all options should be diagnoses (not a mix of diagnoses and pathogens).

*Plausibility:* The correct answer should be the "most" correct; distractors should be plausible enough to entice test-takers who don't know the answer. Otherwise the correct answer can be found by elimination. Start by generating the correct answer; plausible parallel distractors are harder to write.

**Rule 5 — Each item should be reviewed to identify and remove technical flaws that add irrelevant difficulty or benefit savvy test-takers.**
The bulk of the text (vignette/case) should precede, not follow, the lead-in. The vignette follows a logical sequence: demographics → history → physical examination → laboratory data → initial treatment → subsequent findings. Use a template. Ask: if options were removed, could a knowledgeable test-taker still answer? Is anything confusing? Any clues a testwise student could exploit? Have a colleague review.

## Chapter 6: Testing Application of Foundational (Basic) and Clinical Knowledge

### Choosing the Topics to Test

Exam content is driven by exam purpose and test-taker population. Specialty exam content is geared toward minimum competence in that specialty at start of practice. A formative midpoint exam differs in focus from an end-of-clerkship exam.

### Determining Level of Cognition to Assess

Items fall into two general categories:
1. **Recall of a Fact** — assesses rote memory (no application). These often begin "Which of the following findings is most likely in a patient with [disease]?" — clinically backwards; patients don't name their disease and ask for signs.
2. **Application of Knowledge** — requires identifying relevant information, interpreting it in context, integrating with known facts, and answering.

**Recall items** — best for formative assessment or simpler concepts that don't lend themselves to vignettes. NBME exams no longer use pure recall items.

**Application items** — preferred for medium-to-high-stakes summative exams. Use vignette-based items requiring higher-order thinking.

### Figure 1 — Recall vs. Application of Knowledge

| | Formative | Summative |
|---|---|---|
| Recall | Easy detection of skill deficits; rapid fire; attention-keeping; helpful in classroom | Best for simple concepts; quantity over quality; may seem artificial; may promote shallow study; no longer on NBME exams |
| Application | Promotes clinical reasoning; supports problem-based / team-based learning; tests recall AND reasoning | Better approximates real life; may need more testing time; integration-of-skills harder to detect deficiencies |

### Example: Recall vs. Application (Same Topic)

**Recall item:**
> A woman is diagnosed with venous thromboembolism. Which of the following is the most appropriate treatment?

**Application item (same topic):**
> A 47-year-old woman comes to the emergency department because of shortness of breath and left lower extremity pain with ambulation. Yesterday she returned from Europe after a 10-hour flight. She has no remarkable medical history and takes only an oral contraceptive daily. Vital signs are within normal limits. Physical examination shows asymmetrical swelling of the left calf of greater than 2 cm compared to the right side. The remainder of the physical examination discloses no abnormalities.

The second version supports multiple possible lead-ins (diagnosis, next diagnostic step, treatment, mechanism), each requiring clinical reasoning.

### Benefits of Application of Knowledge Items

1. Authenticity — requires integrating information to "solve" clinical problems.
2. Items focus on important information, not trivia.
3. Identifies test-takers who have memorized facts but cannot apply them. Test-takers must differentiate relevant from irrelevant information.

### Guidelines for Clinical Vignette Content

- Test application via clinical vignettes posing medical decisions in patient-care situations
- Focus on common or potentially catastrophic problems; avoid "zebras" and esoterica
- Pose clinical decision-making tasks expected of a successful test-taker
- Pose situations that would be handled by a provider/specialist at the target competency
- Focus on tasks the successful test-taker must handle at the next training stage or upon specialty practice
- Focus on areas where clinical reasoning mistakes are commonly made

### Patient Vignette Template (use components as plausible; in this order):

1. **Age, gender** (e.g., 45-year-old woman); add option to indicate self-identified
2. **Site of care** (e.g., emergency department)
3. **Presenting symptoms** (e.g., headache)
4. **Duration of symptoms** (e.g., 2 days)
5. **Patient history** — past medical, family, psychosocial, ROS (if important and plausible)
6. **Physical findings**
7. **Results of diagnostic studies**
8. **Initial treatment, subsequent findings**

### The Shape of a Good Item

A well-constructed one-best-answer item has a distinctive silhouette: a rich clinical scenario (stem), followed by a concise uniform option list.

**Stem checklist:**
- Focuses on important concepts, not trivial facts
- Can be answered without looking at the options
- Includes all relevant facts; no additional data in options
- Is not "tricky" or overly complex
- Is not negatively phrased (no "EXCEPT" or "NOT")

### The Patient Chart / Table Format

A variation using a patient-chart or tabular layout (age, gender, site of care, history of present illness, past medical history, medications, allergies, psychosocial, vitals, physical exam). Best for detailed cases. Use caution with complex scenarios (trauma, multiple comorbidities) where timing and condition changes can be lost.

### The F-type Format (Sequential Item Sets)

F-types: 2–3 items around an unfolding clinical scenario. Rules:
1. Include enough rich clinical context in the initial scenario to support unfolding.
2. Advance the clinical situation in time; provide additional patient info to bring the test-taker to baseline, if necessary.
3. Imply a response to the previous item but **don't turn the next item into a recall type**.

### Guidelines on Writing Lead-ins (see Appendix B for full list by competency)

The lead-in should be a single, clearly formulated question satisfying the cover-the-options rule.

**Example stem + lead-in that stands alone:**
> A 52-year-old man has had increasing dyspnea and cough productive of purulent sputum for 2 days. He has smoked one pack of cigarettes daily for 30 years. Temperature is 37.2°C. Breath sounds are distant with a few rhonchi and wheezes. Leukocyte count is 9000/mm³ with a normal differential. Gram stain of sputum shows numerous neutrophils and gram-negative diplococci. X-ray of the chest shows hyperinflation.
>
> Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

### Difficulty Scales With Vignette Length (same item, three formats)

- **No vignette:** 99% / 90% High/Low correct
- **Short vignette:** 98% / 82% High/Low correct
- **Long vignette:** 98% / 66% High/Low correct

Long vignettes are harder for low-performers because they must synthesize, not just recognize.

### Additional Points on Vignettes

**Verbosity, window dressing, red herrings:** Apparent verbosity can actually reflect good clinical vignettes that assess application. Items should reflect "real-life" tasks where test-takers identify relevant findings and integrate them — multi-step cognitive processes. A template helps prevent unnecessary text while keeping quality high.

**Real patients:** Real patients are complicated; not everything that's complicated is important to assessment. Some window-dressing incidental findings are fine. Red herrings from real cases may be problematic for introductory learners.

**Unreliable histories:** Patients in vignettes should tell the truth; or the provider's interpretation of the patient's story should be given. Do not write "The patient claims to drink only one bottle of beer each night" — this requires a veracity interpretation test-takers can't make from text alone. Instead: "The patient drinks 16 oz of beer with dinner each night" or "The patient's description of his alcohol consumption is contradictory."

## Patient Characteristics (PCs) — New Guidance in the 6th Edition

**Definition:** sex/gender identity, race/ethnicity/country of origin/native language, sexual orientation, occupation/military status, socioeconomic status, disability.

**Purpose:** Encourage thoughtful use of PCs to represent the diversity seen in patient populations. Wording that supports stereotypes or describes behaviors linked to known healthcare disparities can undermine item validity because examinees' responses may be affected by factors external to their clinical knowledge.

### Core Recommendations

1. **Consider including PCs when they:**
   - Add to overall exam-level representativeness (PCs should appear with sufficient frequency to avoid cueing when clinically relevant; consider inclusion even when not critically relevant), OR
   - Are clinically relevant (contribute to diagnosis or distractor quality), OR
   - Are necessary to understand the context (item would be unreasonably difficult or misleading if PCs were excluded)

2. **Avoid PCs that reflect stereotypes** based on race/ethnicity, country of origin, gender, sexual orientation, etc.

3. **Ensure gender and race/ethnicity (and country of origin) are reported as self-identified.**

### Varying PCs — Concrete Suggestions

**Patients and families:**
- Vary gender in physical activities and professions (sports, construction, airline captains)
- Vary mentions of activities reflecting diverse interests (avoid reinforcing stereotypes)
- Vary gender identity and relationships (same-sex couples, same-sex parents, nonbinary, transgender)
- Vary degrees of disability (wheelchair user with pneumonia, communication scenario with neurodivergent patient)
- Vary gender and relationship of caregivers (father brings child to office, niece brings uncle to ED)
- Vary occupation and military status of patients and caregivers
- Vary race/ethnicity and country of origin to add representativeness

**Health care providers:**
- Vary gender of clinical staff (physicians, surgeons, nurses, pharmacists, nutritionists, lab techs, clerical staff)
- Vary specialty of physicians in professionalism, legal/ethical (e.g., provider error), and communication scenarios

**Optional PCs:**
- Some items may include "sex assigned at birth" or "country of origin" as appropriate

### Decision Tree for PC Inclusion

1. **Does the disease/condition occur in other populations with reasonable frequency?**
   - **Yes** → Continue.
   - **No** → Go to 4.
2. **Are PCs necessary for clinical reasoning (item can't be answered without them)?**
   - **Yes** → Include PCs but consider changing population to a less well-known one (Example A).
   - **No** → Go to 3.
3. **Does including PCs carry a risk of stereotyping?**
   - **Yes** → Omit PCs or revise item (Example B).
   - **No** → Include PCs (Example C).
4. **Does PC inclusion make the item too easy (cues the disease)?**
   - **Yes** → Omit PCs to avoid cueing (Example D).
   - **No** → Go to 5.
5. **Does PC omission make the item too difficult?**
   - **Yes** → Include PCs but change population to a less well-known one (Example E).
   - **No** → Include PCs to add to exam-level representativeness (Example F).

### Worked Examples

**A (Dermatologic lesion):** 80-year-old woman, skin lesion — disease occurs in all populations. Changing to a skin-tone less frequently depicted addresses representativeness.

**B (HIV + multiple partners + adherence):** Content risks perpetuating stereotypes — omit unnecessary PCs and leave race/ethnicity "unspecified."

**C (Lactose intolerance, American Indian patient):** ~75% of American Indian adults have lactose intolerance, but it also occurs across other populations; include PC if no stereotype is reinforced, or vary to another ethnicity.

**D (Sarcoidosis, Swedish patient):** Scandinavia has highest known incidence; this PC would cue the answer — leave race/ethnicity "unspecified" or swap to a less cueing one.

**E (Thalassemia, Greek patient):** Name itself derives from Greek; this PC gives the answer — include PCs that don't cue.

**F (Dengue fever, Brazil-origin patient):** Geographical distribution is the primary diagnostic anchor — omitting PC would make the item unfair.

## Structuring Items to Fit Task Competencies

Defined task competencies focus the item writer's testing point. Each competency calls for a slightly different approach. Sample lead-ins organized by competency are in Appendix B. Key competencies below.

### Foundational (Basic) Science

Items requiring understanding and application of basic-science principles. Not answerable from clinical knowledge or pattern recognition alone. Usually framed with a clinical vignette describing patient symptoms, signs, history, lab findings. Example lead-ins:

- Which of the following is the most likely cause/mechanism of this effect?
- Which of the following is the most likely infectious agent?
- Which of the following is the most likely explanation for these findings?
- Which of the following is the most likely location of this patient's lesion?
- Which of the following is the most likely pathogen?
- Which of the following findings is most likely to be increased/decreased in this patient?
- A biopsy specimen is most likely to show which of the following?
- This patient most likely has a defect in which of the following?
- This patient most likely has a deficiency in which of the following enzymes?
- Which of the following cytokines is the most likely cause of this condition?
- Which of the following structures is at greatest risk for damage during this procedure?
- The most appropriate medication for this patient will have which of the following mechanisms of action?

### Diagnosis

Four subcategories: Obtaining/Predicting History and Physical; Selecting/Interpreting Diagnostic Studies; Formulating the Diagnosis; Determining Prognosis/Outcome. See Appendix B for full lead-in lists.

### Management

Topic areas: Health Maintenance and Disease Prevention; Pharmacotherapy; Clinical Interventions/Treatments. In most management items, the examinee must infer the diagnosis to determine the appropriate management. See Appendix B for lead-ins.

## Chapter 7: Using Media as Part of Clinical Vignettes

CBT administration makes it straightforward to add media. Advantages:
- Authenticity for assessing knowledge and skills
- Allows assessment of skills that purely text-based items cannot measure
- Avoids textual cues that benefit savvy test-takers in long vignettes

**Considerations when adding media:**
- Content area being covered (cardiology auscultation → ECG image; neurologic exam → video)
- Novelty (may require tutorial orientation; simplicity helps)
- Memorability (students may share memorable features; write multiple items per image to offset)
- Richness of patient description required (media + long vignette = more authentic, but more test-taker time)

### Types of Media

- Static images (imaging, patient photographs)
- Videos (physical exam findings, patient-provider interactions)
- Interactive media (avatars)
- Sound files (heart/breath sounds)

### Content Areas Best Served By Media

- Dermatologic and musculoskeletal examination findings (research shows faster response time with media vs text)
- Cardiology (heart sounds)
- Neurologic examination findings
- Ethical and communication scenarios (intonation, body language can't be conveyed in text)

### Media Creation Tips

**DO:**
- Use a plain background
- Avoid visual cues (office equipment, paintings, identifiable rooms)
- Record in a well-lit room
- Have the patient wear plain clothes or hospital gown (no logos)
- Keep background/clothing consistent across multiple patients/exams
- Have the provider talk to the patient as in a normal exam
- Have the provider avoid using names
- Leave videos as recorded; provide editing instructions separately
- Limit video length to 30 seconds
- Provide signed patient consent form

**DON'T:**
- Pick a brightly colored or identifiable room
- Allow brightly colored/logo clothing or jewelry
- Show provider or patient faces if not essential
- Explain everything in detail or narrate
- Add fade-in/fade-out transitions
- Resize/change video dimensions

### Media Accessibility

- **Hearing impairment:** captions/subtitles on audio-bearing videos; visual waveform for clinical audio (e.g., heart sounds)
- **Visual impairment:** text descriptions for images (written so they don't unfairly cue the answer); minimum 8pt font or zoom; sans-serif fonts
- **Color blindness:** avoid red/green pairing; use black and shades of gray where possible; use patterns (not just color) in bar graphs

## Appendix A: Quick-Reference Guide to Approaching Item Writing

1. **Getting Started** — Consider curriculum, classic/common presentations from your practice, what you want your test population to recognize or not miss.
2. **The Testing Point** — Start with what you want to test before writing. Where is the site of care?
3. **The Site of Care** — Clinic, hospital, ED, ICU — match the competency level.
4. **The Case** — Frame the vignette around your testing point. View sample items in Chapter 6 as style guides. Consider if media could replace text effectively.
5. **The Lead-in** — Use Appendix B; match your testing point objective.
6. **The Option Set** — Have enough information in the vignette to support the key and link to distractors. Ensure option set is free of flaws from Chapter 3.

## Appendix C: NBME Retired Item Formats

NBME has retired the following item types due to flaws that negatively impact measurement. Listed for reference only — not used in current NBME exams but may appear in legacy instructor materials.

- **B-type** — Matching sets without a clinical vignette or lead-in; unclear question.
- **C-type** — Multiple true-false; requires test-takers to guess item writer's intent on how "true" an option must be.
- **D-type** — Complex matching; confusing directions, poor discrimination.
- **H-type** — Paired quantitative comparison; ambiguous threshold for "relevant difference."
- **I-type** — Paired conditional relationships; fewer options = higher guessing rate, trivial focus.
- **K-type** — Multiple true-false sets; cueing via response combinations, can't assess clinical judgment.
- **R-type** — Extended matching sets with shared option pool; risk of nonfunctional distractors, high reading load.

**Current NBME item formats:** A-type, F-type, G-type only.
