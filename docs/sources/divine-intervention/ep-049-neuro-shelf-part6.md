# ADEM (ACUTE DISSEMINATED ENCEPHALOMYELITIS)

Multiple neuro deficits in a kid after VZV vaccine or after URI → ADEM
Pathophys: Inflammatory demyelinating lesions in brain & spinal cord
Dx: MRI
Prognosis: Full recovery

# MULTIPLE SCLEROSIS

Classic presentation: Female in her 30s with disparate neuro deficits
Uhthoff's phenomenon: sxs worse in heat
CN2 pathology: Optic neuritis (unilateral eye pain + decreased visual acuity + APD)
Associated Vit deficiency: Vit D (higher prevalence farther from equator)
Dx: MRI brain & spinal cord (multiple demyelinating lesions separated in space & time)
LP findings: Oligoclonal bands
Exam findings: APD, Lhermitte's sign (electric shock with neck flexion)

## Treatment
Acute exacerbation: Very high-dose corticosteroids
Chronic/DMARDs: Fingolimod, Natalizumab (AE: JC virus reactivation → PML), Glatiramer, Rituximab, Interferon-beta

## Bladder Dysfunction in MS
Urge incontinence → oxybutynin, trospium, darifenacin/solifenacin, tolterodine (detrusor hyperactive → quiet with antimuscarinic)
Overflow incontinence → bethanechol, neostigmine (detrusor hypotonia → activate with muscarinic agonists/AChE-inhibitors); alternative: intermittent self-cath; high PVRs

## Spasticity Treatment
Baclofen (GABA-B receptor agonist), Tizanidine (alpha-2 agonist → decreases NE release), Dantrolene (CCB), Benzos (GABA-A receptor agonist), Botulinum toxin for localized spasticity

# VASOVAGAL SYNCOPE

Pt sees blood or has emotional stressor then passes out → vasovagal syncope
Dx: Tilt table test
Tx: Midodrine (alpha-1 agonist)

# HEMORRHAGIC STROKE IN HEMOPHILIA

Most common cause of death in patients with Factor 8/9 deficiencies: Hemorrhagic stroke
Pathophys: Defect of secondary hemostasis
Inheritance: X-linked recessive

# STROKE MANAGEMENT

## Stepwise Diagnostic Testing
1st: Non-con head CT (differentiate hemorrhagic vs ischemic)
If NCCT negative: Diffusion-weighted MRI
Additional: Carotid artery duplex US of internal carotid arteries, Echo (LA appendage thrombus or PFO)

## Treatment
Ischemic stroke initial tx: Aspirin (or clopidogrel, dipyridamole)
Do NOT give anticoagulant unless AFib
If high suspicion for SAH but NCCT negative: LP → look for xanthochromia
SAH tx: Lower BP + nimodipine

# FACIAL NERVE (CN7) — UMN vs LMN

## LMN CN7 Lesion
Paralysis of ipsilateral upper AND lower facial muscles, dry mouth, loss of lacrimation → LMN CN7 lesion
Pathway: CNs (except CN2) are LMNs; UMNs comprise corticobulbar tract (decussate prior to synapsing with CNs)
CN7 also carries parasympathetic fibers
Why ipsilateral: Decussation happens upstream to CN7

## UMN CN7 Lesion (Corticobulbar)
Contralateral lower facial weakness with forehead sparing + loss of taste anterior 2/3 tongue → UMN CN7 lesion
Why contralateral: Corticobulbar tract decussates BEFORE synapsing on CN7 nucleus
Why upper face sparing: Bilateral corticobulbar pathways synapse on CN7 nucleus (need to knock out both to affect upper face)

# CRANIAL NERVE REVIEW

## CN5 (Trigeminal)
Loss of facial sensation + jaw deviation to ipsilateral side + impaired corneal reflex → CN5
Facial sensation: CN5
Corneal reflex: Afferent = CN5, Efferent = CN7
Motor innervation: Muscles of mastication (masseter, pterygoids, temporalis)

## CN1 (Olfactory)
Anosmia → CN1
Associated syndrome: Kallmann syndrome (anosmia + hypogonadotropic hypogonadism)
Pathophys: Failure of GnRH neurons & olfactory neurons to migrate

## CN3 (Oculomotor)
Mydriasis with eye deviated down and out → CN3
CN3 innervates all eye muscles except superior oblique (CN4) & lateral rectus (CN6)
CN3 carries parasympathetic fibers to pupillary constrictors
Why down & out: Lateral rectus → abduction, Superior oblique → depression
Causes: PCom aneurysm, Uncal herniation
Compression lesions: Mydriasis often happens before eye deviation (parasympathetic fibers run on outside of CN3)
Ischemic lesions (e.g. DM): Eye deviation happens first

## CN6 (Abducens)
Horizontal diplopia with failed abduction on lateral conjugate gaze → CN6 lesion

## CN4 (Trochlear)
Vertical diplopia with jaw tilted towards side of lesion + difficulty going down stairs → CN4 lesion
Only CN that decussates (emerges dorsally & crosses to opposite side)
Head tilt is TOWARDS side of lesion

## CN8 (Vestibulocochlear)
Sensorineural hearing loss + vertigo + abnormal caloric test → CN8
Caloric test: COWS (cold opposite, warm same)

## CN12 (Hypoglossal)
Deviation of protruded tongue to ipsilateral side → CN12
"Lick your wounds"
Innervates all intrinsic tongue muscles except palatoglossus (CN10)

## CN11 (Spinal Accessory)
Weakness in shoulder shrug + problems turning head to opposite side → CN11

## CN9 and CN10
Loss of taste posterior 1/3 tongue + dysphagia + absent gag reflex → CN9 or 10
Taste posterior 1/3 tongue → CN9
General sensation posterior 1/3 tongue → CN9
Taste extreme posterior tongue → CN10
General sensation extreme posterior tongue → CN10
Gag reflex: Afferent = CN9, Efferent = CN10

## Optic Chiasm
Bitemporal hemianopsia ("tunnel vision") + APD → optic chiasm compression
Causes: Pituitary adenoma, Craniopharyngioma

# PUPILLARY DEFECTS

## APD (Afferent Pupillary Defect) — assume R-sided
Shine light into R eye → nothing
Shine light into L eye → bilateral constriction

## EPD (Efferent Pupillary Defect) — assume R-sided
Shine light into R eye → only L constriction
Shine light into L eye → only L constriction
Eye with defect doesn't constrict regardless

# PEDIATRIC BRAIN TUMORS (REVIEW)

Most common pediatric brain tumor: Pilocytic astrocytoma (posterior fossa)
Hemorrhagic lesion in cerebellum with rosettes → medulloblastoma (2nd MC; location: cerebellum; associated: Turcot syndrome)
Brain tumor that drains "motor oil fluid" and is calcified → craniopharyngioma (Rathke's pouch; sella turcica; bitemporal hemianopsia)
Kid with abdominal mass crossing midline with calcifications → neuroblastoma (posterior mediastinum; opsoclonus myoclonus syndrome)

# NEUROLOGIC LOCALIZATION

## Cervical Radiculopathy
Neck pain radiating to arm with specific dermatomes + multiple peripheral nerves involved → cervical radiculopathy
Pathophys: Problem with nerve ROOT (vs myelopathy = problem with spinal cord)

## Internal Capsule Lesion (Subcortical)
Complete paralysis of face, arms, legs with no sensory losses + contralateral "clumsiness" → internal capsule lesion
Pathway: Motor fibers condense and form posterior limb of internal capsule
Artery: Lenticulostriate arteries

## Cortical Stroke
Inability to calculate + eyes looking towards side of lesion + facial paralysis contralateral + UMN signs → cortical stroke
Acalculia → dominant parietal lobe lesion
Nondominant parietal lobe (often R) → L-sided hemineglect
Eyes looking toward side of lesion → frontal eye field lesion
R frontal eye field → L PPRF → controls L abducens and R oculomotor; R frontal eye field lesion → no communication to L PPRF → R PPRF unopposed → R deviation

## Thalamic Lesion (Subcortical)
Complete sensory loss on L + severe pain on R → thalamus lesion

## Cerebellar Lesion
Ataxia, past pointing, dysdiadochokinesia, intention tremor → ipsilateral cerebellar lesion

## Spinal Cord Compression
Bowel/bladder dysfunction + UMN and LMN findings + sensory level → spinal cord compression (e.g. cauda equina)
UMN findings below level of lesion, LMN findings at level of lesion

## Lateral Medullary Syndrome (Wallenberg)
Trouble swallowing + problems with eye adduction on conjugate gaze + sensory loss L face + sensory loss R body → Wallenberg
Sensory loss L face → L spinal trigeminal nucleus
Sensory loss R body → L spinothalamic tract
Trouble swallowing → nucleus ambiguus lesion (CN9/10)
Affected arteries: PICA

## Subthalamic Nucleus
Hemiballismus and bradykinesia → contralateral subthalamic nucleus lesion

## Peripheral Nerve
Stocking/glove distribution sensory loss + LMN findings → peripheral nerve lesion

## Muscle Disease
Trouble rising from seated position + elevated CK → polymyositis/dermatomyositis

## NMJ
Fatigable muscle weakness → NMJ issue (e.g. MG)

# BASILAR SKULL FRACTURE

4 signs: Bruising behind mastoid (Battle's sign), Raccoon eyes, CSF rhinorrhea, CSF otorrhea

# PERIPHERAL NERVE vs RADICULOPATHY

One nerve involved, stocking-glove distribution → peripheral neuropathy
Multiple nerves involved, dermatomal distribution → radiculopathy

# PERIPHERAL NERVE INJURIES

## Upper Extremity
Weakness in shoulder abduction/deltoid paralysis → axillary nerve (anterior shoulder dislocation, surgical neck fracture)
Weakness in wrist extension → radial nerve (midshaft fracture of humerus; reflex: triceps)
Problems with thumb abduction + sensory loss ventrolateral 3.5 digits → median nerve compression in CTS (compression by flexor retinaculum; RFs: pregnancy, RA, hypothyroidism)
Weakness with finger spread + problems with flexion at MCPs and extension at IPs → ulnar nerve (dorsal interossei: finger aBduction + MCP flexion + IP extension)

## Lower Extremity
Weakness in foot eversion and dorsiflexion → peroneal nerve (DroPED)
Weakness in foot inversion and plantarflexion → tibial nerve (TIP)
Problems with hip flexion → femoral nerve (F for Femoral and Flexion)
Problems with hip adduction + cannot internally rotate → obturator nerve (lots of AIR between thighs = Adduction/Internal Rotation)

# REFLEX NERVE ROOTS

Biceps → C5/6
Triceps → C7/8 (radial nerve)
Knee extension/patellar tendon → L3/4
Plantarflexion/ankle jerk → S1/2

# DERMATOME LEVELS

Nipple line → T4
Xiphoid process → T7
Umbilicus → T10
