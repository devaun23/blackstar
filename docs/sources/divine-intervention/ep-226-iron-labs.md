# FERRITIN (iron stores)
## Increased
- Anemia of chronic disease: hepcidin sequesters iron on bone marrow macrophages
- Lead poisoning: inhibits ferrochelatase and ALAD → iron builds up around mitochondria
- Hereditary hemochromatosis: C282Y/HFE gene mutation

## Decreased
- Iron deficiency anemia: lacking iron altogether

# TIBC (total iron binding capacity)
Opposite of ferritin: lots of iron stored → won't seek more iron

# TRANSFERRIN SATURATION
% saturation of protein that moves iron in bloodstream

## Increased
Lead poisoning: iron overload state

## Decreased
- Anemia of chronic disease: hepcidin 1) locks up iron in macrophages 2) prevents reabsorption in GI tract
- Iron deficiency anemia

# MCHC (mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration)
Concentration = mass/volume

## Increased
Hereditary spherocytosis: AD mutation in spectrin/ankyrin/band3.2 → too little membrane → decreased volume → increased MCHC
Dx: eosin-5-maleimide test, osmotic fragility; Tx: splenectomy

## Decreased
Iron deficiency anemia: decreased hemoglobin = decreased mass

# FREE ERYTHROCYTE PROTOPORPHYRIN
Binds with iron to create heme

## Increased
Iron deficiency anemia: "if iron doesn't show up, protoporphyrin is left at the altar"

## Decreased
- Porphyria cutanea tarda (UROD deficiency)
- Acute intermittent porphyria (PBG deaminase deficiency)
- Lead poisoning

# RETICULOCYTE COUNT
RBC lifespan = 4 months; if hemolysis → lifespan = 30 days → ramp up production

## Increased
Hemolytic anemia, Sickle cell disease, Hereditary spherocytosis
Tend to run out of folate/raw materials quickly

## Decreased
Iron deficiency anemia, B12/folate deficiency, Aplastic crisis

# RDW
## Increased
Iron deficiency anemia: body tries to keep up concentration by varying MCV (big ↔ small)
