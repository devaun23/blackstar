# Palliative Care / Hospice

Pt with terminal lung cancer in hospice that's not really eating. What meds can you give them?
Megestrol (progestin analog)
Dronabinol (cannabinoid)
Corticosteroids
Don't improve survival or reduce morbidity

Pt with terminal lung cancer in hospice with depression. What meds would you try?
Stimulants: methylphenidate
If super anxious, consider benzo
SSRIs will take too long to act

Pt with metastatic malignancy complains of SOB. What meds would you try?
Opioids (e.g. morphine) + bowel regimen

Pt on chemo with severe n/v. What meds would you try?
Ondansetron (serotonin receptor antagonist)
Adverse effect? QT prolongation

Pt with terminal GBM. Days to weeks to live. Feels nauseous in the context of increased ICP. NBS?
Glucocorticoids to reduce ICP

Pt with burning & tingling in extremities (neuropathic pain). What meds would you try?
TCAs, SNRI (e.g. duloxetine), Gabapentin/pregabalin

Pt with cancer pain & hx of depression on treatment. Started on pain med and started to have fevers + myoclonus → serotonin syndrome 2/2 tramadol
Tramadol is usually not a good choice for cancer pain because it's not that strong and has lots of interactions

Pt with widely metastatic cancer to liver & kidneys presents with severe pain. What pain med? Fentanyl patch
Avoid morphine in liver & kidney disease

Pt with bad cancer pain. Morphine dose was increased at last visit. Still in severe pain. NBS? Increase dose or frequency of opioid regimen

Pt with metastatic malignancy in hospice complains of pain. What drug should be avoided? Meperidine (Can cause seizures)

## Palliative vs Hospice
Palliative care does NOT preclude life-prolonging therapy
Hospice: physician must determine pt has less than 6 months to live

# Ethics

Physician that smells of alcohol. NBS? Report to higher authority (supervisor, ethics committee, medical board)

Medical error was made. NBS? Admit error to pt. Data suggests most likely outcome is physician NOT getting sued

Physician-assisted suicide: Illegal in every state

## Autonomy and Capacity
60-70s yo pt that is very ill. Some medical therapies remain but pt is not interested. NBS? Respect pt's wishes as pt understands situation, understands risks & benefits, communicates choice clearly

Pt requests therapy that is not standard of care and won't affect outcomes. NBS? No obligation to administer futile therapy

Pt requests abortion. Physician doesn't feel comfortable. NBS? Transfer care to another physician

Old senile person not taking meds, keeps getting admitted. NBS? Appoint legal guardian

Adolescent < 18 yo → parents make decisions
Exceptions: Substance abuse, Mental health, Reproductive health

## Capacity vs Competence
Capacity: understands situation, understands risks & benefits, clearly communicates decision — Determined by physician
Examples of lacking capacity: Delirium/AMS, Under influence of drugs
Competence: Determined by legal system

## Decision-Making Standards
23 yo M overdosed on opioids, now comatose. Next-of-kin withdraws care because pt said "I don't want to be on life support." → Substituted judgment
Pt comatose. Surrogate chooses option A because physician shows better outcomes → Best interest standard
Physician treating unresponsive trauma pt does what is medically indicated → Best interest standard

## Advance Directives
Healthcare POA: designated person makes decisions if pt can't
Living will: document where pt outlines interventions they would/would not want
Surrogate decision-maker order (no POA): Spouse → adult children → parent → adult sibling

## HIPAA
Don't disclose pt information to family without authorization
Exceptions: Protecting pt (suicidal), Protecting public (homicidal), Following the law

# Quality Improvement

## Handoffs
Should happen in person
Provide key info in standardized fashion
Use if-then statements
Key active problems, key medications, labs to f/u

## Error Analysis
If a medical error occurs, first thing: Analyze the error!
Fishbone/Ishikawa diagram — Keep identifying contributing factors

## PDSA Cycle (Core QI principle)
P = plan (planning the intervention)
D = do (putting into practice)
S = study (study results)
A = act (refine the intervention)

## Diagnostic Errors / Cognitive Biases
Availability bias: Physician assumes diagnosis because they've seen similar presentation before
Anchoring bias: Depending too heavily on initial piece of information or prior diagnosis
Blind obedience bias: Resident doesn't question attending's diagnosis
Premature closure: Making diagnosis without considering other possibilities

# Screening — IPV
HITS (does your partner hurt, insult, threaten, or scream at you?)
HARK (humiliation, afraid of partner, rape, kick)
STAB (slapped, threatened)

# Screening — Alcohol Abuse
FIRST quantify drinking:
Woman OR >65 yo: >7 drinks/week or >3 drinks/one sitting
Man: >14 drinks/week or >4 drinks/one sitting
THEN employ formal screen:
CAGE (Cut Down, Annoyed, Guilt, Eye opener)
AUDIT-C is better than CAGE

Clues suggesting alcoholism on NBMEs:
Megaloblastic anemia (MCV > 100)
High GGT
AST/ALT > 2:1

Signs/sxs of alcohol withdrawal → give benzos (hallucinosis, DTs)
Remember thiamine before glucose if alcoholic comes to ED (avoids Wernicke-Korsakoff)
If delirious from EtOH withdrawal: Don't give antipsychotic (can cause seizures) → Tx: Benzos

# Substance Abuse Treatment
Opioid addiction → Suboxone (buprenorphine/naloxone)
Opiate withdrawal → Clonidine

Smoking cessation:
Nicotine replacement therapy
Bupropion (NDRI) — CI: eating disorder
Varenicline (nicotinic receptor partial agonist)
Most effective? Combination > varenicline > bupropion > NRT
Never choose e-cigarette on NBME

# Screening Guidelines

## AAA
US for men age 65-76 if they've ever smoked or have family hx

## Osteoporosis
Women > 65 yo
Women < 65 yo with risk factor (anorexia, prolonged steroid use)
FRAX > 9.3
T < -2.5 → bisphosphonate

## Cervical Cancer
Age <21 → NO Pap
Age 21-29 → Pap q3y
Age 30+ → + cotesting Pap q5y
HIV or immunodeficiency → yearly Pap
Hysterectomy for benign reasons → no screening
Hysterectomy for endometrial hyperplasia/cancer → Pap of vaginal cuff

## Colon Cancer
Methods: Colonoscopy q10y, Flex sig q5y, Flex sig q10y + annual FIT, Annual FIT, Annual FOB
No family hx → age 50
Family hx → age 40 OR 10 years before dx of relative (whichever earlier)

## Lung Cancer
Annual low dose CT scan
Age 55-80, Current smoker OR quit < 15 years ago, 30 pack-year hx

## Prostate Cancer
Shared decision-making
> age 70 → NOT recommended

## Breast Cancer
USPSTF: start at age 50, q2y
ACS: start at age 40, q1y

# Vaccines

Healthcare workers → Hep B
Influenza every year in the fall
1 dose Tdap + Td booster every 10 years
Tdap in every pregnancy at 27-36 weeks
Varicella can't be given to infants < 1 year old

HPV: Girls age 9-26, Boys 11-21, MSM 11-26

Meningococcal vaccine: Teens going to college, Military recruits

Asplenic or functionally asplenic → vaccinate against encapsulated organisms (Strep pneumo, H. flu, Neisseria meningitidis)

Pt on eculizumab or terminal complement deficiency → vaccinate against Neisseria meningitidis

Pt with egg allergy CAN get influenza vaccine

## Live Attenuated Vaccines
Varicella, Zostavax, MMR, Yellow fever, Intranasal influenza, Oral polio
Who shouldn't get: Babies <1 year, Pregnant women, HIV with CD4 <200

## Inactivated Vaccines
Hep A/B, Tdap (toxoid), Pneumococcal, Meningococcal, Influenza (injection), Polio (injection), Shingrix

## Pneumococcal Vaccine
>65 yo → just PPSV23 OR PCV13 then after 1 year PPSV23
<65 yo with certain conditions → PCV13 then after 8 weeks PPSV23:
Asplenic*, Sickle cell*, Immunocompromised*, HIV, CKD, Nephrotic syndrome, Transplant, Hematologic malignancy, Cochlear implant or CSF leak
*get second dose of PPSV23 5 years later
<65 yo with chronic disease → just PPSV23:
DM, Heart disease, Liver disease, Lung disease, Smoking, Alcoholism
