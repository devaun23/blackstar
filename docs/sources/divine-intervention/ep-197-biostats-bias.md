# Selection Bias
People in study are a bad representation of population
Examples:
- Berkson's bias: using hospital population (sicker than general pop)
- Attrition bias: differences in loss to follow up — Solution? Intention-to-treat analysis
- Selecting from particular geographic area
- Volunteer bias: participants differ from non-participants

# Measurement Bias
The way data is obtained distorts information
Examples:
- Hawthorne effect: people act differently when observed
- Pygmalion effect / observer-expectancy bias: researcher subconsciously influences participants to produce expected result
- Solution? Blinding

# Lead-Time Bias
Confusing early detection with increased survival
Cancer X causes death 10 years after first mutation. Detect at year 5 → live 5 years after dx. Detect at year 3 → live 7 years after dx. BUT still die at year 10.
True benefit only if treatment extends survival beyond natural history

# Length-Time Bias (Late-Look Bias)
Never encounter worst cases — people with severe disease die before screening
Those screened have more indolent disease → appears screening saves lives
Solution? Stratify by disease severity

# Recall Bias
Especially relevant to case-control studies
Example: Mothers of children with birth defects remember drugs better than mothers of normal infants
Mitigation: Reduce time between exposure & recall, Corroborate information

# Confounding
Third factor positively or negatively associated with BOTH exposure AND outcome
Example: Sunburn associated with ice cream consumption (confounder = summertime)
If stratification eliminates difference → confounder

# Effect Modification
If stratification DOESN'T erase effects → effect modification
Example: Drug works better in obese patients → obesity is an effect modifier
Example: Cancer risk in smokers even higher among heavy drinkers
