# PULMONARY FUNCTION TESTS — OVERVIEW

## When to order PFTs
- Pt long term smoker with cavitary lesions on CXR and CT with hypercalcemia → SC cancer of the lung (paraneoplastic PTHrP)
  - NBS: Consider PFTs (FEV1) — understand lung function before resection
  - What's a reasonable FEV1 compatible with life? Approx. 1 Liter
- Pt with RA about to start methotrexate → PFTs before starting (methotrexate can be toxic to lungs)
- Pt started on methotrexate, amiodarone, busulfan, or bleomycin → PFTs to establish baseline

## PFTs for neuromuscular disease
Pt with bloody diarrhea and SOB → Guillain-Barré (ascending muscle paralysis)
If it involves the airway → Intubate!
What is used to monitor progression of GB? Spirometry

# RESTRICTIVE VS OBSTRUCTIVE LUNG DISEASE

## Restrictive
Pt with fibrosis — lungs are scarred down, like shrink wrap
Lung volume? Decreased
FEV1/FVC is normal or mildly increased (getting little in, only getting a little out)

## Obstructive
Pt with big lung volume, on CXR flattened diaphragm or hyperinflated lung. Air trapping.
Chronic increased volume → lung dilation → systolic function impaired (hard to get air out)
FEV1/FVC ratio goes DOWN

## Lung Volumes
- Total lung capacity (TLC) = all the air you can take in
- Forced vital capacity (FVC) = all the air you try to blow out forcibly
- Residual volume = TLC - FVC
- FEV1 = how much air is blown out in one second (most people: 80-90%)

## FEF 25-75%
Surrogate to FEV1/FVC for OBSTRUCTIVE disease
If FEF is down = dealing with obstructive diseases

## Percent Predicted
Population measure: 70-80%
If <70% in FEV1 = probably have some lung pathology
If 120% = higher than normal lung volumes → think obstructive

# DLCO (DIFFUSION CAPACITY)

Gas exchange test for the lungs. How good of a job does lung perform with gas exchange?
Oxygen traverses alveolar membrane → wall of pulmonary capillaries → blood vessels

## Decreased DLCO
- Pulmonary fibrosis (more fibrotic tissue in the way = less efficient diffusion)
- Emphysema (even though it's obstructive) — destruction of proteases → no membranes/alveoli → no surface area for diffusion

## Increased DLCO
- Goodpasture (defect with Ab on Type IV collagen) — blood in lung, O2 bathes blood directly
- Wegener/GPA (sinusitis, kidney problem, c-ANCA, hemoptysis) — blood in lung, increased DLCO
- Pulmonary hemorrhage — DLCO is high

## Normal DLCO
- Extrapulmonary causes of restrictive lung disease
- Chronic bronchitis (obstructive disease with potential normal DLCO)
  - BUT emphysema = obstructive with low DLCO (reduced surface area)

# METHACHOLINE CHALLENGE

Pt with asthma with equivocal spirometry → NBS: Methacholine challenge
Mechanism: Muscarinic receptor agonist → causes bronchoconstriction

Ergotamine challenge → Prinzmetal angina or variant angina (promotes vasoconstriction)

# OXYGEN DELIVERY

## Oxygen delivery equation
O2 delivery = Cardiac Output x Hemoglobin x O2 Sat x 1.34 + (0.0031 x PaO2)

- PAO2 = partial pressure of oxygen at Alveoli
- PaO2 = partial pressure of oxygen at pulmonary capillary/blood vessels

## Clinical scenarios
What happens to patients with Hb 4?
- O2 delivery will drop (cause Hb dropped)
- Compensate: Increase Cardiac Output → risk of high output heart failure
- Increase O2 concentration in liquid part doesn't improve much (0.0031 factor)

## CO poisoning
CO takes the spot of Oxygen in Hb (normally has 4 spots for oxygen)
Patient becomes hypoxemic

# A-a GRADIENT

Spread between PAO2 and PaO2
PAO2 = 150 - (PaCO2 x 1.25)
Normal PAO2 is ~100 (PaCO2 is normally ~40)

## Normal A-a gradient = 10 or less
Don't lose a lot of O2 between alveoli and artery

## Elevated A-a gradient (something in the lungs preventing proper oxygenation)
- Pulmonary embolism (blood not flowing through part of the lung → PaO2 goes down)
- Right-to-left shunt — PFO, VSD, or ASD with Eisenmenger physiology (blood bypasses the lung)
- Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (Osler-Weber-Rendu) — AV malformations, blood not passing through capillaries

## Normal A-a gradient with hypoxemia (extrapulmonary causes)
- Overdose on opioids — RR down, not receiving enough oxygen in the first place
- High elevations — less oxygen content in the atmosphere
