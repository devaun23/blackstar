# PALLIATIVE CARE PRINCIPLES
For seriously ill patients AND their families — not only for dying patients
No age requirement; can be given at ANY stage of illness (even curative intent)

# DOCUMENTS
Living will: patient's directions for life-sustaining treatment if incapacitated
Durable POA: designated person for broader healthcare decisions
POLST: physician orders for current situation (not hypothetical)
If patient is lucid → their own decisions supersede all documents

# WHO ANALGESIC LADDER
VAS 1-3: Step 1 (non-opioid); VAS 4-6: Step 2 (weak opioid); VAS >7: Step 3 (strong opioid)
For opioids not enough → increase dose, titrate, or add rescue doses

# PAIN BY TYPE
Bone mets → bisphosphonates or calcitonin
GI obstruction from mass → glycopyrrolate (muscarinic antagonist, reduces bowel contractions)
Neuropathic → gabapentin, pregabalin
Tumor infiltrating nerves/cord compression → STEROIDS (e.g. Pancoast tumor)

# DYSPNEA IN CANCER
DOC: opioids (esp morphine) — decrease O2 consumption, dilate pulmonary vessels, blunt response to hypoxia/hypercapnia
Obstructing airway → stent; Massive pleural effusion → thoracentesis
Pancreatic duct obstruction → stent (improves QOL not survival)

# NAUSEA
DOC: ondansetron (5-HT3 antagonist); Alt: metoclopramide (dopamine antagonist; AE: EPS → NMS risk)
Aprepitant (NK-1 antagonist)
If NMS develops: Tx dantrolene; MC renal complication: rhabdomyolysis → ATN
Nausea from bowel obstruction → steroids (decrease edema)

# CONSTIPATION (opioid-induced)
Ppx: Senna (stimulant laxative)
Tx: methylcellulose/psyllium (need fluids!), PEG, lactulose, methylnaltrexone (mu receptor antagonist)
High-grade bowel obstruction → do NOT give laxatives (risk perforation/peritonitis)
SBO → NGT to wall suction; LBO (sigmoid volvulus) → sigmoidoscopy + rectal tube

# ANOREXIA & CACHEXIA
Pathophys: TNF-alpha + abnormal energy metabolism
Tx: megestrol/medroxyprogesterone (appetite stimulants); steroids; dronabinol (synthetic cannabinoid)

# HOSPICE
Life expectancy <6 months; can be at home or facility
Goal: comfort, not cure — symptom management

KEY: Palliative care ≠ dying. You get it because you have a serious illness.
