# SSRI

- MOA: Inhibit 5-HT reuptake
- Uses: Depression, Anxiety, Bulimia, Binge-eating, PTSD, Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder

## Individual SSRIs

1. Fluoxetine (Prozac): Safe for pregnancy, children, adolescents; Bulimia Nervosa (reduces binging/purging)
2. Paroxetine (Paxil): Not commonly used
3. Sertraline (Zoloft): GAD in children with functional impairment; Postpartum depression (safe in breastfeeding); Social anxiety; Least sexual dysfunction, least cardiac interactions, least sedation; Most GI effects
4. Fluvoxamine (Luvox): 1st line OCD
5. Citalopram (Celexa): SE = QT prolongation; Calming SSRI
6. Escitalopram (Lexapro): Most selective SSRI, less drug-drug interactions; Calming SSRI

## SSRI Uses

- MDD, PTSD (+Prazosin for nightmares), Frontotemporal Dementia, Body Dysmorphic Disorder

## SSRI Side Effects

- Early: Headache, Nausea, Insomnia (GI distress from widespread serotonin receptors in GI)
- Late: Sexual dysfunction/anorgasmia, Weight gain
- Serotonin syndrome, SIADH
- Black box warning: Increases suicidal thinking/behavior in children and adults <25

## Antidepressant Discontinuation Syndrome

- Onset 2-4 days post abrupt stop
- Clinical features: Fatigue, chills, myalgias, rhinorrhea
- Treatment: Reinstitute serotonergic antidepressant

## SSRI Dose Adjustment

- Lower dose if nausea, diarrhea, headaches, increased anxiety/insomnia/somnolence
- Anxiety patients: Start lower, increase gradually over several weeks

# SNRI

- MOA: Inhibit 5-HT and NE reuptake
- Uses: Depression, GAD, Diabetic Neuropathy

## Individual SNRIs

1. Atomoxetine: Non-stimulant for ADHD
2. Venlafaxine: Migraine prophylaxis, Social Anxiety, Panic Disorder
3. Duloxetine/Milnacipran: Fibromyalgia, Neuropathic pain
4. Desvenlafaxine/Levomilnacipran: SE = Blurry vision, Stimulant, Sedation, Nausea

- Common SE: Tachycardia, Hypertension

# Atypical Antidepressants

## Bupropion (NE/DA Reuptake Inhibitor)

- Uses: Depression, Seasonal Affective Disorder, Cocaine abuse, ADHD, Smoking cessation (as Varenicline)
- Desirable in patients with low motivation, weight loss goals, or insomnia
- SE: Lowers seizure threshold (contraindicated in Bulimia Nervosa)
- Lacks sexual side effects

## Mirtazapine (alpha-2 antagonist, 5-HT antagonist)

- Used for SE: Appetite increase, weight gain, somnolence

## Trazodone

- Use: Lack of sleep (insomnia)

## Vortioxetine

- Multimodal antidepressant

# MAO-I

- Phenelzine, Tranylcypromine
- Indication: Atypical Depression

# TCA

- Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline (safer in elderly), Imipramine, Desipramine, Clomipramine (2nd line OCD)
- Indication: Pain
- SE: Cardiotoxicity (3 C's — Cardiotoxicity, Coma, Convulsions)

## TCA Overdose

- CNS toxicity: Mental status changes, drowsiness, delirium, coma, convulsions
- Cardiotoxicity: QRS prolongation >100 msec -> ventricular arrhythmias; Hypotension (leading cause of mortality); Tachycardia
- Anticholinergic toxicity: Dilated pupils, Hyperthermia, Flushed dry skin, Intestinal ileus
- Inhibition of: Central/peripheral mACh-R (delirium, mydriasis, anhidrosis, tachycardia, ileus), Peripheral alpha1-R (vasodilation), Cardiac fast Na channels (arrhythmia, death), Presynaptic NE/5HT reuptake (antidepressant effects), H1-R (sedation, increased appetite)
- Treatment: Supportive, Activated charcoal within 2 hours (unless ileus), NaHCO3 (shortens QRS, reverses Na channel blockade)

# Typical Antipsychotics

- MOA: Block D2-R, increase cAMP
- Uses: Schizophrenia, Delirium, Tourette, Huntington Disease, OCD
- Drugs: Haloperidol, Trifluoperazine, Fluphenazine, Chlorpromazine (SE: Corneal deposits), Thioridazine (SE: Retinal deposits)

## Side Effects

- Anti-Dopamine: EPS (Dystonia, Parkinsonism, Akathisia)
- Anti-HaM: Antihistamine (sedation), Alpha-1 blockade (hypotension), Anticholinergic (memory changes)
- Hyperprolactinemia (also seen with Risperidone)

# Atypical Antipsychotics

- MOA: Block 5-HT2 and D2
- Uses: Schizophrenia, Depression, Mania

## Individual Atypicals

### Aripiprazole (Partial Dopamine Agonist)

- Augmented with SSRI for MDD
- SE: Metabolic syndrome, Akathisia

### Clozapine (Multireceptor Antagonist)

- Treatment-resistant schizophrenia, Schizoaffective disorder, Suicide risk, Parkinson psychosis
- SE: Agranulocytosis (neutropenia), Seizures, Myocarditis, HTN, Constipation

### Olanzapine (Multireceptor Antagonist)

- IM for Bipolar Disorder; 2nd line Anorexia Nervosa; Safe for elderly

### Quetiapine (Fast Dissociating D2 Antagonist)

- Insomnia, Parkinson psychosis, Bipolar psychosis, Acute bipolar depression; Safe for elderly; Lowest EPS risk
- SE: Weight gain, QTc prolongation (EKG required)

### Risperidone (D2/5HT2 Antagonist)

- Long-acting injectable for noncompliant patients
- SE: Hyperprolactinemia (infertility, galactorrhea, menstrual irregularities); Worsens Lewy body dementia

### Others

- Lurasidone: Acute bipolar depression
- Iloperidone, Paliperidone: SE = Hyperprolactinemia

## Common Atypical Antipsychotic Side Effects

- Sedation, Metabolic syndrome (weight gain, diabetes, lipid elevation)
- QTc prolongation
- Agranulocytosis (severe with Clozapine)

## EPS Risk Ranking (High to Low)

- Haloperidol > Risperidone > Ziprasidone > Lurasidone > Olanzapine > Quetiapine (lowest)

# Mood Stabilizers

## Lithium

- Preferred in liver disease; Reduces suicide risk in Bipolar
- First sign of toxicity: Abdominal pain; Levels >1.5 mEq/L; Suspect in elderly with dehydration
- Monitoring: CMP, UA, EKG, Pregnancy test, Thyroid function tests
- SE (LiTHIUM): Low Thyroid (hypothyroidism), Heart (Ebstein anomaly), I (Nephrogenic DI), Unwanted Movement (tremor), Hyperparathyroidism (elevated calcium)
- Narrow therapeutic index
- Decreased clearance: AKI, ACE-I, NSAIDs, Thiazides

## Valproate

- Preferred in renal disease
- SE: Teratogen (spina bifida, neural tube defects), Hepatotoxicity, Thrombocytopenia, Pancreatitis, Agranulocytosis

## Lamotrigine

- Safer anticonvulsant; Safe in pregnancy; Longer titration (outpatient)
- SE: Stevens-Johnson Syndrome

## Carbamazepine

- Uses: Trigeminal neuralgia, Absence seizures
- SE: Agranulocytosis, Aplastic anemia, Hyponatremia (SIADH), CYP inducer and self-inducer

## Oxcarbazepine

- Safe in pregnancy; SE: SIADH

# Anxiolytics

## Buspirone (5-HT1A Agonist)

- Use: GAD
- SE: No addiction, sedation, tolerance, or alcohol interaction

# Barbiturates (GABA-A, Cl Channel Duration)

- Phenobarbital, Pentobarbital, Thiopental, Secobarbital

# Benzodiazepines (GABA-A, Cl Channel Frequency)

- Overdose antidote: Flumazenil
- Formulations
  - Liver disease: Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam
  - IV: Diazepam, Lorazepam
  - Shortest half-life: Alprazolam
  - Long half-life (liver toxicity risk): Chlordiazepoxide, Diazepam
- Uses: Acute agitation (Lorazepam), Anxiety, Panic disorder (acute), Specific phobia, Catatonia
- Elderly adverse effects: Confusion, falls, Paradoxical agitation (confusion, aggression, disinhibition within 1 hour)

# CNS Stimulants (Increase DA/NE)

- Methylphenidate (Ritalin), Lisdexamfetamine, Dextroamphetamine
- Uses: ADHD, Binge Eating Disorder
- SE: Weight loss, Bruxism, Agitation, Tics, Insomnia

# Non-Stimulants for ADHD

- Atomoxetine: Adult-onset ADHD
- Clonidine (alpha-2 agonist): Hyperactive-dominant ADHD, Opioid withdrawal
- Guanfacine (alpha-2A agonist): Hyperactive-dominant ADHD
- SE: HTN

# Psychiatric Emergencies

## Extrapyramidal Side Effects ("Kinesias")

1. Hours-Days: Acute Dystonia (sudden onset muscle stiffness, oculogyric crisis, laryngospasm)
   - Treatment: Benztropine (anticholinergic) or Diphenhydramine
2. Days-Months: Akathisia (restlessness, need to move; strongest with Haloperidol, Risperidone)
   - Treatment: Beta-blocker (Propranolol), Benztropine, Benzo (last resort)
   - Decrease antipsychotic dose; discontinue if no clinical benefit
3. Parkinsonism: Treatment = Benztropine, Amantadine
4. Months-Years: Tardive Dyskinesia (choreoathetoid movements, mouth/tongue — IRREVERSIBLE)
   - Treatment: Valbenazine, Deutetrabenazine (VMAT inhibitors)

## Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (Genetic Predisposition)

- Due to: High-potency antipsychotics, Antiemetics (Promethazine), Withdrawal of Parkinson medications
- Pathophysiology: Central DA receptor blockade -> Hyperthermia/Dysautonomia; Nigrostriatal disruption -> Rigidity
- Malignant FEVER: Myoglobinuria, Fever, Encephalopathy, Unstable Vitals, Enzymes (elevated CPK), Rigidity (lead-pipe)
- Treatment: Discontinue antipsychotics (or restart DA agents), Benzodiazepines, Dantrolene (ryanodine receptor antagonist), Bromocriptine (DA agonist)

## Lithium Toxicity

- Decreased clearance: AKI, ACE-I, NSAIDs, Thiazides
- Clinical features: Nephrogenic DI, Hyperreflexia, Ataxia, Slurred speech
- Treatment: Discontinue lithium, Hydrate with isotonic NaCl, possible hemodialysis

## Serotonin Syndrome

- Triggers: SSRI, SNRI, MAOI, TCA, Buspirone, Trazodone, Tramadol, Ondansetron, Triptans, Linezolid, MDMA, Dextromethorphan, Meperidine, St. John's Wort
  - Discontinue antidepressants 2 weeks before beginning MAOI
- Clinical features
  - Neuromuscular hyperactivity: Clonus/Hypertonicity, Hyperreflexia, Tremor, Seizures (vs NMS = hyporeflexia)
  - Autonomic instability: Diaphoresis, Diarrhea, Hyperthermia, Rhabdomyolysis
  - GI: Diarrhea, Hyperactive bowel sounds
  - Altered mental status, Renal failure, Death
- Treatment: Discontinue all serotonergic medications, Supportive care, Benzodiazepines, Cyproheptadine (5-HT2 antagonist) if supportive measures fail, Intubation if temp >106F

## Hypertensive Crisis

- Tyramine-rich food or sympathomimetics + MAOIs -> increased tyramine -> displaces NE -> increased sympathetic stimulation
- Clinical features: Hypertension, Headache -> intracranial bleeding, stroke, death
- Treatment: Phentolamine (reversible alpha-blockade)

## Anticholinergic Toxicity

- Etiology: Competitive inhibition of muscarinic ACh receptors
- Risk drugs: Antihistamines (Diphenhydramine), Psychotropics (Haloperidol, Clozapine, TCA), Antiparkinson (Benztropine), Antimuscarinics (Oxybutynin), Mydriatics (Atropine), Plant-based (Jimson weed, Nightshade)
- Clinical features: Hallucinations, Mydriasis, Hyperthermia, Tachycardia, Dry skin/mucous membranes, Ileus, Urinary retention
- Workup: EKG for QRS/QTc prolongation
- Dx: Clinical (no confirmatory labs)
- Treatment: Supportive + Activated charcoal, Benzo for agitation/seizures, Physostigmine for severe central/peripheral symptoms

# QT Prolongation

- QT interval >450 msec
- Pathophysiology: Medications block outward delayed rectifier potassium current; Electrolyte derangements (common in alcohol use disorder)
- Complication: Delayed ventricular repolarization -> Torsades de pointes -> polymorphic VT -> cardiac arrest
- Prevention: ECG testing prior to drug administration in at-risk patients; withhold if QTc >450 msec
- High-risk drugs: Haloperidol, Ziprasidone, Citalopram

# Opioid Detox Summary

1. Methadone: Long half-life, Heroin detox, Long-term MAINTENANCE
2. Buprenorphine: Prevent relapse; precipitates withdrawal with full agonist (higher affinity binding)
3. Naloxone: Mu-R, Acute OD, Reverse CNS/respiratory depression (GIVE NOW)
4. Naltrexone: Alcohol use disorder/lower cravings, Post-detox relapse prevention

# Miscellaneous

## Primary Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison Disease)

- Significant salt craving and hyperpigmentation (vs secondary AI)
- Pathophysiology: Autoimmune destruction of bilateral adrenal cortex
- Clinical features
  - Mineralocorticoid deficiency: Salt wasting, hypotension, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, weight loss
  - Glucocorticoid deficiency: Fatigue, anorexia, psychiatric symptoms (irritability, depressed mood)
  - Androgen deficiency: Loss of libido, reduced pubic hair (more apparent in women)
- Dx: Cosyntropin stimulation testing (synthetic ACTH -> low cortisol = diagnostic)

## Cushing Syndrome

- Clinical features: Central obesity, Skin atrophy with wide purplish striae, Proximal muscle weakness, HTN, Glucose intolerance, Hyperpigmentation (if ACTH excess), Depression/anxiety
- Dx: 24-hour urinary cortisol, Late-night salivary cortisol, Low-dose dexamethasone suppression test

## Dhat Syndrome

- Culture-bound syndrome in South Asian cultures with somatic complaints related to loss of semen
- Management: Patient-centered approach with open-ended questions

## Gastroparesis

- RF: DM (autonomic neuropathy), Opioids, Anticholinergics, Vagus nerve injury, Neurological conditions, Idiopathic/postviral
- Clinical features: Postprandial vomiting, Early satiety, Weight loss, Abdominal pain, Food aversion
- Dx: Gastric emptying scan + Upper endoscopy +/- CT/MR enterography; Nuclear gastric-emptying study
- Treatment: Frequent small meals, Promotility drugs (Metoclopramide, Erythromycin), Gastric electrical stimulation

## Erectile Dysfunction

- Psychogenic: Normal nonsexual nocturnal erections, Sudden onset, Situational
- Organic: Vascular, Neurologic, Endocrine, Hypogonadism

## Premature Ejaculation

- Decreased ejaculatory latency (<1 min), Uncontrolled orgasm, Distress
- Treatment: SSRIs (Paroxetine prolongs ejaculatory latency), Topical lidocaine, Psychotherapy/couples therapy

## Hyperventilation Syndrome

- Subjective SOB with good oxygenation, clear lungs, deep breathing with audible sighs
- Neurological symptoms from cerebral vasoconstriction or alkalosis-induced hypocalcemia/hypophosphatemia
- Clinical features: Paresthesias, Headache, Lightheadedness, Carpopedal spasms
- Treatment: Reassurance and breathing retraining (abdominal/diaphragmatic breathing); Paper bag breathing NO LONGER recommended (risk of hypoxia)
