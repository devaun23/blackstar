# Substance Abuse Disorders

## Substance Abuse Disorder Criteria

- 2 of the following for 1 year: Cannot cutdown, Craving, Too much (increased amount), Time-consuming, Neglecting responsibilities, Relationship damage, Reduced activities, Physiological dangers ignored, Physical dangers ignored, Tolerance, Withdrawal

## Motivational Interviewing

- Engaging, Focusing, Evoking (assign numerical value to readiness; explore personal motivations), Planning

## Urine Drug Screen

- Confirmatory testing (gas chromatography/mass spectroscopy) required for in-office UDS (high false-positive/negative rate)
- Controlled substance refills: Instruct patient that additional urine study needed before refill

## Thiamine Deficiency

### Korsakoff Syndrome

- Pathophysiology: Lesions in anterior/medial thalami and corpus callosum
- Clinical features: Anterograde and retrograde amnesia, Intact long-term memory, Confabulation, Apathy, Lack of insight, History of alcohol use disorder
- Treatment: Recovery rare with thiamine (vs significant recovery in Wernicke encephalopathy)

### Wernicke Encephalopathy

- Pathophysiology: Mammillary body atrophy and dorsomedial thalamic neuron loss
- RF: Alcohol use disorder, Anorexia, Hyperemesis gravidarum
- Classic Triad: Ataxia, Encephalopathy, Oculomotor dysfunction
- Complication: Korsakoff Syndrome (may occur without prior WE episodes)

# Depressants

## Alcohol

- Intoxication: Elevated GGT, AST 2x ALT; GABA-A potentiation -> sedation; low NMDA
- Alcohol Use Disorder
  - AST:ALT ratio 2:1
  - Clinical features: Macrocytosis, Tolerance, Tremor, GERD/HTN history, Sleep disturbance/anxiety from mild withdrawal
  - Management
    1. Acamprosate (glutamate modulator) — CI: renal disease
    2. Naltrexone (mu-opioid antagonist) — decreases craving, can initiate while still drinking; CI: patients taking opioids
    3. Disulfiram (aldehyde dehydrogenase inhibitor) — for highly motivated abstinent patients

### Alcohol Withdrawal Syndrome

- Low GABA -> unopposed excitation; High NMDA -> Delirium Tremens/excitotoxic neuron death
- Timeline
  - 6-24 hrs: Mild — Intact orientation, Autonomic hyperactivity, CNS hyperactivity (anxiety, insomnia, tremulousness, tachycardia, HTN)
  - 12-48 hrs: Seizures (single or multiple generalized tonic-clonic)
  - 12-48 hrs: Alcoholic hallucinosis (intact orientation, visual/auditory/tactile hallucinations, stable vitals)
  - 48-96 hrs: Delirium tremens (agitation, confusion, fever, tachycardia, HTN, diaphoresis, hallucinations)
- Management
  - Liver disease: Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam
  - IV formulation: Diazepam, Lorazepam
  - Long half-life (risk in liver disease): Chlordiazepoxide, Diazepam
  - Benzos improve agitation and prevent progression to seizures/delirium

### Marchiafava-Bignami Disease

- Severe corpus callosum and white matter damage in chronic alcohol use with malnutrition
- Clinical features: Dementia, Motor dysfunction, Dysarthria

## Toxic Alcohols

- Methanol: Optic neuritis due to formic acid -> Optic disc hyperemia -> Blurred vision
- Ethylene glycol: Kidney dysfunction due to calcium oxalate

## Barbiturates

- Intoxication: Marked respiratory depression (Tx: symptom management)
- Withdrawal: Delirium, Cardiovascular collapse

## Benzodiazepines

- Sudden withdrawal -> Excitatory state: Autonomic hyperactivity, Delirium, Insomnia, Increased anxiety, Psychosis, Tremors, Seizures (especially short-acting Alprazolam)
- Treatment: Reinitiate long-acting benzo (Diazepam) and taper over months

## Opioids

### Opioid Overdose

- Clinical features: Miosis (<2mm), Respiratory depression, Slurred speech, Sedation/unconsciousness, Decreased gag reflex, Hypotension, Hypothermia, Bradycardia
- Treatment: Naloxone (Narcan) — short-acting antagonist
  - Offered to every patient on chronic opioid therapy or at high risk for OD
  - High risk: Escalating/high dose, Concurrent benzo use, History of substance use disorder, Close contacts (children)

### Opioid Use Disorder

- Maintenance treatment (MAT): Steady opioid receptor activation prevents withdrawal, diminishes craving, reduces euphoric effects
  - Buprenorphine (partial agonist): Lower respiratory depression risk, lower misuse risk, no daily clinic dosing required
  - Methadone (full agonist): Greater risks (respiratory depression, lethal OD, misuse, daily dosing); SE: Hyperalgesia, Hypogonadism, QTc prolongation
  - Methadone overdose: Long half-life >24 hours -> admitted for prolonged observation (1-2 days)
  - Naltrexone (antagonist): Post-detox relapse prevention
- Acute pain in OUD patients: IV short-acting opioids (Morphine)

### Opioid Withdrawal (4-48 hours, lasts 3-5 days)

- Pathophysiology: Noradrenergic hyperactivity in locus coeruleus (abrupt cessation removes inhibitory effect -> sudden NE increase)
- Clinical features: GI (nausea, vomiting, cramping, diarrhea), Cardiac (increased HR/BP, diaphoresis), Psychological (insomnia, yawning, dysphoria), Other (arthralgias, myalgias, mydriasis, lacrimation, rhinorrhea, piloerection — "flu-like")
- Management: Buprenorphine or Methadone; Clonidine (alpha-2 agonist, decreases autonomic symptoms); AVOID Naloxone (precipitates rapid withdrawal)

### Opioid Prescribing Best Practices

- Prior to initiation: Screen for RF, UDS, Query PDMP, Sign controlled substance agreement
- During therapy: Follow-up q1-3 months, Query PDMP before each refill, RANDOM urine drug screens
- End-of-life care: Morphine dose may be increased despite respiratory depression (Doctrine of double effect)

## Inhalants

- Intoxication: Disinhibition, Slurred speech, Disturbed gait, Disorientation, Drowsiness
- Withdrawal: Dysphoria, Irritability, Sleep disturbance (Tx: Benzo, Antipsychotics)

# Stimulants

## General Stimulant Intoxication

- Acute-onset agitation, paranoia, adrenergic excess (diaphoresis, mydriasis, HTN, tachycardia)
- Pathophysiology: Block monoamine transporters -> DA/NE buildup -> sympathomimetic toxidrome
- Management: Benzodiazepines (enhance GABA-A inhibition); Antipsychotics NOT recommended 1st line (QT prolongation, seizure threshold, heat dissipation)

## Amphetamines

- Intoxication: Indirect adrenergic stimulation (tachycardia, HTN, hyperthermia, diaphoresis, mydriasis), confusion, paranoia, agitation, insomnia; returns to baseline after hours of abstinence
- Treatment: Lorazepam (blunts CNS catecholamine release)

## Methamphetamine Use Disorder

- Clinical features: Skin excoriations (picking), Poor dentition/bruxism, Aggressive behavior, Severe insomnia, Psychosis (delusions, paranoia, tactile hallucinations — "bugs under skin"), Sympathetic hyperactivity
- Note: Visual and tactile hallucinations more common in substance-induced psychotic disorders
- Treatment: CBT, Antipsychotics

## Synthetic Cathinones ("Bath Salts")

- Amphetamine-like with prolonged effect (up to 1 week)
- Clinical features: Severe agitation, Combativeness, Psychosis, Delirium, Seizures, Elevated BP/HR
- NOT detected in routine UDS

## Cocaine

- Intoxication: Sympathetic stimulation + "manic" symptoms (elevated mood, hyperactivity, grandiosity, pressured speech) + Psychotic symptoms (paranoia, hallucinations) + Sudden cardiac death; Chronic: Perforated nasal septum
- Withdrawal ("crash"): Severe depression, suicidal ideation, increased dreaming, hypersomnia, intense craving

## Nicotine / Caffeine

- Lack substance abuse disorder classification
- Caffeine withdrawal: Headache, difficulty concentrating, flu-like symptoms

# Hallucinogens

## LSD

- Perceptual distortions, Pupillary dilation, Depersonalization, Paranoia

## Marijuana

- Clinical features: Conjunctival injection, Slowed time, Munchies, Dry mouth, Tachycardia
- Higher doses: Anxiety, Social withdrawal, Dysphoria, Paranoia
- Metabolized in liver; stored in lipophilic tissue (detectable up to 30 days)

## MDMA ("Ecstasy")

- Intoxication: Disinhibition, Bruxism, Thirst, Distorted sensory-time perception
- Withdrawal: Changed appetite, Depression, Anxiety, Difficulty concentrating

## Phencyclidine (PCP — "Pain Proof")

- NMDA antagonist; inhibits reuptake of 5HT, DA, NE
- Clinical features: Severe agitation, Delusions of enhanced strength, Psychosis, Analgesia, HTN, Tachycardia, Disorientation, Prominent horizontal-vertical nystagmus
- Detected in UDS
- Complication: Trauma from being restrained
- Treatment: Benzodiazepines (Lorazepam, Diazepam)

## Miscellaneous

- Anabolic steroids: SE = Acne, Baldness, Gynecomastia, Hepatic dysfunction, Altered lipids, Mood/behavior changes
- Toluene (glue sniffing): Encephalopathy, Abdominal distress, Type 1 RTA (nonanion gap metabolic acidosis)

# Delirium

- Delirium Tremens: Alcohol disorder, autonomic instability, tactile hallucinations

# Dissociative Disorders

## Depersonalization/Derealization Disorder

- Persistent/recurrent episodes of depersonalization and/or derealization
- Intact reality testing

## Dissociative Amnesia

- Inability to recall important personal information (usually traumatic/stressful)
- Not explained by another disorder
- Subtype: Dissociative fugue (amnesia associated with traveling/wandering)

## Dissociative Identity Disorder

- Only disorder requiring presence of multiple personality states
- Marked discontinuity in identity with fragmentation into >=2 distinct personality states
- Transitions sudden, usually precipitated by stress
- Chronic auditory hallucinations from INSIDE the head (vs psychotic = OUTSIDE)
- Associated with severe trauma and abuse
- Treatment: Long-term trauma-focused psychotherapy

# Psychotic Disorders

## Psychosis — Definition

- 1 from positive symptoms (#1-3), 2 of 5 total
- Positive: Hallucinations (auditory, visual, tactile, olfactory), Delusions (Paranoid, Erotomanic, Grandiose, Cotard, Capgras, Folie a deux), Disorganized thought process, Disorganized behavior
- Negative: Anhedonia, Alogia, Avolition, Asociality, Blunted affect
- Illusion = alteration of something present; Delusion = false fixed belief despite evidence

## Dopaminergic Pathways

1. Mesocortical: Affects negative symptoms
2. Mesolimbic: Affects positive symptoms (increased DA = euphoria, delusions, hallucinations)
3. Nigrostriatal: Movement coordination (decreased DA = EPS and parkinsonism; increased DA = chorea/tics)
4. Tuberoinfundibular: Normal DA inhibits prolactin; decreased DA = hyperprolactinemia (worse with Risperidone)

## Delusional Disorder

- >=1 month of specific, well-formed delusions
- Only delusions present; function normally; no eccentric behavior/unusual speech/social deficits
- Subtypes: Persecutory, Erotomanic, Grandiose, Jealous, Somatic
- Treatment: Antipsychotics, CBT
- Vs Paranoid PD: Lifelong patterns beginning in early adulthood

## Brief Psychotic Disorder

- Duration: 1 day to 1 month
- Triggered by traumatic/stressful event
- Disorganized speech, Bizarre behavior, Hallucinations

## Schizophreniform Disorder

- Duration: 1-6 months

## Schizophrenia

- Duration: >6 months
- Pathophysiology: Smaller prefrontal cortex/amygdala/hippocampus, enlarged ventricles; abnormal neuronal connectivity; altered dopaminergic/glutamatergic neurotransmission
- Early onset (<18): More severe course; prodromal phase (social withdrawal, academic decline)
- Dx: >=2 symptoms (at least 1 from #1-3): Hallucinations, Delusions, Disorganized speech, Disorganized/catatonic behavior, Negative symptoms + continuous impairment >=6 months + significant functional decline
- Worst prognosis: Male, early onset, slow progression
- Treatment
  - Involuntary hospitalization if danger to self/others or grave disability
  - 1st gen antipsychotics if failed newer medications: Haloperidol, Trifluoperazine, Fluphenazine (SE: EPS, tardive dyskinesia, NMS, increased prolactin)
  - 2nd gen antipsychotics: Risperidone (long-acting injectable for noncompliance; SE: hyperprolactinemia), Clozapine (treatment-resistant; SE: agranulocytosis), Olanzapine, Quetiapine (lowest EPS risk), Aripiprazole (partial DA agonist)
    - Common SE: Dyslipidemia, Hyperglycemia, Weight gain; monitor fasting glucose/lipids, BP, BMI, waist circumference
  - Adjunctive psychosocial interventions (social skills, CBT, family intervention)
  - Treatment goal = Recovery (symptom remission >2 years, satisfactory vocational functioning, independent living, peer relationships)
  - Noncompliance: Long-acting injectable antipsychotic (Risperidone)
- Suicide risk: More common in young males, preserved insight, high IQ, command hallucinations; 2/3 completed suicides within 6 years of onset; highest risk 1 year after 1st hospitalization
- Co-occurring substance use: 50% of newly diagnosed have alcohol or drug use disorder

## Schizoaffective Disorder

- Duration: >2 weeks
- Major mood disorder present for majority of illness duration
- Psychosis alone for 2 weeks (lifetime history)

## Dementia with Lewy Bodies

- Dementia with >=2 of: Visual hallucinations, Parkinsonism, Fluctuating cognition, REM sleep behavior disorder
- ANTIPSYCHOTICS WORSEN symptoms (worsening confusion, parkinsonism, autonomic dysfunction) — strongest drugs: Risperidone, Haloperidol
- Early deficits: Executive functioning (finances, shopping), Visuospatial (getting lost)
- Depression common
- Dopamine antagonist sensitivity: Repeated falls, syncope, autonomic dysfunction

## Parkinson Disease Psychosis

- May develop from disease, treatment, or both
- Dopamine agonists (Pramipexole, Ropinirole) = greatest psychosis risk
- Management: Dose reduction of anti-Parkinson meds; if fails -> low-potency antipsychotic (Quetiapine, Clozapine, Pimavanserin)

## Anti-NMDA Receptor Encephalitis

- Autoimmune encephalitis; median onset age 21; >50% have associated ovarian teratoma
- Clinical features: Psychiatric (anxiety, psychosis, insomnia), Autonomic instability, Cognitive impairment, Rigidity, Hyperreflexia, Dystonia, Focal seizure post flu-like prodrome
- Dx: CSF antibodies to GluN1 subunit of NMDAR
- Treatment: Prompt tumor removal, Immunosuppressive treatment

## Acute Agitation

- Treatment: Benzodiazepine (Lorazepam) + Haloperidol for rapid sedation
- Medication administered prior to drug abuse workup if combative/agitated
