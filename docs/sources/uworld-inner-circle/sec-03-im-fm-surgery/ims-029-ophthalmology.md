# ANATOMY

## Eye Chambers
Anterior segment contains 2 chambers (anterior + posterior) → aqueous humor
Iris demarcates anterior chamber from posterior chamber
Posterior segment of eye → vitreous humor

## Pupillary Pathways — Miosis and Mydriasis
Miosis (parasympathetic): Edinger-Westphal nucleus → ciliary ganglion via CN III → short ciliary nerves → sphincter pupillae
Mydriasis (sympathetic): hypothalamus → ciliospinal center of Budge (C8-T2) → superior cervical ganglion → long ciliary nerve → pupillary dilator
Superior tarsal muscles (sympathetic, superior cervical ganglion) → open eyes wide

## Pupillary Light Reflex
Brainstem reflex: light in retina → CN II → pretectal nuclei in midbrain → bilateral Edinger-Westphal nuclei → bilateral pupillary constriction
Illumination of 1 eye → bilateral pupillary constriction (direct + consensual)

## Corneal Reflex
Brainstem reflex: contraction of orbicularis oculi in response to corneal stimulation
Afferent limb: CN V1 (ophthalmic division)
Efferent limb: CN VII

## Eyelid
Elevation by levator palpebrae superioris (oculomotor nerve, CN III)

# OCULAR PATHOLOGIES

## Blepharospasm
Form of local dystonia — recurrent forceful contraction of eyelid muscles
Etiology: Parkinson disease, antipsychotics, or idiopathic; affected by bright lights
Generally bilateral and symmetric
Meige syndrome → blepharospasm + spasm of lower face (jaw/tongue)
Tx: sensory trick (brush skin around eyes), trigger avoidance (dark glasses), severe cases → botulinum toxin

## Cavernous Sinus Thrombosis
Infection spreads from facial skin via valveless facial/ophthalmic venous system
CN III, CN IV, CN V1, CN V2, CN VI involvement → difficulty with extraocular movements
Low grade fever + periorbital edema (days later, impaired venous flow)
Severe headache + vomiting (intracranial HTN) + papilledema
Dx: MR venography
Tx: broad-spectrum antibiotics; prevention/reversal of cerebral herniation

## Horner Syndrome (Oculosympathetic Pathway)
1st neuron: hypothalamus down lateral brainstem (Wallenberg syndrome → Horner may be present)
2nd neuron: preganglionic sympathetic in lateral horn of spinal cord
3rd neuron: superior cervical ganglion → smooth muscles of eyelid, pupillary dilators, sweat glands of forehead/face
Causes → Pancoast tumor, Brown-Sequard syndrome, lateral brainstem lesion, PICA or AICA stroke
CF: ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis
Cluster headaches → pseudo-Horner (ptosis, miosis, NO anhidrosis)

# CRANIAL NERVE LESIONS

## CN II — Visual Field Defects
Occlusion of posterior ciliary artery (supplies choroid + optic nerve head) → afferent pupillary defect

## Macular Sparing
Posterior cerebral artery problems → macular sparing visual defects
Macula receives dual blood supply: PCA + collateral from MCA

## Optic Chiasm Compression → Bitemporal Hemianopsia
Sellar masses: pituitary adenoma (prolactinoma, GH-secreting), craniopharyngioma (Rathke pouch derivative), anterior communicating artery aneurysm
CF: "tunnel vision" — bitemporal heteronymous hemianopsia

## CN III Lesion
Eyes look "down and out" — medial rectus, inferior rectus, levator palpebrae superioris, inferior oblique all non-functional
CN III travels close to uncus of temporal lobe (between superior cerebellar and posterior cerebral arteries)
Aneurysm of superior cerebellar or posterior cerebral artery → CN III defect
Uncal herniation → blown pupil + "down and out" eye

## Medial Longitudinal Fasciculus (MLF)
Affected MLF is ipsilateral to CN III
Lesion → impaired conjugate horizontal gaze; affected eye cannot adduct, contralateral eye abducts with nystagmus (internuclear ophthalmoplegia)
How to differentiate MLF vs CN III: test accommodation — MLF lesion has normal accommodation; CN III lesion does not

## Accommodation
1st phase: ciliary muscles make lens rounder/thicker (increase refractive index) — controlled by Edinger-Westphal nucleus
2nd phase: make image focus on same retinal location in each eye simultaneously
3rd phase: increase depth of field (pupillary sphincters make pupils smaller)

## CN IV — Trochlear Nerve
Crosses in back of brainstem → left CN IV lesion causes RIGHT eye issue
SO4: superior oblique muscle depresses and intorts the eye
Lesion → eyeball stays UP when attempting to look downward → vertical diplopia
Difficulty reading books or going down stairs
Compensation: tilt head AWAY from lesion, pull chin TOWARD the lesion

## CN V — Trigeminal Nerve
CN V1 (ophthalmic division) → afferent limb of corneal/blink reflex
Check blink reflex before determining brain death (brainstem reflex)

## CN VI — Abducens Nerve
Runs in CENTER of cavernous sinus → lesioned in high ICP, cavernous sinus infection/thrombosis
RF: hypercoagulable disorders (Factor V Leiden)
Controls lateral rectus (abduct the eye)
Runs in middle of pons → medial pontine lesion causes CN VI + UMN lesion
Paramedian pontine artery infarct → ipsilateral CN VI + contralateral UMN (hyperreflexia, positive Babinski)

## CN VII — Bell Palsy
Peripheral CN VII palsy — reactivation of HSV → inflammation, edema, nerve compression, myelin degeneration
Unilateral mouth droop + disappearance of nasolabial fold
Ipsilateral decreased lacrimation, hyperacusis, decreased taste anterior 2/3 tongue
Upper face involvement (weakness closing eye/raising eyebrow) → distinguishes from UMN stroke (which spares upper face)
Tx: glucocorticoids + valacyclovir

## Wernicke Encephalopathy
Ophthalmoplegia (horizontal nystagmus + bilateral CN VI palsy) + ataxia + confusion
Sx mimic alcohol intoxication but alcohol levels low/undetectable
Causes: chronic malnutrition — alcohol, systemic malignancy, hyperemesis of pregnancy
Tx: thiamine

# GENETIC EYE DISEASES

## Lens Dislocation
Marfan syndrome → lens dislocation UP and OUT (AD, fibrillin defect)
Homocystinuria → lens dislocation DOWN and IN (AR, cystathionine-beta-synthase deficiency → high homocysteine)
Homocystinuria CF: intellectual disability, marfanoid habitus, hypercoagulability → early MI (sulfa groups damage vessel walls)
Homocystinuria → improvement with B6 (pyridoxine) supplements

# PEDIATRIC OPHTHALMOLOGY

## Amblyopia
Functional reduction in visual acuity due to visual disturbance in early childhood (<5 yo, vision still developing)
Unilateral: ≥2 line difference between eyes; Bilateral: vision worse than 20/40 in age ≥4
Etiology: strabismus (asymmetric red eye reflex, asymmetric corneal reflex), asymmetric refractive error (normal reflexes), vision deprivation (cataracts → dull red reflex, ptosis)
Detection delayed until affected eye is occluded (children's pirate eye patch)
Tx: corrective lenses; patching eye with better vision; atropine drops to blur normal eye; surgery for cataract removal

## Strabismus
Ocular malalignment → esotropia, asymmetric red eye reflex, asymmetric corneal reflex
Dx: cover test — pt focuses on object, one eye covered, misaligned eye shifts to fixate
Rule out malignancy with dilated funduscopic exam
Complication: untreated strabismus → amblyopia (decreased VA of misaligned eye)

# INFLAMMATORY EYE CONDITIONS

## Blepharitis
Inflammation at eyelid margin, prominent at meibomian gland opening
Assoc: seborrheic dermatitis, rosacea, allergic disorders, Staph, HSV, Demodex mite
Burning/itching of lids + discharge + eyelash crusting in morning + foreign body sensation
Tx: warm compresses, gentle scrubs, lid massage

## Acute Dacryocystitis
Nasolacrimal duct obstruction → stasis of tears → infection
Painful swelling and redness around medial canthal region + discharge
Tx: Staph coverage + ophthalmology consultation

## Chalazion
Granulomatous reaction to obstructed meibomian gland → solitary rubbery nodule deep to eyelid margin

## Hordeolum (Stye)
MCC: Staph aureus; inflammation of eyelash follicles or tear gland
Tender erythematous nodule at eyelid margin
Tx: warm compress; incision + curettage for persistent hordeolum with large chalazion

## Conjunctivitis

### Bacterial Conjunctivitis
Conjunctival injection + purulent thick discharge (reappears after wiping)
Tx: erythromycin ophthalmic ointment

### Allergic Conjunctivitis
Bilateral; seasonal allergies/atopic disorders
Watery scant stringy discharge + ocular itching + foreign body sensation
Tx: topical antihistamines, vasoconstrictors, mast cell stabilizers, artificial tears

### Viral Conjunctivitis ("Pink Eye")
MCC: adenovirus; sometimes prodromal URI/rhinorrhea/gastroenteritis
Watery scant discharge + burning, gritty sensation; late summer/fall

### Kawasaki Conjunctivitis
Bilateral conjunctivitis + fever + LAD + mucocutaneous inflammation
Tx: IVIG + aspirin; complication: myocarditis

### Atopic Keratoconjunctivitis
Severe ocular allergy — itching, tearing, thick mucus discharge, photophobia, blurred vision
More prolonged course than allergic conjunctivitis; thickening of eyelids and surrounding skin

## Trachoma — MCC Infectious Blindness Worldwide
Chlamydia trachomatis serotypes A, B, C
Initial: follicular conjunctivitis ("pale follicles and inflamed tarsals")
Chronic: eyelid scarring + eyelash inversion (trichiasis) → inverted eyelashes rub eye → blindness
Tx: oral azithromycin (simultaneously to entire population in crowded places, refugee camps)

## Keratitis

### Bacterial Keratitis
Improper contact lens use or corneal trauma → mucopurulent drainage + hazy cornea with round white ulceration

### Viral Keratitis (HSV, VZV)
Frequent cause of corneal blindness
Pain, photophobia, tearing, blurred vision
Conjunctival erythema around corneal border or limbus → "ciliary flush"
Dendritic ulcers + corneal vesicles
Dx: slit-lamp with fluorescein → dendritic (branching) ulcers; viral culture or PCR
Tx: oral/topical ganciclovir or trifluridine; oral suppressive therapy for recurrent cases
Topical glucocorticoids AVOIDED → risk of vision loss

## Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus
Elderly + fever, malaise, periorbital pruritus + neuropathic pain (burning, stabbing)
Vesicular rash around eye in CN V1 distribution
Involvement of side or tip of nose → highly correlates with ocular involvement
May lead to acute keratitis, corneal ulceration, vision loss
Tx: systemic antiviral — acyclovir, famciclovir, valacyclovir

## Corneal Abrasion
Injury to corneal surface epithelium → pain + conjunctival injection + tearing + foreign body sensation
Vision NORMAL if abrasion is small
Dx: fluorescein + backlight → linear lesion
Localized uptake → corneal abrasion; uptake followed by waterfall clearing → full-thickness laceration (open globe)

## Uveitis

### Anterior Uveitis
Inflammation of anterior uveal tract (especially iris)
Assoc: spondyloarthritis, sarcoidosis, IBD
Significant pain + red eye + blurry vision + painful eye movements
Slit lamp: cells and flare
Hypopyon → layering of pus in anterior chamber

### Panuveitis
Simultaneous inflammation of anterior (iris, ciliary body) and posterior uvea (choroid)
Photophobia, tearing, perilimbal injection ± hypopyon

## Episcleritis
Inflammation of episclera without uveal tract involvement
Mild-moderate discomfort, photophobia, watery discharge
Assoc: RA, IBD, vasculitis

## Endophthalmitis
Invasive infection of globe (bacterial or Candida)
Disruption of external eye surface via trauma
Conjunctival irritation + purulent haziness + hypopyon
Tx: IV antibiotics ± surgery

## Preseptal vs Orbital Cellulitis
Preseptal (anterior to orbital septum): unilateral eyelid erythema/swelling, chemosis, fever, leukocytosis; recent insect bite or skin break; Tx: oral doxycycline
Orbital (posterior to septum): preseptal features PLUS pain with EOM, proptosis, ± ophthalmoplegia with diplopia; Tx: IV antibiotics ± surgery

## Subconjunctival Hemorrhage
Benign painless red eye — small conjunctival vessel rupture → blood between sclera and conjunctiva
Normal exam; Tx: reassurance

# VISION CHANGES AND LOSS

## Amaurosis Fugax
Rapid transient monocular vision loss — "curtain descending over visual field" (resolves within minutes)
MCC: retinal ischemia from atherosclerotic emboli from ipsilateral carotid artery
Also: cardioembolic (atrial thrombus from AFib)
Dx: neck duplex ultrasound + echocardiogram

## Central Retinal Artery Occlusion
Painless monocular vision loss if completely occluded

## Retinal Vein Occlusion
RF: venous thrombosis, HTN, DM, hyperviscosity (leukemia, myeloma)
CF: floaters + blurry/distorted vision

## Giant Cell Arteritis
Temporal artery inflammation (ECA branch) → anterior ischemic optic neuropathy
Unilateral headache + jaw claudication + fever + fatigue
Polymyalgia rheumatica (proximal muscle stiffness)
CNS: TIA/stroke, vertigo, hearing loss
Fundoscopy: pale optic disc edema
Tx: PMR only → low-dose glucocorticoids; GCA → intermediate-to-high dose; GCA with vision loss → high-dose IV glucocorticoids

## Retinal Vasospasm
Transient monocular vision loss; increased stroke risk if concomitant carotid artery disease

## Afferent Pupillary Defect (Marcus Gunn Pupil)
Light to affected eye → bilateral pupils cannot constrict (damaged optic nerve cannot transmit signal)
Light to unaffected eye → bilateral constriction (consensual reflex works)
Light back to affected eye → pupils dilate (return to baseline)

## Optic Neuritis
Inflammatory demyelinating lesion — associated with MS
Acute vision loss (central) + afferent pupillary defect with disc swelling + pain on eye movement
Dx: visual evoked potential testing
Methanol toxicity → formic acid → optic disc hyperemia + blurred vision

## Optic Nerve Injury
Trauma-related (direct penetrating or indirect shearing from facial trauma)
Decreased color vision + afferent pupillary defect
Dx: CT orbit; urgent ophthalmic referral

## Papilledema
Causes enlarged blind spot; disc normal; visual field defects (if present) are patchy

## Diabetic Retinopathy
PDR and diabetic neuropathy are chronic microvascular complications of persistent hyperglycemia
Presence of PDR correlates with diabetic kidney disease
Late findings: floaters, decreased VA, focal visual field defects, acute monocular vision loss

## Hypertensive Retinopathy
MC asymptomatic or monocular vision loss
Fundoscopy: focal spasm of arterioles, progressive sclerosis and narrowing

## Retinal Detachment
Posterior vitreous detachment → mechanical depolarization → flashes of light
Vitreous debris → "floaters" (dark spots or webs)
PVD tears retina → fluid seeps behind → retinal detachment
Begins peripherally → peripheral visual defect → eventually central → "curtain descending" (does NOT resolve in minutes, unlike amaurosis fugax)
Relative afferent pupillary defect depending on degree of detachment
Ophthal exam: gray elevated retina with wrinkled appearance

## Macular Degeneration (Age-Related)
Vision loss >50 yo; central visual field loss
Atrophic (dry): slow vision loss with scotoma (blind spot); drusen deposits visible on fundoscopy
Exudative/neovascular (wet): unilateral aggressive vision loss starting with straight line distortion
Dx: Amsler grid (straight line test requires fine VA from macula)
Tx: wet → VEGF inhibitor (bevacizumab, pazopanib, sorafenib); dry → vitamin E, vitamin A, beta carotene

# LENS AND PRESSURE DISORDERS

## Cataracts
MCC: oxidative damage of lens with aging
Progressive painless bilateral blurred vision + bothersome glare + difficulty reading
Opacification of lens + glare/halos around lights + loss of red reflex (later)
Younger patients: DM, trauma, glucocorticoid use, chronic HIV
Tx: surgical removal of lens + prosthetic lens implantation

## Glaucoma — Angle-Closure (ACG)
Narrowing/closure of anterior chamber angle → decreased aqueous outflow → elevated IOP
RF: lens located more forward against iris; pupillary dilation
Triggers: anticholinergics (tolterodine), sympathomimetics, sulfonamides (TMP-SMX → swelling of posterior chamber), low ambient light (movie theater)
Headache + ocular pain + nausea + decreased vision + "halos around lights"
Conjunctival redness + corneal opacity + fixed mid-dilated pupil → "angry eye"
Dx: tonometry + gonioscopy
Tx: pilocarpine (miosis, opens trabecular meshwork) + timolol (reduce production) + apraclonidine (reduce production, increase outflow) + acetazolamide (systemic, rapidly reduce production); definitive → laser iridotomy

## Glaucoma — Open-Angle (OAG)
Insidious loss of peripheral vision → optic nerve head atrophy + enlarged optic cup
"Optic disc rim thinning, increased cup/disc ratio"
Elevated IOP from topical/systemic glucocorticoids (decreased outflow via drainage channels) → also central blurriness from corneal edema
Dx: tonometry
Tx: topical prostaglandin (latanoprost, bimatoprost → increase drainage through uveoscleral pathway); topical BB (timolol — caution in asthma); topical CA-inhibitor (dorzolamide); laser trabeculoplasty
Atropine CONTRAINDICATED → pupillary dilation causes angle narrowing

## Hyphema
Blood in anterior chamber → MCC: blunt force trauma
Spontaneous episodes: von Willebrand disease or DM (microvascular disease)

## Hypopyon
Layering of inflammatory cells in dependent portion of anterior chamber
Perilimbal injection/ciliary flush
Conditions: anterior uveitis/iritis (inflammatory), endophthalmitis or keratitis (infectious)

## Presbyopia
Age-related loss of lens elasticity → difficulty with lens accommodation → cannot focus on near objects
No history of eye problems; Tx: reading glasses

## Myopia (Nearsightedness)
Increased axial length of eye or corneal protrusion → image focused anterior to retina
RF: positive FH, East Asian descent
Complications: retinal detachment, macular degeneration

# SPECIAL FINDINGS

## Macula Cherry Red Spots
Tay-Sachs disease → cherry red spot ONLY on macula; deficiency of hexosaminidase A; GM2 ganglioside accumulates in brain
Niemann-Pick disease → cherry red spot + hepatosplenomegaly; deficiency of sphingomyelinase; sphingomyelin accumulates in brain + liver + spleen

## Orbital Floor Fracture
Inferior rectus muscle entrapment → vertical diplopia + restricted upward eye movement

## Open Globe Laceration
Small high-velocity airborne particles → foreign body sensation, pain, excessive tearing, decreased vision
Globe deformity, extrusion of vitreous/iris, peaked/teardrop pupil (iris stretching)
Asymmetric anterior chamber depth + loss of VA or afferent pupillary response + reduced IOP
Tx: eye shield, CT eye, IV antibiotics, tetanus prophylaxis
Sympathetic ophthalmia: autoimmune — TC sensitized to previously sequestered eye antigens → decreased vision in UNINJURED eye + bilateral conjunctival erythema
Eyes and testes have immune privilege — inflammation inhibited unless self-antigens released by trauma
Tx: remove injured eye if not expected to recover → prevent bilateral blindness

## Periorbital Ecchymosis
"Raccoon eyes" → orbital/skull fracture

## Pterygium
Wedge-shaped proliferation of conjunctival tissue expanding from lateral aspects toward cornea
Cause: chronic UV light exposure

## Retinoblastoma
Malignant proliferation of retinal cells — MC ophthalmic malignancy in children <2 yo
RB1 tumor suppressor gene at G1 checkpoint
Germline (1 inherited + 1 acquired mutation) → higher risk bilateral retinoblastoma
Acquired (2 acquired mutations) → unilateral retinoblastoma
#1 presentation: leukocoria (white pupillary reflex)
#2 presentation: esotropia due to central vision loss (nasal deviation of eye)
Invasion of optic nerve → increased risk of brain metastasis
Dx: ultrasound of eye + funduscopic exam (nodular cream-colored retinal mass)
Tx: chemotherapy

## Retinitis Pigmentosa
Genetic mutation → loss of photoreceptors → progressive retinal degeneration; onset from age 10
Night blindness + progressive mid-peripheral visual field loss + decreased VA
Fundoscopic: retinal vessel attenuation, optic disc pallor, abnormal retinal pigmentation
Prognosis: legally blind by 40

# SYSTEMIC DISEASES WITH OCULAR MANIFESTATIONS

## Graves Disease
TRAB autoantibodies → thyrotoxicosis → sympathetic activation → contraction of superior tarsal muscle → lid lag and retraction
TC activation + stimulation of orbital fibroblasts by TSH autoantibodies → orbital tissue expansion → proptosis
Ocular irritation + impaired extraocular motion + proptosis

## Lambert-Eaton Syndrome (Paraneoplastic)
Diplopia + ptosis

## Myasthenia Gravis
Autoantibodies against AChR on motor end plate → fatigable muscle weakness
Fluctuating diplopia + ptosis

## Diabetic Retinopathy
Vascular proliferation ± vitreous hemorrhage
Late findings: floaters, decreased VA, focal visual field defects, acute monocular vision loss

## Hypertensive Retinopathy
Progressive arteriolar changes in retina
MC asymptomatic or monocular vision loss; symptoms due to retinal hemorrhage
