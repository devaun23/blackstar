# INFECTIOUS CAUSES

## Pneumonia

1. Common cause of mortality and morbidity in children <5yo
2. Neonatal: GBS, E. coli, Listeria, Klebsiella
3. 2yo-5yo: Viral pneumonia more common
   1. Viral causes
      1. = RSV
   2. Bacterial causes
      1. H. influenza type B: low risk if immunizations up to date
      2. S. Aureus: high risk due to beta-lactamase expression
         1. Amoxicillin alone not effective in eradicating organism, Amoxicillin-clavulanate necessary
         2. "Lobar consolidation, fixed effusion"
4. CAP: Community acquired pneumonia — Strep pneumo, Tx: 3rd gen Cephalosporin — Ceftriaxone
5. Atypical: M. pneumoniae (CXR: patchy or diffuse interstitial infiltrates)
6. Pneumonia + Diarrhea: Legionella pneumophilia
   1. Pulse temperature dissociation (fever with relative bradycardia)
7. Hypoxemia
   1. Alveoli become filled with inflammatory exudate --> marked impairment of alveolar ventilation in affected lung portion --> Hypoxemia --> Right-to-Left intrapulmonary shunting, an extreme form of V/Q mismatch
   2. Perfusion of lung tissue in the absence of ventilation
   3. Depending on extent of lung tissue damage, hypoxemia may or may not correct with increase in FiO2
   4. P jiroveci particularly causes severe hypoxemia that is difficult to correct with supplemental oxygen

## Aspiration Pneumonia

### Bacterial Aspiration Pneumonia

1. Common cause of hospital-acquired pneumonia
2. RF:
   1. Reduced consciousness
      1. Sedative or antipsychotic medications — impair cough reflex and directly impair glottal function (increase aspiration risk)
      2. Illicit drugs or alcohol
      3. Anesthesia
      4. Generalized seizure
   2. Dysphagia
      1. Neurologic disorders
         1. Parkinson disease
         2. Multiple Sclerosis: bulbar symptoms presents (scanning speech)
            1. Pleuritic pain, dyspnea, leukocytosis, exudative (parapneumonic) pleural effusion
      2. Esophageal motility defects
      3. Protracted vomiting
      4. Gastroesophageal reflux
   3. Pharyngeal or glottal dysfunction
      1. Tracheostomy
      2. Intubation
      3. Nasogastric feeding
   4. Dental issues
      1. Gingivitis
      2. Poor dental hygiene
   5. Dementia
      1. Impaired swallowing mechanism — pharyngeal dysphagia causes aspiration due to insufficient closure of airway during swallowing
3. CF: Fever, productive cough
   1. Infiltrate in dependent portion of the lung
      1. Upright — lower lobes
      2. Recumbent — superior portion of lower lobe or posterior portion of upper lobe
4. Imaging
   1. Abscess = Cavitary lesion with air fluid levels
5. Tx: Same as CAP if empyema or lung abscess is absent
   1. Ceftriaxone + Azithromycin

## Recurrent Pneumonia

1. Same lung lobe
   1. Bronchial obstruction
      1. Extrinsic:
         1. Neoplasm
            1. Lung cancer: Evaluate with CT chest — high suspicion in long term smoker with recurrent pneumonia and localized airway obstruction
               1. Airway obstruction secondary to carcinoma
               2. Suspect if enlarging perihilar mass
         2. Adenopathy
      2. Intrinsic: Bronchiectasis, Foreign body
   2. Recurrent aspiration
      1. Altered consciousness: Seizures, Sedative, Antipsychotics, Alcohol, Illicit drugs
      2. Dysphagia:
         1. Neurologic disorder
            1. Parkinson disease: lingual bradykinesia and impaired cough reflex, routinely perform swallow study to assess function
         2. Esophageal motility issue
         3. Poor dental hygiene
         4. Gastroesophageal reflux
2. Different lung lobes
   1. Immunodeficiency: HIV, leukemia, CVID (common variable immunodeficiency)
   2. Sinopulmonary disease: Cystic fibrosis, Immotile cilia
   3. Noninfectious: Vasculitis, Bronchiolitis obliterans with organizing pneumonia

## Causes of Hypoxemia

| Cause | Examples | A-a gradient | Correct with supplemental O2 |
|-------|----------|-------------|------------------------------|
| Reduced PiO2 | High altitude | Normal | Yes |
| Hypoventilation | CNS depression, Morbid obesity | Normal | Yes |
| Diffusion limitation | Emphysema, ILD | Increased | Yes |
| V/Q mismatch | Small PE, lobar pneumonia | Increased | Yes |
| Large intrapulmonary shunt | Diffuse pulmonary edema (ARDS) | Increased | No + decreased lung compliance |
| Large dead-space ventilation | Massive PE, R-L intracardiac shunt | Increased | No |

# SYSTEMIC DISEASE

## Intubation Indications

1. Expanding hematoma, swelling or emphysema in neck (risk of airway obstruction)
2. Glasgow-Coma score of 8 or less (eight and intub-eight) [risk of not protecting airway]
3. Severe inhalation injury (i.e., breathing smoke during house fire)
4. Breathing is noisy or gurgly

## Anaphylaxis

1. Acute illness involving the skin/mucosa and either respiratory or cardiovascular compromise
2. RF: NSAIDs (nonimmunologic mast cell activation), BB (unopposed alpha effects), Dicloxacillin (IgE-med mast cell degranulation)
3. Types
   1. Biphasic: Initial reaction — asymptomatic period 1-30hr — recurrence of symptoms
      1. IM Epinephrine up to 3 times
   2. Protracted: Symptoms last hours to days despite treatment
4. CF
   1. CVS — Vasodilation (hypotension and tissue edema), Tachycardia
   2. Respiratory
      1. Upper airway edema --> stridor and hoarseness
      2. Lower airway: Bronchospasm --> wheezing
   3. Cutaneous — Urticarial rash, pruritis, flushing
   4. GI — N, V, Abd pain
5. Immediate management
   1. IM Epinephrine
      1. IM preferred, repeated up to 3 times
      2. IV in severe/refractory case
      3. Alpha-1 and beta-2 agonist effects quickly cause vasoconstriction and inc BP, and bronchodilator to improve wheezing
      4. Decreases systemic inflammatory mediator release from mast cells and basophils and halts anaphylaxis
      5. *NE not used due to negligible beta-2 activity (lack of bronchodilation)
   2. IV crystalloid & Trendelenburg position for hypotension
   3. Albuterol for bronchospasm
   4. Early intubation FOR UPPER AIRWAY OBSTRUCTION *Note: stridor and hoarseness (wheezing is lower airway sign)
6. Adjunct management
   1. H1/H2 Antihistamines
   2. Glucocorticoids (anti-inflammatory)
   3. Glucagon for patients on beta blockers (reversal)
   4. Hospital admission for refractory cases
7. Complications
   1. Death from hypoxia +/- distributive shock

## Acute Lung Injury (ALI) — Nitrofurantoin

1. Short term use: Acute hypersensitivity
   1. Symptom onset 3-9 days from medication initiation
   2. Basilar crackles
   3. CXR: bilateral, basilar opacities
   4. Erythematous maculopapular rash
   5. Unilateral pleural effusion
   6. Labs: Leukocytosis, Eosinophilia
2. Long term use: Interstitial lung disease

## Severe ALI — Atelectasis

1. Mucus plugging
   1. General anesthesia reduces FRC
      1. Subsegmental atelectasis may lead to obstruction and inflammation --> large airway obstruction and segmental collapse
      2. Mediastinal shifting toward opacification (vs. opposite in pleural effusion) to occupy vacant space
   2. Smoking
2. Low-grade fever and mild respiratory insufficiency

## Primary Adrenal Insufficiency

1. PP
   1. Autoimmune Adrenalitis #1 MCC
   2. Infectious Adrenalitis: *fever, pulmonary airspace disease (cough) with lymphadenopathy and prior residence in endemic area
      1. Miliary TB #2 MCC Worldwide
      2. Histoplasmosis
2. CF: Destruction of all 3 layers
   1. Decreased Aldosterone --> Lightheadedness, Orthostasis — Renal Na wasting with accompanying hyponatremia (ADH stimulation) and hyperkalemia
   2. Decreased Cortisol --> Hypotension, Hypoglycemia, Peripheral eosinophilia (cortisol facilitates eosinophil migration from bloodstream to tissue)
   3. Decreased Androgens

## TB

1. Reactivation: Apical cavitary lesion
   1. Low grade fever, night sweats, weight loss +/- cough with blood-tinged sputum
2. Occurs in patients from Endemic areas: highest risk in those who lived in US <=5 years
   1. Mexico
   2. Philippines
   3. China, Vietnam, India
   4. Dominican Republic, Haiti

## Neuromuscular Dysfunction

1. Myasthenia Gravis
   1. Myasthenic crisis and Acute Respiratory failure — Endotracheal intubation
      1. Diaphragmatic impairment "poor inspiratory effort" and respiratory failure; bulbar weakness "chewing and sensation of choking on food"
2. ALS
3. Guillain-Barre Syndrome

## Diffuse Alveolar Hemorrhage

1. Life-threatening disorder
2. CF: hemoptysis, falling hematocrit, diffuse pulmonary infiltrates and hypoxemic respiratory failure
3. Medical emergency due to the morbidity and mortality associated with failure to treat promptly

### Anti-GBM Disease

1. "Basement Membrane Antibodies" Renal (proteinuria <3.5g/day, acute renal failure, dysmorphic red cells in urine) and Pulmonary (sob, cough, hemoptysis)
2. Dx: renal biopsy showing linear IgG antibodies along glomerular BM

## Dyspnea of Pregnancy

1. Normal physiologic changes cause dyspnea
2. Progesterone-induced hyperventilation = Hi PaO2, Low PaCO2, Respiratory alkalosis
3. Hi Minute ventilation, Lo RV and FRC, Normal VC and FEV1

## Diaphragmatic Paralysis — ALS Patient

1. SOB at night, atrophic tongue with fasciculation, elevated hemidiaphragm, PFT — Dec Forced Vital Capacity, Dec Maximal Inspiratory Pressure, Normal DLCO
2. Represents restrictive pattern due to extrinsic limitation of lung expansion; DLCO/alveolar gas exchange normal
3. Also have disordered oropharyngeal neuromuscular transmission due to peripheral nerve damage (bulbar muscle weakness) which causes oropharyngeal dysphagia (coughing after eating, nasal congestion, aspiration). Similarly seen in Multiple sclerosis.

### Unilateral Diaphragmatic Paralysis

1. Etiology: Phrenic nerve, Viral infection (Herpes, poliomyelitis), Neurologic (ALS, GBS), Idiopathic
2. Dyspnea upon exertion (not at rest); Orthopnea (gravity opposes chest wall and lung expansion in recumbent position)
3. Dx: Paradoxical movement of diaphragm on brisk inspiration

## AV Malformation

### Hereditary Hemorrhagic Telangiectasia

1. Nasal telangiectasia cause recurrent epistaxis
2. Pulmonary AVMs cause hemoptysis
   1. CXR: "Multifocal, well-circumscribed, smooth nodules"
   2. Infrascapular murmur (continuous pulmonary bruit) due to turbulent flow of AVM

## Diaphragmatic Hernia

1. Suspicion for diaphragmatic rupture in patient with prior blunt thorax-abdominal trauma and abnormal CXR
   1. CXR: bowel loops in thorax, mediastinal shift
   2. Delayed presentation: in children after progressive expansion and diaphragmatic injury
2. Dx: CT chest and abdomen
3. Surgical repair after CT; CT aids in operative approach (thoracotomy vs laparotomy)

## Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA)

1. Necrotizing pulmonary vasculitis
2. CF:
   1. Upper Respir: sinusitis/otitis, saddle nose
   2. Lower Respir: lung nodules/cavitation
   3. Renal: rapidly progressive GN
   4. Skin: Livedo reticularis, non healing ulcers
   5. ANEMIA OF CHRONIC DISEASE
3. Dx:
   1. ANCA (Antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies):
      1. PR3 (Proteinase 3-ANCA/C-ANCA) mostly, MPO (Myeloperoxidase-ANCA/p-ANCA ~20%)
      2. *Note: HIV increases chances of false positive ANCA results and should be ruled out
   2. Biopsy:
      1. Skin: Leukocytoclastic vasculitis
      2. Kidney: Pauci-immune GN
      3. Lung: Granulomatous vasculitis
   3. CXR: Pulmonary nodules and alveolar consolidation
4. Management: CST + Immunomodulators (MTX, Cyclophosphamide)

## Sickle Cell — Pulmonary HTN

1. Right heart failure due to pulmonary remodeling causing Pulmonary HTN
2. Intravascular hemolysis causes chronic inflammation and endothelial dysfunction — hyperplasia and hypertrophy of small pulmonary vessels = vascular remodeling and increased pulmonary vascular resistance — decreased RV output and left ventricular filling = low CO
3. Initial presentation of pulmonary HTN as exertional dyspnea and fatigue
