Liver Markers:
- Hepatocellular LFT pattern = inc Transaminases
- Cholestatic LFT pattern = inc Alkaline phosphatase, inc Direct hyperbilirubinemia
- Aspartate Aminotransferase = SGOT
- Alanine Aminotransferase = SGPT
- Hepatomegaly: Liver palpable below the right costal margin

# Hepatitis A

1. Transmission: Fecal-oral, crowded homeless shelter, day care center children often don't have jaundice and go undiagnosed
   1. Increased risk: Unvaccinated, International travelers, Men who have sex with men
2. Sx:
   1. Fever, N, RUQ pain, Jaundice
   2. Hepatomegaly, Alk Phos >150U/L
   3. Direct Hyperbilirubinemia, Dark urine bilirubinuria, pale stool lack bilirubin, Jaundice and pruritus in 2 weeks
3. Dx: Elevated enzymes >1000U/L, IgM Anti-HAV
4. Tx: Supportive, complete recovery in 2-3 months

# Hepatitis B

1. Dx: HBsAg and IgM anti-HBc
   1. Window period between HBsAg and anti-HBs, but Anti-HBc remains elevated during window period

# Hepatitis C

1. Chronic Hep C = HBsAg present for >6months — Hep C more likely to be chronic than Hep B
   1. IgG Anti-HBc antibodies present, but don't confer immunity (Note: Hep B is cleared if Anti-HBs and Anti-HBc present but Anti-HBc does not indicate immunity)
2. Vaccinate against HAV and HBV (risk of severe liver failure in unvaccinated/nonimmune)
3. ~20% develop Cirrhosis — Thrombocytopenia, Hypoalbuminemia, Coagulopathy (inc PT/INR)
   1. Diagnosis confirmed with liver biopsy — extensive fibrosis and regenerative nodules
4. Associations
   1. Mixed Cryoglobulinemia: low complement, (+) Rheumatoid factor, palpable purpura
5. Person exposed to Hep C (+) patient
   1. No post-exposure prophylaxis is indicated given there are no effective agents and the risk of transmission is very low

# Chronic Liver Disease (Infectious Hepatitis)

1. Vaccinations
   1. General population vaccinations: IM influenza, Tdap
   2. Disease specific vaccines
      1. PPSV23
         1. >65yo (general population)
         2. <65yo with comorbidities — chronic liver, lung, heart disease; DM, Smoking
      2. Hep A and Hep B if patient lacks documented immunity

# Asymptomatic Abnormal LFTs

1. 1st: Evaluate for hepatitis risk factors (drug and alcohol), travel outside US, blood transfusions or high-risk sexual practices
   1. Alcohol
   2. Meds — NSAIDs, Antibiotics, HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors, Antiepileptic drugs, Antituberculous drugs, Herbal preparations
   3. Viral agents
   4. Sarcoidosis
2. 2nd: Discontinue any of the above agents and retest in 6 months
   1. Continued LFTs elevation --> Chronic LFT elevation
3. 3rd: Evaluation of Chronic LFT elevation
   1. Viral Hep B and C testing
   2. Hemochromatosis
   3. Fatty liver
   4. *If above tests are normal — evaluate for muscle disorders (polymyositis) and thyroid disease

# Acetaminophen overdose

1. Hepatocellular pattern of liver injury Elevated AST and ALT >10,000 U/L
2. CF
   1. May be asymptomatic during first 24 hours
   2. Hepatic failure: asterixis, encephalopathy
3. Management:
   1. May be asymptomatic during first 24 hours --> activated charcoal if ingested within past 4 hours
   2. Obtain acetaminophen levels
   3. N-acetylcysteine

# Alcoholic Hepatitis

1. Fever and jaundice
2. RUQ pain +/- epigastric pain
3. Abdominal distention due to ascites
4. Tender hepatomegaly
5. Lab values:
   1. AST >2x ALT, Elevated ALT, Elevated GGT, Elevated Ferritin, inc Alkaline phosphatase (nonspecific), inc Bilirubin, inc INR
   2. Macrocytic anemia
6. Dx: Clinical diagnosis
7. Tx: Abstinence, Hydration + Nutrition, Acid suppression

# Autoimmune Hepatitis (Anti-smooth muscle antibodies)

1. CF
   1. Asymptomatic
      1. 25% asymptomatic and identified by routine lab work with Hepatocellular pattern of liver injury — Elevated AST and ALT
   2. Symptomatic
   3. Associated with other autoimmune disorders: Vitiligo, Autoimmune thyroiditis, Celiac disease
2. Labs
   1. Hepatocellular pattern of liver injury — Elevated AST and ALT; Alkaline phosphatase and Bilirubin are normal or mildly elevated
   2. inc inc gamma gap (total protein - albumin = >4 g/dL)
   3. inc Autoantibodies
      1. Anti-smooth muscle autoantibodies
      2. Anti-liver/kidney microsomal type 1
      3. Antinuclear (nonspecific)
3. Histology: Portal and periportal lymphoplasmacytic infiltration
4. Tx: Prednisone +/- Azathioprine

# Alpha-1-antitrypsin deficiency (AATD)

1. Autosomal Dominant
2. Emphysema and Liver disease (Chronic hepatitis, Cirrhosis)

# Nonalcoholic Fatty Liver Disease

1. Middle aged, obese with features of metabolic syndrome (Central obesity, DM, Hyperlipidemia, Hypertension)
2. RF: Cushing Syndrome (increased glucocorticoid production)
3. PP (all possibilities)
   1. Increased transport of FFA (free fatty acids) from adipose tissue to the liver
      1. Peripheral insulin resistance --> inc peripheral lipolysis, TG synthesis and Hepatic uptake of FFA
      2. Hepatic FFA increase oxidative stress and produce pro inflammatory cytokines (TNF-a)
      3. FFA further increase insulin resistance by impairing insulin-dependent glucose uptake --> inc hepatic gluconeogenesis
      4. *Insulin normally decreases lipolysis in adipose tissue (encourages storage of fat in adipose tissue)
   2. Decreased oxidation of FFA in the liver
   3. Decreased clearance of FFA from the liver (due to decreased VLDL production)
4. Stages
   1. Bland steatosis
   2. Steatohepatitis: Inflammation and necrosis AST/ALT ratio <1
   3. Fibrosis and Cirrhosis
5. Histology: Can resemble alcohol-induced liver disease with minimal/no alcohol history
   1. Macrovesicular fat deposition, peripheral displacement of nuclei
6. US: Hyperechoic texture = hepatic fatty infiltration
7. Tx: Diet and exercise, Bariatric surgery if BMI >35

# Ischemic Hepatitis

1. Due to global hypoperfusion and/or hypoxemia
2. Zone 3 (centrilobular) necrosis
3. Severe aminotransferase elevations with bilirubin levels unaffected for few days

# Acute Liver Failure

1. To establish diagnosis
   1. Acute onset liver injury (elevated aminotransferases >1000U/L)
   2. Encephalopathy
   3. Impaired synthetic function (INR>1.5) w/o cirrhosis
2. Commonly due to drug toxicity and acute viral hepatitis (HepD superinfection in IVDA especially in asx Chronic HBV carrier)

# Chronic Liver Disease

1. Complication: Hepatopulmonary syndrome
   1. Due to intrapulmonary vascular dilations
   2. CF: Platypnea (increased dyspnea while upright), Orthodeoxia (oxygen desaturation while upright)

# Cirrhosis

1. VW HAPPENS
   1. Viral Hepatitis (B & C)
   2. Wilson's Disease
   3. Hemochromatosis
      1. HFE gene mutation
      2. CF: Skin, MSK: Chondrocalcinosis (Pseudogout)
      3. Dx: HFE gene mutation or Iron studies (if HFE mutation negative)
   4. Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
   5. Primary Biliary Cholangitis
   6. Primary Biliary Sclerosis
   7. EtOH
      1. Alcoholic: dec liver inflammation and portal pressure by complete abstinence from alcohol
         1. Alcohol-related liver inflammation and active fibrogenesis improves; reduction in portal pressure makes ascites more diuretic-responsive
      2. Baclofen: decreases cravings in alcoholic liver disease and can help with cessation
   8. NASH: Non-alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease
2. Labs
   1. Thrombocytopenia, Hypoalbuminemia, Coagulopathy (inc PT/INR)
3. Management
   1. Tx underlying liver disease
      1. HBV, HCV: Antiviral therapy
      2. NASH: Weight loss
   2. Preventive care
      1. Avoid alcohol and hepatotoxic medications
      2. Vaccinate for HAV and HBV if not immune
   3. Manage complications
      1. Screen for esophageal varices — Endoscopy
      2. Screen for HCC — Ultrasound +/- Serum AFP every 6-12 months
      3. Frequent clinical assessment for ascites and encephalopathy (prophylactic tx not recommended)
         1. Ascites (Tx: Diuretics)
         2. Encephalopathy would present with abnormal mental status, sleep disturbance, asterixis (Tx: Lactulose, Rifaximin)

# Cirrhosis: Complications

## Ascites: SAAG

1. >1.1 = Portal Hypertension — Increased capillary hydrostatic pressure
2. <1.1 = TB, CA "more stuff so closer to 0"

## Varices

1. Major cause of morbidity and mortality
2. Diagnostic upper endoscopy to assess for varices and determine risk of hemorrhage
3. Management: Prophylaxis
   1. Medium/large size: Start nonselective BB — Propranolol, Nadolol
      1. Decrease adrenergic tone in mesenteric arterioles --> unopposed alpha-mediated vasoconstriction and decreased portal venous flow
   2. Endoscopic variceal ligation (if BB are contraindicated)
4. Management: Active Bleeding
   1. Endoscopic Sclerotherapy
   2. Octreotide
      1. Long acting somatostatin analogue
      2. Causes splanchnic vasoconstriction and reduces portal blood flow by (-) Glucagon release

## Hepatic Encephalopathy

1. CF
   1. Hypokalemia: Worsens HE as intracellular acidosis (excreted intracellular K replaced by H+) causes increased NH3 production in renal tubular cells
      1. Prompt K repletion required
   2. Metabolic alkalosis: Worsens HE — promotes conversion of NH4 (brain impermeable) to NH3 (brain permeable)

## Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)

1. E.coli and Klebsiella
2. Abnormal Reitan trail test: similar to connect-the-dots pictures
3. Dx: Diagnostic paracentesis

## Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC) --> Repeatedly bloody Ascites

## Portal Vein Thrombosis (Pre-hepatic obstruction of blood flow)

1. Acute: Abdominal pain
2. Chronic: Variceal bleeding

# Drug-induced liver injury

1. Isoniazid causes liver injury with features similar to viral hepatitis
   1. "Panlobular mononuclear infiltration, hepatic cell necrosis"

# Hepatic hydrothorax

1. Pleural effusion not due to cardiac or pulmonary abnormality
2. Seen in patients with cirrhosis and portal hypertension
3. Small defects in the diaphragm allow peritoneal fluid to pass into pleural space on the right side due to less muscular hemidiaphragm --> transudative pleural effusion
4. CF: Dyspnea, cough, pleuritic chest pain, hypoxemia
5. Dx: Documentation of effusion (CXR) and testing to exclude other causes (Thoracentesis, Echo)
6. Tx: Salt restriction and diuretic
   1. Severe symptoms: Therapeutic thoracentesis
   2. Definitive Tx: Liver transplant
   3. Chest tube placement avoided due to risk of large-volume protein, fluid and electrolyte losses and renal failure

# Jaundice: Hereditary Hyperbilirubinemia

## Impaired Conjugation

1. Gilbert syndrome: MC inherited disorder of bilirubin metabolism, Episodic Jaundice
   1. PP: Decreased Hepatic UDP glucuronosyltransferase activity = Dec Conjugated Bilirubin
   2. CF: Recurrent episodes of mild jaundice, provoked by stress
   3. Dx: Isolated inc Unconjugated bilirubin
      1. Normal CBC, blood smear, reticulocyte count
      2. Normal AST, ALT, Alkaline phosphatase
   4. Tx: Benign, no treatment required
2. Crigler-Najjar syndrome
3. Newborn physiologic jaundice

## Impaired Uptake and secretion from liver

1. Dubin-Johnson syndrome: intermittent scleral icterus and conjugated hyperbilirubinemia with normal LFTs
2. Rotor syndrome

## Unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia non-hereditary

1. Hemolytic anemia (rbc destruction) — Low Hb, Inc Reticulocyte count

## Enzyme patterns

1. Normal transaminase and Alkaline phosphatase — Inherited bilirubin metabolism disorder (Dubin-Johnson)
2. inc transaminase and normal Alkaline phosphatase — Intrinsic liver disease (viral hepatitis, hemochromatosis)
3. inc Alkaline phosphatase out of proportion to transaminase — Intrahepatic cholestasis or biliary obstruction
   1. Malignant biliary obstruction
      1. Etiology: Cholangiocarcinoma, Pancreatic/hepatocellular carcinoma, Metastasis
      2. CF: Jaundice, pruritus, acholic stools, dark urine
         1. Weight loss, RUQ pain, RUQ mass or hepatomegaly
         2. inc Direct bilirubin, ALP, GGT
      3. Dx: Serum tumor markers (CEA, CA-19, AFP)
         1. Abdominal imaging
         2. EUS or ERCP for tissue diagnosis if unclear

# Solid Liver Masses

## Focal Nodular Hyperplasia (young F)

1. Anomalous arteries, arterial flow and central scar on imaging
2. Vs. Hepatic Adenoma: Not associated with oral contraceptives, increased arterial flow on imaging

## Hepatic Adenoma (young and middle age F: Long term oral contraceptives)

1. Possible hemorrhage or malignant transformation
2. US: Well-demarcated hyperechoic lesion
3. Contrast CT: Early peripheral involvement (needle biopsy avoided due to bleeding risk)
4. Surgical excision preferred
5. Rupture: Severe RUQ pain, hemorrhagic shock — Fever, inc liver enzymes, Hypotension, Tachycardia, Anemia

## Hydatid cyst

1. Echinococcus tapeworm infection
2. Large hydatid cysts can cause hepatomegaly, RUQ pain, nausea/vomiting

## Hepatic vein thrombosis = Budd-Chiari Syndrome (Post-hepatic obstruction of blood flow)

1. RUQ pain, Ascites, Hepatosplenomegaly

## Pyogenic liver abscess (Acutely ill)

1. Fever, Hepatomegaly, Elevated liver transaminases, Jaundice, Weight loss
2. Shaking chills
3. Significant leukocytosis
4. Marked hepatic tenderness with guarding and rocking tenderness
5. Imaging: Multiloculated, hypoechoic hepatic lesions

## Regenerative Nodules: Acute or Chronic liver injury (cirrhosis)

## Hepatocellular Carcinoma

1. CF
   1. Decompensated liver failure: Ascites (persistently bloody), Jaundice, Hypoalbuminemia
   2. Cachexia "temporal wasting"
   3. Palpable liver nodule
   4. Weight loss
2. MCC: Cirrhosis
   1. Screening with Abdominal US every 6 months since patients may be initially asymptomatic
   2. Patients unaware of cirrhosis diagnosis until complications of variceal bleeding or HCC develop
3. Labs: Thrombocytopenia, Hypoalbuminemia, Coagulopathy (inc PT/INR), Hyperbilirubinemia
4. Dx
   1. Triple phase arterial contrast CT
   2. inc AFP (glycoprotein produced by fetal liver and yolk sac)

## Liver Metastasis: Single/multiple lesions; Known extrahepatic malignancy

1. MC malignancy of the liver
2. Commonly from Colorectal cancer or Pancreatic cancer (venous drainage through portal system)
   1. Evaluate with Colonoscopy (localizes tumor and provides a tissue diagnosis)
   2. Abdominal CT often misses primary intraluminal tumors
   3. Surgical resection of liver tumor and primary tumor can be curative

# Trauma — Hepatic Laceration (Blunt Abdominal Trauma)

1. Hepatic Hematoma can lead to Hematobilia if a drain is not inserted during liver laceration repair

# ASCITES

1. Color
   1. Bloody: trauma, malignancy (MC is Hepatocellular Carcinoma), TB *HCC is repeatedly bloody
   2. Milky: chylous
   3. Turbid: infectious
   4. Straw: benign, cirrhosis
2. Neutrophils: >250/mm peritonitis (secondary to SBP)
3. Total Protein
   1. >2.5g/dL: high protein — CHF, Constrictive pericarditis, Peritoneal carcinomatosis, TB, Budd-Chiari, Fungal
   2. <2.5g/dL: low protein — Cirrhosis (high hydrostatic pressure), Nephrotic syndrome
4. SAAG
   1. >1.1g/dL: portal hypertension — Cardiac ascites, Cirrhosis, Budd-Chiari
   2. <1.1g/dL: no port-htn — TB, Peritoneal carcinomatosis, Pancreatic ascites, Nephrotic syndrome
5. Complication: Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (Perform paracentesis)
