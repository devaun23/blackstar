# ISCHEMIC HEART DISEASE

Note: ASCVD relates to disease of heart and blood vessels. #1 RF: Smoking (followed by Diabetes)

## Stress Test

Note: Diseased (stenotic) coronary arteries do not constrict during pharmacologic vasodilator stress test but instead dilate to a lesser degree than normal vessels

1. Exercise ECG test: Increase in HR ± myocardial contractility → increase in myocardial O2 demand
2. Pharm with Adenosine or Dipyridamole: A2A Receptor agonist → coronary vasodilator → several-fold augmentation of blood flow in nonobstructed arteries vs stenosed = nonuniform isotope uptake = ischemic defect
3. Dobutamine stress Echo: Increase in HR ± myocardial contractility
   1. Indication: Patients with contraindications to vasodilator stress testing (hypotension, obstructive lung disease)
   2. Contraindication: Severe arrhythmia or tachycardia
4. Exercise Stress Echo: Preferred if patient has abnormal EKG (LBBB); shows areas of hypoperfusion

## Coronary Artery Disease

1. Evidence of ischemia at 90% maximal HR during exercise ECG not unusual with known CAD
2. Diagnosis: Single photon emission CT scan
   1. Technetium-99 (Tc-99m) perfusion agents injected at rest and stress testing
   2. Normal tracer uptake
   3. Decreased tracer uptake at rest and exercise—Fixed defect = scar tissue = decreased perfusion and CAD
   4. Decreased tracer uptake with stress, normal at rest—Reversible defect = inducible ischemia = CAD
   5. Management: Antiplatelet for MI prevention, BB, Modification of RF

## Stable Angina

1. Mismatch of myocardial oxygen supply and demand
2. Diagnosis: Exercise stress Echo (regional wall motion abnormalities) or Exercise stress ECG (if no baseline ECG abnormalities)
3. BB—Metoprolol, Atenolol (1st line): Decrease myocardial contractility and HR → Decreased O2 demand
4. CCB
   1. Non-dihydropyridine: Diltiazem, Verapamil — 2nd line if BB contraindicated
   2. Dihydropyridine: Amlodipine, Felodipine — Increase myocardial O2 supply via coronary vasodilation; Reduce O2 demand via reduction in Cardiac Afterload
      - Caution: Used in conjunction with BB due to risk of reflex tachycardia with monotherapy
5. Nitroglycerin
   1. MOA: Direct vascular smooth muscle relaxation → systemic venodilation
   2. Short term: Sublingual — brief anginal relief
   3. Long acting: Isosorbide mononitrate — added to BB for persistent stable angina
   4. Anti-ischemic: Decrease Cardiac Preload → Dec LV EDV and ESV → Dec Afterload/LV wall stress = Decrease in myocardial O2 demand
   5. Coronary arteries: Nitrate-induced coronary vasodilation paradoxically decreases coronary perfusion pressure across stenotic lesion by diverting blood flow to arterioles supplying normal myocardium
6. Ranolazine: Reserved for refractory stable angina
7. Stable converting to Unstable: Longer and more frequent episodes—Immediately admit vs exercise test in 24hrs

## Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS)

1. MCC: Coronary atherosclerosis
2. Atypical Presentation
   1. RF: Women, Elderly, Diabetics (no overt chest pain)
   2. Symptoms: Chest pain 80-85%, Dyspnea 70-75%, Nausea 40-55%, Vomiting 15-20%, Epigastric pain 10-15%
   - Note: Abdominal pain and n/v are atypical, cardiac causes must be excluded first
3. Management: ECG is 1st choice in anyone with history and risk factors of ACS

### Unstable Angina (USA)

1. Presentation: Sudden onset chest discomfort, arm numbness, diaphoresis, T-wave inversion, "Burning sensation in the center of the chest"
2. In-patient Management
   1. Anti-platelet: ASA + clopidogrel or ASA + ticagrelor
   2. Anticoagulation: UFH, enoxaparin, bivalirudin
   3. BB: Cardioselective (Metoprolol, Atenolol) — CI in heart failure and bradycardia; IV for HTN only
   4. Nitrates: Reduce O2 demand and ischemic pain — Caution with hypotension (RV infarction)
   5. High-intensity statin: Atorvastatin, Rosuvastatin
   6. Coronary revascularization: PCI within 24 hours
      1. Complication: Coronary stent thrombosis → fully occlusive thrombus = STEMI
         - May occur acutely (<24hrs), sub acutely (<1mo) or delayed up to a year
         - Greatest risk prior to reendothelialization (exposed stent material is thrombogenic)
         - DAPT does not completely eliminate risk
         - CF: STEMI, cardiogenic shock, sudden cardiac death
         - Management: Revascularization via PCI
      2. Complication: Coronary perforation → Cardiac Tamponade
3. Discharge Management
   1. Dual antiplatelet: Low-dose ASA (indefinitely) + P2Y12 inhibitor (12 months)
   2. BB: Metoprolol, Carvedilol (indefinitely)
   3. ACEi or ARB (indefinitely for reduced LVEF)
   4. High-intensity statin (indefinitely)
   5. Mineralocorticoid antagonist: Spironolactone (for reduced LVEF with symptoms or comorbid DM)

### NSTEMI

1. Presentation: Similar to USA + Troponin elevation
   - Note: Significant troponin elevation within 6-12 hours differentiates NSTEMI from UA
2. Management: Similar to USA, Medical management initially, reperfusion within 24 hrs

### STEMI

1. Elderly: Atypical symptoms (dyspnea, nausea, diaphoresis); Large areas of ischemia (anterolateral-LAD) can cause LV failure and cardiogenic shock
2. Diagnosis: ST-segment elevation in 2 contiguous leads
3. Management
   1. 1st: Stabilization — MONA-BASH-C, use Morphine if Nitro is inadequate
   2. 2nd: Immediate Coronary Reperfusion — Emergency Cardiac Cath and PCI > fibrinolysis
      - Antithrombotic (ASA, P2Y12-I) and anticoagulation given prior to procedure
4. STEMI with Flash Pulmonary Edema
   1. Acute anterior wall STEMI → Ischemic HF (S3, BP 110/70, HR 60, O2 sat 90% NC) → Acute pulmonary edema
   2. Management
      1. Furosemide: Decreases Cardiac Preload—lowers pulmonary capillary pressure; CAUTION: HYPOTENSION AND AKI
      2. BB: Avoided in Decompensated CHF or Bradycardia
      3. Nitroglycerin: AVOID IN HYPOTENSIVE
      4. Morphine: INCREASED MORTALITY IN HYPOTENSIVE PATIENT BY ↓PRELOAD
5. Stents
   1. Metal and drug-eluting stents are thrombogenic until re-endothelialization
   2. Thrombotic risk mitigated by DAPT (ASA + P2Y12R-blocker)
   3. ACS due to stent thrombosis presents as STEMI/NSTEMI/USA with high morbidity and mortality
   4. Match leads of new changes with location of stent
      1. Anterior: V1-V4 for LAD
      2. Lateral: V5-V6, I, aVL for LCx
      3. Inferior: II, III, aVF for RCA

### Vasospastic (Variant/Prinzmetal) Angina

1. Cause: Hyperreactivity of coronary smooth muscle (Most similar to Raynaud phenomenon)
2. Presentation: "Nocturnal substernal chest pain that awakens from sleep, sedentary lifestyle"; Young <50yo, Smoking
3. Diagnosis: Ambulatory ECG (during episode): ST elevation; Coronary angiography: No CAD
4. Treatment: CCB (preventative), Sublingual NO (abortive)
