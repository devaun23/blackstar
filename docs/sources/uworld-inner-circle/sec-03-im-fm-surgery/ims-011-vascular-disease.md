# DISEASES OF VASCULATURE

## Hypertensive Emergency

1. Acute increase in BP >180/120mmHg
2. Tx: Hydralazine, Labetalol, Nitroprusside (SE: Cyanide poisoning)

## Aortic Dissection

### History of

1. HTN
2. Marfan Syndrome
3. Turner Syndrome (Aortic dissection risk increased further with pregnancy)
4. Cocaine may cause rapid and transient hypertension leading to aortic dissection in young pt
   - Sudden onset, severe chest pain and pleural effusion due to hemothorax

### Clinical Features

1. Acute Type A (Ascending) → Immediate surgery
2. Aortic rupture into pericardial space → hemopericardium → Cardiac Tamponade and Cardiogenic Shock
   1. Aortic Regurg — proximal extension into aortic valvular annulus "descending diastolic murmur"
   2. Elevated creatinine — distal extension into renal arteries
3. Surgical Emergency — Intraoperative TEE performed to identify additional abnormalities
4. Type B: Descending

### Presentation

1. >20mmHg difference in SBP between arms
2. CXR: Mediastinal widening

### Complications

1. Cardiac Tamponade — syncope due to pericardial fluid accumulation
2. Type A: Vertebral artery dissection
   1. Neck pain, ischemic stroke, headache
   2. Posterior spinal cord ischemia (loss of proprioception/vibration, mild weakness)
3. Type B: Anterior Spinal Cord Ischemia
   1. Type B disrupts thoracic spinal cord blood supply → Anterior Spinal artery disruption
   2. Ant Spinal Cord supplies Spinothalamic and Corticospinal tract
      1. Motor paresis at and below lesion (T1 or below: LE affected, UE preserved)
      2. Loss of crude touch/pain sensation
      3. Bladder paresis — urinary retention
   3. *Dorsal columns preserved (blood supply from Vertebral/PICA → Posterior Spinal artery)*

### Diagnosis

1. HD Stable: CT angiography (avoid in renal dysfunction)
2. HD Unstable: TEE (preferred in renal dysfunction)
3. CXR: Widened cardiac silhouette, pericardial effusion
4. MR angiography (time consuming, gadolinium-contrast avoided in kidney disease — nephrogenic systemic fibrosis)

### Management (for non-Type A)

1. Pain control — Morphine
2. IV BB — Labetalol, Propranolol, Esmolol
   - Reduce HR, SBP and LV contractility — reduces aortic wall stress
   - Note: Nitro/Hydralazine ↓ BP via vessel wall dilation but cause further stress and exacerbate tear
3. Sodium Nitroprusside (if SBP>120)

## Thoracic Aortic Aneurysm

1. Presentation
   1. Mostly ascending aorta — between aortic valve and brachiocephalic artery
   2. Degenerative changes → disruption of aortic wall MEDIAL LAYER → loss of elasticity → aortic dilation
      1. Systemic HTN, repeated stress from pulsating arterial wave, enzymatic breakdown of structural proteins = cystic medial necrosis of aorta
      2. Elderly patient with HTN and atherosclerotic disease of vasa vasorum
      3. Occurs mostly in tunica media → progressive weakening → can increase risk of TAA and Aortic Dissection
   3. Underlying CT disease — Ehlers-Danlos or Marfan → EARLY-ONSET cystic medial degeneration
   4. Mostly asx until discovery on CXR "vague chest discomfort, clear lungs, normal heart sounds"
2. Diagnosis
   1. CXR: Widened mediastinum, enlarged aortic knob, tracheal deviation
   2. CT with contrast: Confirm diagnosis (CXR cannot distinguish TAA from tortuous aorta)

## Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm

1. Enlargement of abdominal aorta diameter to >3cm
2. RF: >60yo, Smokers, Male, Hx Atherosclerosis or CT disease
3. Anterior: Direct leakage into peritoneum → rapid hemodynamic instability and shock
4. Posterior
   1. Proximal: Back pain (L1-L4), abdominal pain, flank pain
   2. Distal: Lower abdominal pain, groin pain
   3. Leakage into retroperitoneum = Delayed onset of hemodynamic instability
5. S/S
   1. Few symptoms until significantly expands or ruptures
   2. Acute onset severe abdominal or flank pain, Syncope, pulsatile abdominal mass, flank or umbilical hematoma
   3. CXR: Prevertebral aortic calcifications due to extensive atherosclerosis
   4. Distal ischemia due to embolization of thrombus or atherosclerotic debris
   5. High-output HF due to erosion of AAA into Vena Cava (aortocaval fistula)
6. Dx: HD Stable: CT abdomen | HD Unstable with known AAA: Emergency repair | HD Unstable unknown: FAST → repair
7. Management
   1. Smoking cessation
   2. Labetalol: Nonselective a-1 and B-1 blocker
   3. Elective repair >5.5 cm
   4. Urgent repair for symptomatic and HD stable
   5. Emergency repair for symptomatic and HD unstable
8. Screening: 1-time Abdominal Ultrasound for men 65-75yo with smoking history or family history
9. Association: Peripheral Artery Aneurysms (Pulsatile groin mass below inguinal ligament — femoral, popliteal arteries)
10. Complication: Ischemia of bowel
    - "Surgical repair of infrarenal aorta, LLQ pain, bloody diarrhea"
    - Loss of IMA during aortic graft placement → inadequate collateral perfusion to L and sigmoid colon
11. Complication: Avoid Fluoroquinolones (risk of increased collagen degradation — Achilles tendon rupture, retinal detachment, aortic aneurysm rupture)

## Acute Limb Ischemia

### Causes

1. **Arterial Emboli** — Cardiac/intraarterial thrombus (AF, LV thrombus, IE)
   1. No prior history, "had acute anterior MI, undergoing cardiac rehab"
   2. Most arterial emboli originate from heart
      1. Left atrial (AFib) — "72yo M, severe left lower leg pain; smoker; A fib; DM; distal pulses not palpable"
      2. Prevention: Anticoagulation — NOACs (Apixaban, Dabigatran, Rivaroxaban) or Warfarin
      3. Left ventricle (LV aneurysm)
      4. IE, Prosthetic valve (require higher INR than adenocarcinoma)
2. **PAD (Chronic Arterial Insufficiency, Atherosclerotic disease)**
   1. Arterial Thrombosis
      1. RF: HTN, Hyperlipidemia, Smoking, Prior claudication
      2. Preexisting collateral circulation → less dramatic presentation, Lack classic 6Ps
      3. Diminished pulses in unaffected extremities, shiny hairless legs
      4. Tx: Smoking cessation and Cilostazol (arterial vasodilator)
   2. Leriche Syndrome: Iliac occlusive disease
      1. Acute: Cool, painful, pale LE, motor weakness, paresthesia, pulse deficit
      2. Chronic: Claudication, hair loss, shiny skin, atrophy, and impotence
3. **Iatrogenic/blunt trauma**

### Presentation — 6 P's

1. Pain, Pallor, Paresthesias, Poikilothermia (cool extremity), Paralysis (late), Pulselessness

### Dx

1. 1st test: Ankle-brachial index — <0.9 is diagnostic

### Management

1. Anticoagulation — Start IV Heparin upon diagnosis
2. Thrombolysis vs surgery
3. Amputation — Nonrevascularized limb ischemia, Unsalvageable soft-tissue damage, Life-threatening infection

## Cholesterol Embolization Syndrome

1. RF: Advanced age, obesity, smoking, Hypercholesterolemia, HTN, T2DM, Cardiac Catheterization or vascular procedure
2. Presentation
   1. Derm — Livedo reticularis "painless, purple mottling that blanches"
   2. Renal — Acute or Subacute kidney injury (persists past 2 weeks vs contrast nephropathy resolves in 1 week)
   3. CNS — Stroke
   4. Ocular — Hollenhorst plaques (minute, bright, golden-yellow plaques at bifurcation of retinal arteries — indicates proximal source such as ICA)
   5. GI — Intestinal ischemia, Pancreatitis
3. Diagnosis: High Cr, Eosinophilia, Eosinophiluria; Skin or Renal biopsy: Biconvex, needle-shape clefts
4. Vs. Contrast-Induced Nephropathy: Limited to renal, Muddy-brown granular casts, Resolves 3-5 days

## Mycotic Aneurysm

1. Infection of vessel wall (bacterial, fungal, or viral); rare but severe complication

## DVT

1. Tx: IVC filter (if LE thrombus + contraindication to anticoagulation); Long term anticoagulation (if recurrent or hypercoagulable)
2. Complications: Postthrombotic Syndrome
   1. Most cases arise within 2 years
   2. Venous valve damage → Venous HTN distal to previous thrombus
   3. CF: Leg edema, fatigue, pain, superficial venous dilation, venous stasis ulcers; Worse at end of day
   4. Evaluation: Doppler US to rule out acute DVT
   5. Tx: Exercise, Compression

## Chronic Venous Insufficiency (Venous Stasis Disease)

1. MCC of LE edema
2. PP: Failure of venous valves → blood pools → increased capillary hydrostatic pressure → fluid into interstitial tissue → kidneys retain water and salt → worsen edema
3. RF: Advanced age, obesity
4. CF: Worsens throughout day, improves overnight; Pitting edema, Dermatitis, Telangiectasia, Varicose veins, Lipodermatosclerosis, Skin ulcer
5. Dx: Venous Doppler US (not routinely; confirms venous HTN and reflux)
6. Management: Compression stockings, leg elevation, exercise; Avoid prolonged standing
   - Note: Diuretics avoided due to dehydration risk

## Superficial Thrombophlebitis

1. Localized pain and swelling along the path of the vein
