# MI

## Right-Sided MI

### Hemodynamic Findings

- RV ischemia decreases RV contractility → increases RV end diastolic volume (preload) → increased backward pressure → increased RA and CVP → JVD
- Decreased blood pumped into pulmonary circulation → low PCWP → low LV preload → low CO = hypotension
- Compensatory vasoconstriction to increase SVR to maintain BP

Note: NITRATES, DIURETICS AND OPIOIDS AVOIDED because they can reduce RV Preload and worsen hypotension

### Inferior Wall MI Complications

1. Heart block: Bradycardia in the setting of Hypotension
   - RVMI INVOLVES ISCHEMIC DISRUPTION OF SA AND AV NODE
2. Papillary muscle rupture
   - Posteromedial papillary muscle supplied by RCA — may become ischemic and lead to flash mitral regurgitation with acute CHF 3-5days post MI

### Management

1. Gentle further increase in RV preload via normal saline assists failing RV in pumping blood forward
   - Provides additional hydrostatic pressure to assist blood flow through pulmonary circulation

## Left-Sided MI

1. Pulmonary edema
2. Worsens with increased preload — IV FLUIDS AVOIDED

## Management "MONA BASH-C"

1. Nitrates — caution with hypotension (RV infarct)
2. BB: Oral cardio selective — Metoprolol, Atenolol; CONTRAINDICATED in heart failure and bradycardia
3. Antiplatelet: ASA + P2Y12R-blocker (Ticagrelor, Clopidogrel, Prasugrel)
4. Anticoagulation: UFH, Enoxaparin, Bivalirudin
5. Statin: High potency — Atorvastatin, Rosuvastatin
6. Coronary reperfusion: PCI <90 min from first medical contact; Fibrinolytics (alteplase) if PCI unavailable
7. ACE-I (lower BP, decrease remodeling)

## Mechanical Complications

### RV Failure [RCA]

(See Right-Sided MI above)

### Papillary Muscle Rupture [RCA]

1. Posteromedial papillary muscle supplied by RCA — more commonly seen with inferior wall MI → flash MR with acute CHF 3-5days post MI
2. Presentation
   1. Dramatic presentation, sudden onset pulmonary edema, systolic murmur radiates to axilla
   2. +/- cardiogenic shock
   3. Silent MR murmur if MR is severe enough to cause equalization of LA and LV pressures
3. Diagnosis: TTE or TEE

### Interventricular Septum Rupture

1. RCA (basal), LAD (apical)
2. Presentation: Murmur best heard at left sternal border with thrill
   1. Decreases with inspiration (increased R-sided pressure, decreased shunting)
3. Diagnosis
   1. Septal defect on TEE
   2. O2 step up from RA to RV on Pulmonary artery catheterization
4. Biventricular failure with Pulmonary Edema
   1. Communication between L and R ventricle increases pressure and volume load on RV
   2. Leads to acute R and L sided Heart Failure
   3. Blood flow from LV→RV creates palpable thrill & harsh holosystolic murmur
5. Acute Cardiogenic Shock
   1. Pulmonary edema, hypotension, confusion due to poor organ perfusion
   2. JVD, Hepatomegaly d/t liver congestion

### LV Free Wall Rupture [LAD]

1. RF: Delayed reperfusion or no reperfusion increases risk due to more extensive myocardial necrosis
2. Presentation
   1. Sudden onset chest pain
   2. Blood fills pericardial sac → Cardiac tamponade rapidly develops → Obstructive shock with hypotension and tachycardia
   3. CO markedly drops within minutes and pulseless electrical activity (PEA) ensues

### LV Aneurysm [LAD] — Several Months Post MI

1. Persistent ST elevation and deep Q waves, LV enlargement, Dyskinetic wall motion leading to heart failure
2. Necrosed myocardium is replaced with fibrous scar — convexity of LV wall — impaired ejection fraction
3. Complications
   1. Heart failure (pulmonary edema)
   2. Ventricular arrhythmia
   3. Systemic embolization due to mural thrombus inside aneurysm
4. Dx: Echo — thinned and dyskinetic myocardial wall

### Pericarditis (Post-MI)

1. Early — Peri-infarction pericarditis
   1. Echo to establish presence and severity of pericardial effusion
   2. Pain control: Acetaminophen (NSAIDS avoided for 7days due to free wall rupture risk)
2. Post-cardiac injury Syndrome (PCIS) / Late-Dressler Syndrome
   1. Immune response to damaged mesothelial pericardial cells and blood in pericardial space
   2. Tx: ASA (avoid other NSAIDs)
   3. Avoid anticoagulation to prevent hemorrhagic pericardial effusion

## Sudden Cardiac Death

1. LV systolic dysfunction with ejection fraction <30% → Ventricular arrhythmia (VTach, VFib)
2. Medical therapy: BB (metoprolol), ACE-I/ARB, MRA (spironolactone) following MI
3. ICD: To reduce risk of SCD in persistent LVEF <30% despite medical therapy

## Carotid Artery Stenosis

1. All patients receive medical management: Antiplatelet (ASA), Statin, BP control
2. Symptomatic (TIA or stroke past 6 months): CEA for high grade (>80% stenosis)
3. Asymptomatic: Medical management for low grade (<80%); CEA for high grade
4. Notes
   1. CEA preferred over Carotid artery stent due to lower periprocedural mortality and stroke risk
   2. Bilateral Asx stenosis in the setting of Atherosclerotic RF (Prior smoking, HTN)
