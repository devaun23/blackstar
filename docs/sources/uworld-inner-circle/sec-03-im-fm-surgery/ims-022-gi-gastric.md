# Dyspepsia "burning epigastric pain"

1. Etiology
   1. 75% Functional
   2. Malignancy — gastric, esophageal
   3. Peptic ulcer, NSAIDs, H. Pylori, GERD
2. CF
   1. Burning epigastric pain
   2. +/- Nausea, Vomiting, Epigastric fullness, Heartburn
3. Workup
   1. >60yo: Upper endoscopy
   2. <60: Test and treat for H. Pylori
      1. Upper endoscopy in high-risk patients (overt GI bleeding, significant weight loss, >1 alarm symptom)
4. Alarm symptoms
   1. Progressive dysphagia
   2. Iron deficiency anemia
   3. Odynophagia — pain while swallowing
   4. Palpable mass or lymphadenopathy
   5. Persistent vomiting
   6. FH of GI malignancy

# Gastric Outlet Obstruction

1. Epigastric abdominal pain and postprandial vomiting due to mechanical obstruction
2. Causes
   1. Gastric malignancy — Pancreatic adenocarcinoma
      1. Pancreatic islet destruction from tumor invasion — Unexplained hyperglycemia
      2. Poor prognosis
   2. PUD
   3. Crohn disease
   4. Strictures secondary to caustic agent ingestion — Pyloric stricture
      1. Acid ingestion causes fibrosis 1-3 months after acute injury resolution
   5. Gastric bezoars
3. CF: "Succussion splash" — Retained gastric material >3 hrs after meal generate splash sound indicating hollow viscus filled with both gas and fluid
4. Dx: Upper endoscopy
5. Tx: Surgery

# GERD

1. Presentation
   1. "Substernal, squeezing type chest pain that radiates to neck" "not related to activity"
   2. Prolonged pain lasting >1hr, postprandial symptoms, associated heartburn/dysphagia
2. Diagnosis
   1. Definitive diagnosis of esophageal chest pain difficult, often first require cardiac testing to exclude MI
3. Initial management (<2 episodes/week)
   1. Lifestyle changes, H2-R antagonist (Famotidine, Ranitidine)
   2. Men >50 with symptoms for >5 years or cancer RF or alarm sx: Endoscopy
   3. All others: Daily PPI for 2 months
4. Laryngopharyngeal involvement (cough, hoarseness)
   1. 8-week course of PPI (Pantoprazole)
5. Note
   1. Nitroglycerin commonly relieves GERD chest pain, but not appropriate Tx for GERD
   2. Hiatal Hernia may resemble GERD due to LES incompetence

# Peptic Ulcer Disease

1. Cause: H pylori infection and NSAIDs
2. Types
   1. Duodenal ulcers improve after eating (because of release of bicarbonate into duodenum to neutralize stomach acid)
      1. Nocturnal pain due to circadian rhythm of gastric acid secretion
      2. Postprandial bloating
      3. Worse pain during fasting
   2. Gastric ulcers worsen after eating (because of release of stomach acid)
3. Complication
   1. If patient has signs of a perforation secondary to peptic ulcer disease (severe persistent pain, rebound/guarding, sepsis, etc.) --> abdominal imaging to look for peritoneal free air
4. Regimen:
   1. H. pylori related (i.e. no hx NSAID use), most appropriate to tx with Antibiotics and PPI (vs. abstinence from alcohol)
   2. Triple therapy
      1. Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin + PPI
      2. Clarithromycin + Metronidazole + PPI
   3. Quadruple therapy
      1. Bismuth + Metronidazole + Tetracycline + PPI

# Gastropathy and Acute Gastritis

Gastropathy: inflammation absent or rare

## Acute Erosive Gastropathy

1. Severe hemorrhagic lesions after exposure to irritant = Gastric mucosal erosion
   1. ASA decreases protective PG (mucus and bicarb secretion, mucosal blood flow, epithelial restitution)
   2. Cocaine causes vasoconstriction and significantly reduces gastric blood flow
   3. ASA and Cocaine together allow acid and other luminal substances (proteases, bile acids) to penetrate lamina propria by reducing mucosal defenses --> more injury to vasculature --> hemorrhage = abd pain & hematemesis

## Hypertrophic Gastropathy

1. Menetrier Disease
   1. Massive gastric folds, excessive mucus production with resultant protein loss and little or no acid production
2. Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome
   1. Gastrin secreting pancreatic tumor
   2. Multiple peptic ulcerations in stomach, duodenum and jejunum
   3. Chronic diarrhea and weight loss

## Stress-Related Mucosal Injury

Local ischemia — stress induced splanchnic vasoconstriction, increased Endothelin-1 (COX-2 is protective)
Nausea, vomiting, melena, coffee-ground hematemesis

1. Stress ulcer: Critically ill, shock, sepsis or severe trauma
2. Curling: Proximal duodenum, severe burns or trauma
3. Cushing: Esophagus, Stomach, Duodenum; increased intracranial pressure, high incidence of perforation
   1. Direct vagal stimulation leading to acid hypersecretion

# Chronic Gastritis

## H. Pylori (antrum)

1. Antibodies to H. pylori
2. Antral gastritis with increased acid production — this may give rise to PUD of duodenum or stomach
3. Poor sanitation, household crowding, birth outside of US (acquired in childhood and may persist for life)
4. Associations
   1. Mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT): Possible Lymphoma transformation
   2. Intestinal Metaplasia: Goblet cells and columnar absorptive cells

## Autoimmune (body and fundus)

1. Antibodies to Parietal cells and IF — B12 deficiency = Pernicious Anemia
2. Reduced Serum Pepsinogen I level
3. Impaired Gastric Acid secretion — Achlorhydria (causes hypergastrinemia)
4. Vs. H. pylori (not associated with achlorhydria or pernicious anemia)

## Chronic NSAIDs use

## Complications

1. PUD
   1. Most common in gastric Antrum and 1st part of duodenum (anterior duodenal wall)
   2. RF: H. pylori (Chronic Gastritis), NSAID use, Smoking (impairs mucosal blood flow and healing), high dose CST (suppress PG and impair healing)
   3. Associated: Alcoholic Cirrhosis, COPD, Chronic Renal failure & Hyperparathyroidism (HyperCa stimulates Gastrin)
   4. Complication: Perforation requires emergent surgery
   5. Presentation: Iron deficiency anemia, frank hemorrhage, perforation; worse at night, better with alkali or food
2. Mucosal atrophy and Intestinal Metaplasia
   1. Occurs in H. Pylori and Autoimmune Gastritis
   2. Presence of Goblet cells
   3. Increased risk of Gastric Adenocarcinoma
   4. Achlorhydria of gastric mucosal atrophy permits growth of bacteria that produce carcinogenic nitrosamines
3. Dysplasia

1. Pain worse after food, wt loss from lack of eating, East Asian, initial diagnostic test — EGD

# Surgery — GI

## Abdominal Compartment Syndrome

1. Increased intraabdominal pressure causes organ dysfunction
2. RF
   1. Massive fluid resuscitation (trauma, sepsis)
   2. Major intraabdominal surgery or pathology (pancreatitis)
   3. Intraabdominal fluid collection (bleeding, ascites)
3. Presentation
   1. Tensed, distended abdomen
   2. Inc CVP: Inc intraabdominal and intrathoracic pressure cause extrinsic venous compression
   3. Dec Venous Return: Decreased Preload
      1. Note: Typically CVP and Preload follow same direction; however here CVP is elevated due to external compression (rather than increased intravenous volume) and Preload is decreased due to Vena Cava compression
   4. Dec CO, Hypotension and Compensatory Tachycardia
   5. Dec Ventricular filling: Diaphragmatic elevation compresses heart
4. Tx
   1. Avoid fluid over-resuscitation
   2. Dec Intraabdominal volume: NG tube, Drain fluid
   3. Inc Abdominal wall compliance: Sedation, Paralysis
   4. Definitive: Surgical decompression

## Gastrectomy

1. Complication: Dumping Syndrome
   1. Rapid emptying of hypertonic gastric contents
   2. GI (nausea, diarrhea, abdominal cramps)
   3. Vasomotor (palpitations, diaphoresis) symptoms
   4. Controlled with dietary medication and diminish over time

## Nissen Fundoplication

1. Indicated for GERD treatment
2. Complications
   1. Dysphagia: LES tightening leads to peristalsis disruption
      1. Symptoms within 12 weeks of surgery
      2. Dx clinically and self resolves
   2. Gas-bloat syndrome: LES tightening leads to gastric air trapping
      1. Bloating and inability to belch
      2. Dx clinically and resolves with conservative management (Simethicone, carbonated beverage avoidance)
   3. Gastroparesis: inadvertent vagal nerve injury
      1. Bloating, early satiety, postprandial emesis "partially digested food", food aversion, weight loss
      2. Dx: Gastric scintigraphy + EGD to rule out obstruction
      3. Management: Small, low-fat, low-fiber meals + promotility (Metoclopramide)

## Roux-en-Y gastric bypass

1. Anastomotic leak: breakdown of either gastrojejunal or jejunojejunal anastomosis causing leak into peritoneum
2. 1st week of surgery, fever, abdominal pain, tachypnea, tachycardia
3. Complication
   1. Fat soluble vitamin deficiency (Vitamins A, D, E, K)
   2. Vitamin C deficiency (~3months) = Scurvy
      1. Poor post-op diet
      2. Ecchymosis, petechiae, poor wound healing, perifollicular hemorrhage, coiled hairs
      3. Mild anemia due to blood vessel fragility and bleeding
      4. Normal platelet count, PT, PTT
4. Management: Abdominal CT with oral contrast

## Refeeding Syndrome

1. Alcoholic
   1. Scenario: Chronically malnourished alcoholic becomes hyporeflexic and weak after dextrose fluid
   2. Body utilizing anaerobic glycolysis and lactic acidosis — Reintroduction of carbs — insulin secretion — intracellular shift of PO4, K, Mg — increases PO4 utilization during glycolysis — PO4 depletion and failure of cellular energy metabolism
   3. Causes muscle weakness, hyporeflexia, rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis, arrhythmias, CHF
   4. Dx: Low serum Phosphorus levels
2. Chronic Malnourishment
   1. Nutritional replenishment in chronically malnourished pt causes Hypophosphatemia, Muscle weakness, Arrhythmia, CHF

## Retroperitoneal Hematoma (also a complication of cardiac catheterization)

1. Due to Warfarin therapy
   1. Back pain and signs of hemodynamic compromise (weakness, dizziness, anemia, tachycardia)

## Stress Gastric Ulcer

1. RF: shock, sepsis, coagulopathy, mechanical ventilation, brain injury, burns, high-dose CST
2. Painless GI bleeding (worsening anemia with positive stool occult blood)
3. Tx: PPIs and close monitoring

## Gastric Adenocarcinoma

1. Tumor staging: CT scan

## Obesity

1. Bariatric Surgery Indication
   1. BMI >40
   2. BMI >35 and comorbidity (T2DM, HTN, OSA)

# Trauma — Gastric

## Gastric Perforation

1. H. pylori, NSAIDs, burns, head injury, trauma, cancer
2. Smoking and alcohol slow ulcer healing
3. Perforated peptic ulcer
4. Presentation
   1. Ulcer burrows through gastric mucosa and gastric acid leakage into abdomen causes peritonitis
   2. Posterior stomach --> ulcer may cause pancreatitis "fries it"
   3. Acute onset, severe pain, systemic inflammatory response, right shoulder pain due to phrenic n. irritation
   4. Peritonitis: Guarding, rebound tenderness, abdominal rigidity
   5. Initial test: Upright CXR shows air under diaphragm "pneumoperitoneum/subdiaphragmatic free air"
      1. Air under diaphragm = bowel perforation
   6. Accurate: CT
   7. Tx:
      1. Emergent surgical exploration
      2. NPO, NG tube
      3. IV fluids, BS Abx, PPI

## Hiatal Hernia

1. Gastric herniation into the thoracic cavity
   1. N/V, Postprandial fullness, Dysphagia, Epigastric/chest pain
   2. XR: retrocardiac opacity within thoracic cavity
   3. Management: Asx don't require further workup
      1. However GERD should be medically managed
      2. GERD non responsive to PPI — Nissen fundoplication

# DIARRHEA

## Acute

1. Enterotoxin
   1. Clostridium Difficile
      1. Dx — Stool testing
      2. Sx: watery stool, leukocytosis, low-grade fever
   2. Inflammatory: C. Jejuni, Salmonella, Shigella

## Chronic

1. Secretory: during fasting and sleeping, LOW OSMOTIC GAP
   1. Toxins: V. cholerae
   2. Hormones: VIPomas
   3. Congenital: CF
   4. Unabsorbed bile acids:
      1. Postsurgical changes, bowel resection
      2. Cholecystectomy (ileocecal resection dec ability of intestines to absorb Na against gradient)
   5. Microscopic Colitis
      1. Chronic, watery diarrhea, normal appearing colonic mucosa, biopsy reveals "mononuclear infiltrate within lamina propria and subepithelial COLLAGEN BAND"
      2. RF: F >60yo, concurrent autoimmune conditions, smoking, NSAIDs ("arthritis pt"), PPI, SSRI
2. Osmotic/Malabsorption: high SOG (>125 mOsm/kg)
   1. Celiac Sprue
   2. Tropical Sprue
      1. Patients who have lived in a tropical area >1 month
   3. Whipple's Disease (Tropheryma whippelii)
      1. Multi-systemic illness
      2. White men in 40s-60s with weight loss
      3. GI: Abdominal pain, diarrhea, malabsorption with distention, flatulence, steatorrhea
      4. Migratory polyarthropathy, chronic cough, myocardial or valvular defect leading to CHF or valvular regurgitation
      5. Skin pigmentation, lymphadenopathy
      6. Biopsy: PAS-positive material in lamina propria of small intestine
   4. Lactose Intolerance
3. Inflammatory
   - *Stool test for ova and parasites: Giardia intestinalis and Cryptosporidium species

# GI BLEEDS

## Upper GI

1. PUD
2. Erosive Esophagitis
3. Esophageal varices
4. AVM
5. Mallory-Weiss Syndrome
   1. Mucosal tear, GE junction
   2. Hematemesis, Odynophagia
   3. Alcoholics
6. Cancers of upper GI tract
7. Note: BUN abnormality
   1. Bleeds from upper GI present with inc BUN and inc BUN/creatinine ratio
   2. inc BUN possibly due to increased urea production from intestinal breakdown of Hemoglobin and inc reabsorption in proximal tubule due to associated hypovolemia

## Lower GI

1. Diverticulosis
2. Hemorrhoids
3. Colorectal Cancer
4. AVMs
5. Intestinal Ischemia

## Management

1. Initial: 2 peripheral IV lines, IV fluids (normal saline)
   1. HemUS: Blood transfusion
   2. HemS: Blood transfusion Hb <7g/dL
2. 2nd: Imaging
   1. Upper GI bleed --> Endoscopy
   2. Lower GI bleed --> Colonoscopy
