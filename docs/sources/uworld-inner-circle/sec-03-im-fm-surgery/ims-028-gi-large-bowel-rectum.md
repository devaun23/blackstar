# IBD

## Skin complication: Erythema nodosum

1. Tender, anterior shin, delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction to antigen exposure
2. Bx: septal panniculitis without vasculitis
3. Presence correlates with degree of IBD
4. EN also seen in Sarcoidosis, Hodgkin lymphoma, Fungal, Viral Mono

## Chronic IBD: Toxic Megacolon complication

1. Imaging: CT Abdomen
2. Tx: IV glucocorticoids

## Crohn's (Non-bloody diarrhea, Mouth --> Anus)

1. Microscopy: Noncaseating granulomas
2. Gross findings: transmural inflammation, linear mucosal ulcerations, cobblestoning, creeping fat
3. CF
   1. 50% patients present with palpable abdominal mass in RLQ
   2. Intestinal complication: Fistulae, abscesses, strictures (bowel obstruction)
   3. Malnutrition (multiple small bowel resections, weight loss) --> Thiamine deficiency --> Wernicke encephalopathy
4. Barium Xray: "String sign"
5. Associated with disease progression
   1. Cigarette Smoking
   2. Young age at diagnosis (<30yo), Strictures, Fistulization, Prior Surgery
6. Management
   1. Mild: <4 bowel mvmts a day, intermittent hematochezia, normal inflammatory markers, no anemia, "small, shallow ulcers, abdominal pain improves after defecation"
   2. Next management step — Mesalamine enema or Sulfasalazine (5-aminosalicyclic acid agent)
   3. Enemas used for disease limited to rectosigmoid area, oral therapy used for extensive disease
7. Labs
   1. Similar to other chronic inflammatory diseases
      1. Anemia
      2. Leukocytosis
      3. Reactive thrombocytosis
      4. Elevated inflammatory markers: ESR
      5. *Note: symptoms may appear similar to Celiac disease (chronic diarrhea, abdominal pain, weight loss, anemia from malabsorption) but Celiac lacks oral involvement or elevation of inflammatory markers
8. Tx:
   1. Acute exacerbation — Oral Steroids: Prednisone or Budesonide faster onset than other meds
   2. Inducing Remission
      1. 1st: Infliximab + Azathioprine, Methotrexate, 6-mercaptopurine
      2. *Note: Oral CST initially given in an acute exacerbation, above medications have longer onset time
9. Differentiating from UC
   1. Crohns — Multiple GI portions involved, Normal rectal mucosa, Fistula, Noncaseating granulomas

## UC (Bloody diarrhea)

1. Microscopy: No granulomas
2. Gross finding: mucosal and submucosal inflammation "small shallow ulcers; friable mucosa of entire colon", pseudopolyps
3. Intestinal complication: Toxic megacolon
4. Colon Cancer Screening: Begin 8 years post disease onset, Repeat 1-3years
5. Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis: pruritus, cholestasis can cause cirrhosis

# Diverticula

1. Diverticula are outpouchings of the colonic wall that form at points of weakness
2. Diverticulosis
   1. RF: heavy meat consumption, ASA/NSAIDs, Obesity, Smoking
   2. Tx: high fiber intake in otherwise stable patient
3. Diverticular spasm
4. Diverticular Hemorrhage: Painless hematochezia
   1. MCC: Diverticulosis
   2. Irritation due to bleeding can cause urge to defecate
   3. Large-volume rectal bleeding --> hemodynamic instability, lightheadedness
   4. Dx: Colonoscopy
5. Diverticulitis
   1. Colonoscopy contraindicated due to inflamed diverticula
   2. RF: Chronic constipation
   3. CF: LLQ pain, tenderness, bloody stools, fever

# Chronic Mesenteric Ischemia

1. Etiology: Atherosclerotic ischemic colitis: watershed areas
2. CF: Crampy, postprandial epigastric pain, food aversion, weight loss
   1. Blood shunted away from intestine to stomach shortly after eating
   2. Celiac or superior mesenteric artery may be narrowed and unable to dilate
3. Dx:
   1. Signs of malnutrition, Abdominal bruit
   2. CT Angiography, Doppler US
4. Management:
   1. Risk reduction: tobacco reduction
   2. Nutritional support
   3. Endovascular or open surgical revascularization

# Ischemic Colitis

1. Atherosclerotic ischemic colitis
2. CF: Bloody diarrhea, weight loss

# Clostridioides difficile Colitis

1. PP: Hardy antibiotic-resistant spores ingested and convert to functional bacilli in colon
   1. Exotoxins (Enterotoxin A, Cytotoxin B) penetrate colonic epithelial cells --> Apoptosis and loss of tight junctions
2. RF
   1. Recent Abx use — Disrupt normal colonic flora (Bacteroides)
   2. Advanced age >65yo
   3. Gastric acid suppression (PPI, H2 blocker)
   4. Underlying IBD
   5. Chemotherapy
3. CF
   1. Profuse watery diarrhea
   2. Leukocytosis
   3. Fulminant colitis or toxic megacolon
4. Dx
   1. Stool PCR for C difficile genes
   2. Stool Enzyme immunoassay for C difficile toxin and glutamate dehydrogenase antigen
5. Infection control
   1. Hand hygiene with soap and water
   2. Contact isolation
   3. Bleach (sporicidal)
6. Tx
   1. Fidaxomicin
   2. Fulminant: Vancomycin
7. Surgical intervention: Toxic Megacolon
   1. Diffuse dilation within normal anatomy (vs single dilated loop unlike cecal volvulus)
   2. Toxic: fever, altered sensorium, bloody diarrhea
   3. Peritonitis (absent bowel sounds, tender to palpation and release) --> Laparotomy
   4. Increased serum lactate

# Volvulus

## Cecal Volvulus

1. Cecum and ascending colon twist on mesentery — forming closed loop obstruction
   1. Lumen of obstructed bowel expands due to gas formation from intraluminal bacteria
   2. Slow progressive abdominal distention with nausea and vomiting
2. Younger, prior-self resolving episodes due to congenital mobile cecum
3. Distended and tender abdomen (-) rigidity, XR — large, dilated loop of colon
4. Dx: Abdominopelvic CT Scan
5. Tx: Emergency laparotomy and resection of volvulized segment

## Sigmoid Volvulus

1. RF
   1. Chronic constipation: chronic fecal overloading leads to dilation and elongation of sigmoid colon
   2. Colon dysmotility (neurological, diabetic enteropathy)
2. Tx: Endoscopic detorsion with flexible sigmoidoscopy; Emergency sigmoid colectomy if perforation/peritonitis

# Ileocolic intussusception (prolapse of ileum into colon)

1. Self-resolving episodes of abdominal pain and vomiting
2. CXR: multiple, dilated small-bowel loops with air-fluid levels

# Paralytic Ileus

1. Generalized, uniform distention, obstipation, hypoactive/absent bowel sounds, nausea, vomiting
2. RF: Electrolyte abnormalities (low Phosphate, low K) can cause muscle dysfunction, Retroperitoneal/abdominal hemorrhage
3. Prolonged Postoperative Ileus
   1. Delayed return of bowel function >72hrs after surgery
   2. Abdominal distention (temporary bowel paralysis, gastric secretion backup and gas)
   3. Imaging: Gas filled loops of SI and LI without transition point (vs. SBO)
   4. Management: Conservative, bowel rest, serial examinations

# Colon Cancer

1. Bug associations — Screen with colonoscopy
   1. S. bovis
   2. Clostridium septicum
2. RF:
   1. Hx of adenomatous polyps or colon cancer
   2. Inherited colon cancer syndromes: FAP, Lynch
   3. UC
   4. Diabetes/Obesity
   5. Prior abdominopelvic radiation — prior screening at age 30-40
3. Protective:
   1. High-fiber diet
   2. ASA/NSAID use
4. Right-sided Adenocarcinoma
   1. Obstructive symptoms uncommon due to larger lumen and liquid stools
   2. Occult bleeding, Iron-deficiency anemia
5. Left-sided Adenocarcinoma (Sigmoid Colon)
   1. Unexpected weight loss; Disrupted stool flow leading to crampy/colicky pain
   2. Red/maroon Hematochezia common due to proximity to anal verge
6. Rectal Tumors
   1. Hematochezia or frank red blood; narrowed stools, tenesmus, mass sensation in rectum
7. Special Groups Screening:
   1. FAP: Annual colonoscopies in younger patients
   2. Lynch Syndrome (HNPCC): q1-2yr colonoscopy
   3. IBD: Colonoscopy 8yrs post diagnosis, q1-2yr after that
   4. FH (1st degree relative <60): Colonoscopy at 40 or 10yo prior to FH diagnosis, q3-5yr after that

# Trauma

## Acute Mesenteric Ischemia

1. Pain out of proportion, soft abdomen (- guarding, - rebound tenderness)
2. Pain after eating due to increased oxygen requirement of bowel post muscular contraction + bloody diarrhea
3. Cardiac embolic events
   1. #1 RF: Atrial fibrillation
   2. Valvular disease: Infective Endocarditis
4. Initial: CT abdomen showing air in bowel wall
5. Accurate: Angiography to avoid perforation
6. Surgery: Emergent laparotomy to remove necrotic bowel

## Ischemic Colitis post-surgery

1. Complication of vascular surgery, older patients have extensive atherosclerosis
2. Lower GI bleed after episode of hypotension with fever, nausea, elevated lactic acid
   1. Watershed areas
      1. Impaired blood flow between SMA and IMA — Splenic Flexure
      2. Impaired blood flow between SMA and Sigmoid artery — Rectosigmoid junction
   2. Moderate pain, bloody diarrhea
   3. CT: thickening of bowel wall
   4. Colonoscopy: cyanotic mucosa and hemorrhagic ulcerations, sharp transition from affected to unaffected region
   5. Tx: IV fluids, Bowel rest, Abx (uncomplicated)

## Abdominal Abscess

1. Tx: CT scan, incision and drainage

## Acute Diverticulitis

1. Highly associated with constipation
2. Contraindicated: Endoscopy or barium enema and colonoscopy due to perforation risk
3. Complication: Abscess formation
4. Dx: CT with contrast (Oral and IV)
   1. Increased inflammation in pericolic fat, diverticula, bowel wall thickening, soft tissue masses and pericolic fluid suggesting abscess
5. Tx: Medical if 1st, bowel resection if recurrent
   1. Uncomplicated: Bowel rest, Abx, Observation
   2. Complicated (abscess)
      1. <3cm: IV Abx, Observation
      2. >3cm: CT-guided Percutaneous Drainage

## Colovesical Fistula

1. RF: Diverticular disease, Crohn Disease, Malignancy
   1. Extension of ruptured diverticulum or erosion of diverticular abscess into bladder
2. Presentation: Pneumaturia "air bubbles while urinating", Fecaluria, Recurrent UTI
3. Dx: Abdominal CT with oral or rectal contrast (NOT IV)
4. Colonoscopy to exclude malignancy

## Free perforation of GIT

1. Classic sequence in ongoing inflammation (diverticulitis):
   1. Sudden, severe pain (perforation + vomiting, lightheadedness)
   2. Followed by temporary relief (decompression of inflamed organ)
   3. And then generalized, constant pain (peritonitis + sepsis/septic shock)
2. Free perforation leads to colonic contents spilling into peritoneal cavity
3. Imaging: intraperitoneal free air
4. Tx: Surgical emergency with abdominal washout and resection of ruptured bowel segment
5. Vs. Diverticular microperforation
   1. Contained, responds to medical management with Abx and Bowel rest

# Fecal Incontinence

1. Uncontrolled passage of fecal material for 1 month in >3yo
2. Dx:
   1. Hx + flexible sigmoidoscopy or anoscopy
   2. Accurate: anorectal manometry
   3. Anatomic injury hx: endorectal manometry
3. Tx:
   1. Medical: fiber
   2. Biofeedback: control exercises and strengthening exercises, dextranomer/hyaluronic acid
   3. Colorectal surgery

# RECTUM

## Anal and Perianal Masses

### Hemorrhoids

1. Internal Hemorrhoid
   1. Intermittent itching, painless bleeding, leakage of stool
   2. DRE or Anoscopy (purplish mucosal bulges)
      1. Anoscopy: Noninvasive method preferred in patients lacking CF of a serious cause
   3. Initial Management (for uncomplicated cases)
      1. Dietary: inc fluid intake, inc fiber intake, reduced fat intake, reduce alcohol
      2. Behavioral: Limit toilet sitting time, limit defecation once a day, avoid straining
      3. Topical agents: Benzocaine, Witch hazel, Hydrocortisone
2. External Hemorrhoid Thrombosis
   1. Excruciating anorectal pain exacerbated by sitting, bluish-purple bulge at anal verge
   2. Initial management: Sitz baths, Stool softeners, Topical anesthetics

### Anogenital Wart

1. Pink/flesh colored papule, plaques, cauliflower-shaped mass; mild itching, bleeding
2. Chronic

### Anorectal Cancer

1. Squamous Cell Carcinoma, bleeding, pain
2. Ulcerating, enlarging mass

### Skin Tags

1. Small, flesh-colored papule
2. External terminus of anal fissure (sentinel tag)

### Perianal Abscess (superficial infection)

1. Fluctuant mass/swelling with erythema tender red bump "pimple" like
2. Fever, gradual onset

### Rectal Prolapse

1. Erythematous mass with concentric rings that occurs with Valsalva
2. Mucus drainage, mild abdominal pain, mass sensation

## Anal Fissures

1. Pain and rectal bleeding on defecation; posterior anal midline; chronic fissure may have skin tag at distal end
2. Causes: Local trauma (constipation, prolonged diarrhea, sex), IBD (Crohn), Malignancy
3. Tx: Fiber and fluid, Sitz baths, Stool softeners, topical anesthetics and vasodilators (nifedipine, nitroglycerin)

## Anorectal fistula (fistula in ano)

1. Causes
   1. Perianal abscess rupture
   2. Crohn disease
   3. Malignancy, radiation proctitis
   4. Infection: Lymphogranuloma venereum (Chlamydia types L1-L3)
2. CF
   1. Perirectal pain + discharge
   2. Inflammatory papule/pustule
   3. Palpable fistula tract
3. Management
   1. Assess extent of fistula (Endosonography, Fistulogram, MRI)
   2. Surgery (fistulotomy)

## Radiation Proctitis

1. Acute: Direct mucosal damage, Diarrhea, mucus discharge, tenesmus
   1. Management: Antidiarrheals (loperamide), Butyrate enemas
2. Chronic: Progressive rectal fibrosis from pelvic radiation therapy
   1. Obliterative endarteritis and submucosal fibrosis — anorectal stricture formation
   2. Management: Endoscopic thermal coagulation, Sucralfate or Glucocorticoid enemas

## Gonococcal Proctitis

1. Receptive anal intercourse; direct spread from the vagina
2. CF: Mucopurulent anal discharge, occasional rectal bleeding, Tenesmus, Pruritus, Constipation, rectal pain
3. Dx: NAAT of rectal swab
4. Tx: Ceftriaxone + Doxycycline (Chlamydia coverage)

## Perirectal abscess

1. Crohn's disease (Anal fistula and Perirectal abscess)
2. Deep infection that tracks up along the rectum into the pelvis
3. Mucopurulent anal discharge
4. Rectum spared = lack tenesmus

## Proctalgia fugax (Diagnosis of exclusion)

1. PP: Spastic contraction of the anal sphincter; Pudendal nerve compression
2. CF: Recurrent rectal pain unrelated to defecation, lasts seconds to minutes
3. Evaluation: Normal PE and Labs
4. Management: Reassurance; Nitroglycerin cream +/- biofeedback therapy for refractory symptoms

## Rectal Prolapse

1. Intermittent protrusion of rectum through the anal orifice; F >40yo
2. RF: Multiparity, Vaginal delivery, Pelvic surgery, Pelvic floor dysfunction, Chronic constipation or straining

## Rectal Adenocarcinoma

1. Presentation: "Blood in stool, BRB on toilet paper, fhx of colon cancer, sigmoidoscopy shows fungating mass upper rectum"
2. Most appropriate prior to surgical treatment — Colonoscopy of entire colon
   1. Sigmoidoscopy does not visualize R colon
3. Staging
   1. Tumor markers: CEA
   2. Imaging: CT chest, abdomen, pelvis
   3. Endoscopy/direct visualization: Colonoscopy
