# Acute Pancreatitis

1. Cause
   1. Note: Two most common causes of pancreatitis are alcohol and gallstones
      1. If patient denies alcohol use then next best step is right upper quadrant ultrasound to look for gallstones (HIGH YIELD!!!)
      2. If RUQ U/S is negative, next set of lab tests would be calcium level and lipid studies (looking for hypertriglyceridemia)
   2. I GET SMASHED:
      1. Idiopathic: Post-ERCP, Ischemic/atheroembolic (peripheral vascular disease)
      2. Gallstones*
         1. ALT >150 has 95% ppv for diagnosing gallstone pancreatitis
         2. Elevated Liver enzymes, Amylase, Lipase
         3. Early Cholecystectomy once current episode is resolved to prevent gallstone pancreatitis
      3. EtOH*
      4. Trauma (motor vehicle accident, hitting stomach on bike handles, etc.)
         1. Retroperitoneal hemorrhage = flank bruising
         2. Cullen sign (bruising around umbilicus) = hemorrhagic pancreatitis (or ruptured aortic aneurysm)
         3. Radiates to back with N/V (- painless obstructive jaundice)
         4. Serum amylase and lipase
         5. Tx: Aggressive IV fluids and NPO until symptoms resolve
         6. Complication: 6-8wks post --> Pseudocyst
      5. Steroids
      6. Mumps (parotitis, pancreatitis and orchitis)
      7. Autoimmune
      8. Scorpion sting
      9. Hypertriglyceridemia/Hypercalcemia (TG >1000 mg/dL)
      10. ERCP
      11. Drugs: Valproic acid, diuretics, HIV drugs
2. CF
   1. Diagnosis requires 2 of: Acute epigastric pain radiating to back, Elevated Amylase/lipase >3x normal, Contrast CT or US showing focal or diffuse enlargement
3. Evaluation — Serum amylase and lipase vs. CT abdomen (do laboratory testing before imaging)
   1. Imaging not required if typical sx and elevated enzymes
      1. Lipase rises 4-8hrs, remains 8-14days = more reliable
      2. Amylase rises 6-12hrs, remains 3-5days
   2. CT: Diffuse enlargement of pancreas
   3. ERCP
      1. ALT >150 U/L --> biliary (gallstone) pancreatitis
      2. >1 episode of acute pancreatitis of unknown cause
4. Management
   1. Uncorrectable cause — Hypotension, ischemia, viruses, atheroembolism
      1. IV fluids and supportive care only
      2. Most acute pancreatitis cases self-resolve in 4-7 days with conservative management
5. Complications
   1. Evaluation: CT abdomen is gold standard
   2. Abscess or Hemorrhage: Present with significant pain and discomfort
6. Complication: Severe Acute Pancreatitis
   1. Failure of >1 organ system lasting >48hrs
   2. Evidence of SIRS (Elevated Blood Urea Nitrogen or hematocrit) and Intravascular volume depletion --> inc vascular permeability
   3. CXR: Pulmonary infiltrates, Pleural effusion (third spacing of fluid)
   4. Abdominal CT: Severe pancreatic necrosis
7. Prognosis
   1. Poor Prognosis: Hypocalcemia
      1. Hypocalcemia in the setting of acute pancreatitis is indicative of a high degree of fat saponification
      2. Fat necrosis of peripancreatic fat indicates significant disease burden and hypocalcemia can lead to additional morbidity

# Triglyceride-induced Pancreatitis

1. TG >1000 mg/dL
2. CF
   1. Diagnosis requires 2: Acute epigastric pain radiating to back, Elevated Amylase/lipase >3x normal, Contrast CT or US showing focal or diffuse enlargement
   2. +/- Fever, nausea, vomiting
   3. Eruptive xanthomas
   4. Note: Acute pancreatitis in pregnancy requires hospitalization due to high maternal/fetal morbidity
3. Evaluation
   1. Identify cause via lipid panel (especially pregnant patients who are prone to hypertriglyceridemia in 3rd TM)
4. Management
   1. IV fluid, pain control
   2. TG >= 500: Insulin infusion (limits fatty-acid release from adipocytes)
   3. TG >= 1000 or severe pancreatitis (lactic acidosis, hypocalcemia): Apheresis (remove TG-rich plasma/therapeutic plasma exchange)
5. Complication: Infected necrosis
   1. Hemodynamic decompensation, persistent fevers
   2. CT used to evaluate

# Chronic Pancreatitis

1. "Serosanguinous fluid, postprandial epigastric pain, shifting dullness, alcohol abuse hx, Elevated Amylase, Elevated total protein, Low SAAG" — Pancreatitis vs Alcoholic cirrhosis
2. Recurrent acute pancreatitis --> chronic pancreatitis
   1. Exocrine insufficiency
      1. Lack of digestive enzymes — malnutrition --> cachexia, muscle wasting, weight loss, fat soluble vitamin deficiencies, foul smelling stools +/- steatorrhea
      2. Management: Oral pancreatic enzymes, fat soluble vitamins replacement
   2. Endocrine insufficiency
      1. dec Glucagon, Insulin production --> Diabetes Mellitus with normal response to peripheral Insulin
      2. Management: Insulin
3. Complication: Pancreaticopleural fistula (PPF) with amylase-rich exudative pleural effusion
   1. "Large L-sided pleural effusion, hx of heavy alcohol use, recurrent epigastric abd pain"
      1. Fistula allows pancreatic fluid to leak into pleural space — pleural effusion — exudative and amylase-rich
      2. Cough, dyspnea, dysphagia, chest pain or asx
   2. Management
      1. PPF recur after thoracentesis
      2. Bowel rest to avoid pancreatic stimulation
      3. ERCP with sphincterotomy +/- stent to promote drainage through ampulla of Vater instead of fistula

# Pancreatic pseudocyst

1. RF: Pancreatitis (MC RF for Pancreatitis are gallstones and drinking a lot of alcohol)
2. Collection of leaked pancreatic fluids that form next to the pancreas during pancreatitis
   1. Mature walled-off pancreatic fluid collections surrounded by thick fibrous capsule
   2. May leak amylase-rich fluid into circulation and increase serum amylase
3. CF: Stomach pain, vomiting, and other symptoms of pancreatitis
4. Tx:
   1. Asymptomatic — Expectant (symptomatic therapy, NPO)
   2. Symptomatic — Endoscopic drainage, Drainage with ERCP
5. Complication: spontaneous infection, duodenal or biliary obstruction, pseudoaneurysm (digestion of adjacent vessels), pancreatic ascites, pleural effusion

# Pancreatic Cancer

1. Pancreatic Adenocarcinoma
2. RF: Smoking, Alcohol
   1. Hereditary: 1st degree relative with pancreatic cancer, Hereditary pancreatitis, Germline mutation: BRCA1, BRCA2, Peutz-Jeghers syndrome
   2. Environmental: #1 Cigarette smoking, Obesity, low physical activity, Nonhereditary chronic pancreatitis
3. CF
   1. Gnawing abdominal pain, worse at night "pain interferes with sleep"
   2. Gastric outlet obstruction
      1. Intractable Nausea, Vomiting (HypoK, HypoCl Metabolic Alkalosis), Early satiety
      2. CF: "Succussion splash"
      3. Poor prognosis
   3. Location:
      1. Head of pancreas (=jaundice, Ultrasound) "Painless with jaundice"
         1. Cholestatic LFT pattern (Alk phos and Direct hyperbilirubinemia >> ALT/AST elevation)
         2. Painless Jaundice due to Common bile duct obstruction = yellow skin, pruritus, pale stools, dark urine
            1. Leads to backup of bile with subsequent intra and extra hepatic biliary duct dilation
         3. Double duct sign: tumor compresses Common bile duct and Pancreatic duct
         4. Nontender distended Gallbladder at the right costal margin (Courvoisier sign)
         5. Steatorrhea due to inability to secrete fat-digesting enzymes or blockage in main pancreatic duct
      2. Body/Tail (= no jaundice, CT with contrast) "Painful without jaundice"
         1. Sudden onset Diabetes Mellitus
         2. Abdominal pain without jaundice (do not obstruct biliary system)
      3. Note: CT Abdomen used especially if pancreas is not properly visualized by Abdominal ultrasound

Note: Painless jaundice and weight loss is concerning for malignancy (Cholangiocarcinoma, Pancreatic CA, Hepatocellular CA) and should be evaluated with a CT Abdomen

# Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome (Pancreas or duodenum)

1. CF
   1. Multiple peptic ulcerations in stomach, duodenum and jejunum
   2. Chronic diarrhea and weight loss
   3. Malabsorption due to excessive stomach acid production inactivating pancreatic enzymes
   4. MEN1 association
2. Gastrin secreting pancreatic tumor — Gastrinoma
   1. Elevated Gastrin in presence of acidic gastric pH: Excessive Gastrin — Parietal cell hyperplasia — Excessive Gastric acid
      1. Dx: 1st — Serum Gastrin concentration; 2nd — If Gastrin abnormal, perform Secretin stimulation test
      2. Tx: Initially — PPI
   2. Endoscopy: ulcers, primary duodenal gastrinoma
   3. After gastrinoma confirmation, screen for MEN1 with assays for PTH, Ionized Ca, Prolactin

# Trauma — Pancreatic duct injury (Blunt Abdominal Trauma)

1. Upper abdomen (fixed, retroperitoneal pancreas) compressed against vertebral column
2. Difficult to diagnose initially — PE is nonspecific, enzyme levels don't rise and CT abdomen sensitivity is variable
3. Persistent abdominal discomfort/nausea, increasing amylase, peripancreatic fluid "free fluid in upper abdomen"
