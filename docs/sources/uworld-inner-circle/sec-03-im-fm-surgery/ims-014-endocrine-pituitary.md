# Acromegaly

1. PP: Pituitary somatotroph adenoma
   1. GH stimulates hepatic IGF-1
   2. IGF-1 levels consistently high during the day vs. GH levels that may vary
2. CF:
   1. Coarse facial features, uncontrolled hypertension, enlarged hands, Carpal tunnel syndrome
   2. Enlarging adenoma may suppress other hormones (erectile dysfunction and low testosterone)
   3. Joint involvement of axial and appendicular skeletons
3. Increased Hyperglycemia — Polyuria
4. Increased Metabolic rate — Hyperhidrosis
5. GH stimulates Insulin-like GF-1 — Overgrowth tissues, bone, cartilage, visceral organs
6. Spinal involvement — Kyphoscoliosis
7. Xray:
   1. Initially — Joint space widening
      1. Excess GH causes hyperplasia of articular chondrocytes and synovial hypertrophy
   2. Later — Osteoarthritis resemblance "osteophytes"
      1. Cartilage degeneration
8. Evaluation:
   1. 1st: Serum Insulin-like growth factor 1
   2. 2nd: MRI head to locate pituitary adenoma
9. Tx:
   1. Initial: Transsphenoidal resection of the pituitary adenoma — minimize arthropathy in hypertrophic stages but cannot reverse degenerative changes
   2. Unresectable tumor: Medication
      1. Somatostatin analogue: Octreotide
      2. GH Receptor antagonist: Pegvisomant

# Hyperprolactinoma

1. Causes
   1. Elevated Prolactin levels suppress GnRH, LH, Estradiol
      1. Premenopausal Female: oligo-amenorrhea
         1. Tx: Bromocriptine or Cabergoline
      2. Postmenopausal and Males: Mass effects (symptoms present at larger size)
   2. TRH related
      1. Prolactin also upregulated by TRH — Galactorrhea and Abnormal uterine bleeding in Hypothyroid pt with High TRH
   3. Antidopaminergic drugs — Antipsychotics
      1. Prolactin upregulated but TRH (and TSH) is normal
2. Evaluation
   1. Rule out medication causes
   2. MRI
   3. *Note: FSH and LH levels rarely evaluated due to GnRH suppression and fluctuations during menses
3. Tx
   1. Desire fertility: Cabergoline (dopamine agonist), Bromocriptine
   2. Severe cases: transsphenoidal resection

# TSH-producing Pituitary Adenoma

1. Secondary hyperthyroidism
2. Central Hyperthyroidism — Elevated TSH, T4, T3
3. Mass effects — headache, visual symptoms, impaired production of other pituitary hormones

# Hypogonadism

## Male Primary Hypogonadism

1. inc LH stimulates aromatization of estrogen precursors (androstenedione) from the adrenal gland --> Gynecomastia
2. Causes
   1. Klinefelter syndrome
      1. Testicular fibrosis: small, firm testes, dec Testosterone, inc LH
      2. Complication: Breast Cancer
         1. dec Testosterone production leads to compensatory inc in gonadotropins --> inc Aromatase --> inc conversion of Testosterone to Estradiol
         2. inc Estrogen/Androgen ratio = Gynecomastia in the breast and increased risk for BC
      3. CF
         1. Firm, subareolar mass
         2. Skin & nipple dimpling, induration, ulceration
         3. Often detected at advanced stage
      4. Dx
         1. Mammogram
         2. Biopsy: invasive ductal carcinoma (hormone receptor-positive) most common

## Male Secondary Hypogonadism

1. dec/Normal LH
2. Causes
   1. Pituitary tumors, Hyperprolactinoma (Prolactin suppresses GnRH)
      1. inc inc Prolactin >200ng/mL
   2. Nonfunctioning pituitary adenoma — gonadotrophs
      1. Generally secrete alpha-subunit that produces minimal hormones
      2. Symptoms appear due to mass effect
      3. Low pituitary hormones due to compression of neighboring cells — dec TSH
      4. Mild-moderate inc Prolactin
   3. Meds
      1. Opioids: Suppress GnRH and LH secretion --> Reduced Leydig cell testosterone synthesis
      2. Glucocorticoids
      3. Exogenous Androgens (withdrawal phase)
   4. Infiltrative disease: Hemochromatosis
      1. Elevated transferrin saturation
      2. <50yo, abnormal skin pigmentation, DM, elevated liver enzymes, arthropathy
   5. Chronic/severe illness
   6. Eating disorders (Anorexia Nervosa), Severe weight loss
      1. dec GnRH
   7. Note: 2nd hypogonadism more common in patients with DM and obesity
3. CF
   1. Decreased libido, Fatigue
   2. Testicular atrophy
   3. *Galactorrhea mostly present in Females
4. Lab: dec Testosterone, dec/Normal LH
5. Evaluation:
   1. Measure serum prolactin levels
   2. Pituitary MRI
      1. Recommended in patients with elevated Prolactin, Mass-effect symptoms, Very low Testosterone or disruption in other pituitary hormones

## Liver Cirrhosis and Hypogonadism

1. Hypogonadism due to primary gonadal injury or hypothalamic-pituitary dysfunction
2. inc Estradiol (increased conversion from androgens) — telangiectasias, palmar erythema, testicular atrophy, gynecomastia
3. dec Serum binding proteins for thyroid hormones — dec Total T3 and T4 but free T3 and T4 are unchanged, normal TSH (euthyroid status)

## Hyperandrogenism in Females

1. Rapid-onset hirsutism + virilization (temporal balding, excessive muscle, enlarged clitoris) --> very high androgen levels due to androgen-secreting neoplasm of ovaries or adrenal glands
2. Ovarian source: High Testosterone, Normal DHEAS
3. Adrenal source: High Testosterone and DHEAS
4. Note: Assess Testosterone:DHEAS ratio to differentiate between ovaries and adrenal gland
