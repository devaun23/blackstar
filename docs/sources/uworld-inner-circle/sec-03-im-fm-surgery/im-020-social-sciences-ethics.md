# Weird Social Sciences Topics

*Source: Ep 132*

## Palliative Care — Symptom Management

1. Terminal lung cancer in hospice not eating — Megestrol (progestin analog), dronabinol (cannabinoid), corticosteroids
   1. Don't improve survival or reduce morbidity
2. Terminal lung cancer in hospice with depression — Stimulants: methylphenidate. If super anxious, consider benzo. SSRIs will take too long to act
3. Metastatic malignancy with SOB — Opioids (morphine) + bowel regimen
4. On chemo with severe n/v — Ondansetron (serotonin receptor antagonist). AE: QT prolongation
5. Terminal GBM, days to weeks to live, nauseous with increased ICP — Glucocorticoids to reduce ICP
6. Burning & tingling in extremities (neuropathic pain) — TCAs, SNRI (duloxetine), Gabapentin/pregabalin
7. Cancer pain & hx of depression on treatment, started on pain med and had fevers + myoclonus --> serotonin syndrome 2/2 tramadol
   1. Tramadol is usually not good for cancer pain — not strong enough, lots of interactions
8. Widely metastatic cancer to liver & kidneys with severe pain — Fentanyl patch
   1. Avoid morphine in liver & kidney disease
9. Bad cancer pain, morphine increased at last visit, still in severe pain — Increase dose or frequency of opioid regimen
10. Metastatic malignancy in hospice with pain — Avoid Meperidine (can cause seizures)

## Ethics — Physician Conduct

1. Physician that smells of alcohol — Report to higher authority
   1. Order: Speak to physician --> Local physician program --> Medical Board
   2. If fraudulent situation --> Go straight to Medical Board
2. Palliative care does NOT preclude life-prolonging therapy
   1. Contrast with hospice care (physician must determine <6 months to live)
3. Medical error was made — Admit error to pt
   1. Data suggests most likely outcome is the physician NOT getting sued
4. Physician-assisted suicide — Illegal in every state
5. Very ill pt, some therapies remain but pt not interested — Respect pt's wishes if pt:
   1. Understands the situation
   2. Understands risks & benefits
   3. Communicates their choice clearly
6. Pt requests therapy that won't affect outcomes — No obligation to administer futile therapy
7. Pt requests abortion, physician uncomfortable — Transfer care to another physician
8. Old senile person not taking meds, keeps getting admitted — Appoint legal guardian

## Ethics — Minors

1. Adolescent <18 yo --> parents make decisions regarding medical care
2. Exceptions: Substance abuse, Mental health, Reproductive health

## Capacity vs. Competence

1. Capacity — determined by physician
   1. Criteria: understands situation, understands risks & benefits, clearly communicates decision
   2. Examples of lacking capacity: Delirium/AMS, Under the influence of drugs
2. Competence — determined by legal system

## Decision-Making Principles

1. Substituted judgment — surrogate makes decision based on what patient previously expressed
   1. Ex: 23yo comatose after opioid OD, next-of-kin withdraws care because pt said "I don't want life support"
2. Best interest standard — decision based on what produces best outcomes
   1. Ex: surrogate chooses treatment A because physician provides data showing better outcomes
   2. Ex: physician treating unresponsive trauma pt does what is medically indicated
3. Healthcare POA — designated person makes decisions if pt can't
4. Living will — document where pt outlines what interventions they would/would not want
5. Surrogate decision-maker order (if no healthcare POA): Spouse --> adult children --> parent --> adult sibling

## HIPAA

1. Don't disclose pt's information to family members without authorization
2. Exceptions:
   1. Protecting pt (e.g. protect from killing themselves)
   2. Protecting general public (e.g. homicidal ideation)
   3. Following the law

## Handoff Communication

1. Should happen in person
2. Provide key pieces of information in a standardized fashion
3. Use if-then statements
4. Key active problems, Key medications, Labs to follow up

## Quality Improvement

1. If a medical error occurs — first thing is Analyze the error
   1. Fishbone/Ishikawa diagram — keep identifying factors that contributed
2. Core principle of QI — PDSA cycle:
   1. P = Plan (planning the intervention)
   2. D = Do (putting into practice)
   3. S = Study (study the results)
   4. A = Act (refine the intervention)

## Diagnostic Biases

1. Availability bias — physician assumes same diagnosis they saw recently in similar presentation
2. Anchoring bias — depending too heavily on initial piece of information or first idea
3. Blind obedience bias — resident doesn't question attending's diagnosis
4. Premature closure — physician doesn't consider other possibilities after making initial impression

## IPV Screening

1. HITS (does your partner hurt, insult, threaten, or scream at you?)
2. HARK (humiliation, afraid of partner, rape, kick)

## Substance Abuse Management

### Opioid Addiction

1. Tx: Suboxone (buprenorphine/naloxone)
2. Opioid withdrawal: Tx with Clonidine

### Smoking Cessation

1. Options: Nicotine replacement therapy, Bupropion (NDRI — CI: eating disorder), Varenicline (nicotinic receptor partial agonist)
2. Most effective: Combination > Varenicline > Bupropion > NRT
3. Never choose e-cigarette on NBME

### Alcohol Screening

1. FIRST quantify drinking:
   1. At-risk: Woman OR >65 yo: >7 drinks/week or >3 drinks/one sitting
   2. At-risk: Man: >14 drinks/week or >4 drinks/one sitting
2. THEN employ formal screen:
   1. CAGE: Cut Down, Annoyed, Guilt, Eye opener
   2. AUDIT-C is better than CAGE
3. Clues that suggest alcoholism on NBMEs:
   1. Megaloblastic anemia (MCV >100)
   2. High GGT
   3. AST/ALT >2:1
4. Alcohol withdrawal --> give benzos (alcoholic hallucinosis, DTs)
5. Remember thiamine before glucose if alcoholic comes to ED (avoids Wernicke-Korsakoff)
6. If delirious from EtOH withdrawal — Don't give antipsychotic (can cause seizures), give benzos
