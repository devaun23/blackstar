# MILITARY MEDICINE

## TBI (Traumatic Brain Injury)

1. Prevent: Wear seatbelts, helmets
2. Epidemiology: More common in Males > Females
3. NEVER give steroids in traumatic brain injury
4. Classify by severity using GCS:
   1. >=13 mild TBI = Concussion
   2. 9-12 moderate
   3. <=8 severe TBI
5. Preferred Imaging --> MRI
6. Pathognomonic --> diffuse axonal injury (also seen in rapid acceleration-deceleration injury)
7. Anterior temporal lobes + orbital frontal cortex most susceptible to damage
8. Central diabetes insipidus can occur after TBI
9. Most common cognitive impairment after TBI --> Memory loss
10. Post concussive syndrome --> neuropsych symptoms in a post TBI (mild) pt
    1. Headache, dizziness, inc sensitivity to light and sound, anxiety, depression
11. Second impact syndrome --> a second ep of TBI after a mild TBI --> persistently vegetative or die
12. Increased ICP — Cushing Reflex: Bradycardia, HTN (widened pulse pressure), Irregular respirations
13. Management:
    1. Hyperventilation is the fastest measure to lower ICP but can only be used for short term
    2. Mannitol can be used but contraindicated in CHF
    3. Hypertonic saline is another option but risks hypernatremia
    4. Keep TBI patients normothermic
    5. N-Acetyl Cysteine for prevention of complications
    6. Tranexamic acid within the first 3 hrs to reduce risk of death
    7. After discharge --> send pt to rehab

## PTSD

1. Don't give benzodiazepines to PTSD especially in veterans (worsens it)
2. Don't pick debriefing as a treatment
3. Small volume of hippocampus
4. Military sexual trauma W >> M --> biggest risk factor for PTSD among servicemen/women
5. Exaggerated response to dexamethasone suppression test
6. Abnormal low cortisol and high catecholamines in urine (maladaptive hyperadrenergic response)
7. Homelessness is very common in veterans
8. MC comorbidity in veteran is substance use disorder (commonly alcohol) --> Highest risk can be decades after service
9. Rx: SSRIs (sertraline, fluoxetine, paroxetine), venlafaxine
10. Pts tend to have distress, autonomic hyperactivity on re-experiencing triggers
11. Usually arises from interpersonal trauma vs natural disaster (eg. rape vs earthquake)
12. 4 months pharm Rx does NOT apply to ASD (acute stress disorder, symptoms <1 month)
13. Outcome:
    1. Good: Pre-deployment planning, communication with family while deployed
    2. Poor: Poor mental health in family, behavioral disorders in children, high risk of suicide, divorce
14. Management:
    1. Nightmares: use Prazosin
    2. CBT of choice --> Exposure therapy (PTSD + OCD + Phobia)
    3. Eye movement therapy (EMDR) — bilateral stimulation with brief focus on trauma memory

## Military Infectious Disease

### Leishmaniasis

1. Active military returned from Afghanistan/Iraq/Saudi Arabia/Peru 2-3 weeks ago
2. Painless, purplish ulcerating papule on arm (typically upper extremities)
3. Transmission: Sandfly
4. Dx: Skin Biopsy
5. Tx: Amphotericin B or Paromomycin
6. Visceral form (donovani): fevers, weight loss, pancytopenia, hepatosplenomegaly, DARK SKIN LESIONS
   1. From Mexico, ME, North/East Africa, South America, Asia ("tropical areas")
   2. May see amastigotes on microscopy from tissue biopsy
   3. Rx: sodium stibogluconate or amphotericin B

### Military Vaccines/Protection

1. MMRV, Tdap, typhoid, influenza, polio, hep A + depending on location (yellow fever, anthrax, smallpox)
2. DEET on skin and permethrin on clothes as repellants
3. If medic --> Hep B; Sexually active --> Hep B
4. Outbreak of meningitis --> N. meningitidis, use ceftriaxone or ciprofloxacin, rifampin ppx for close contacts

### RSM (Returning Service Member) Scenarios

1. RSM from Egypt with hematuria or portal hypertension --> Schistosomiasis (haematobium or mansoni)
2. MCC of diarrhea in military --> Norovirus
3. Africa with bleeding, petechiae, contact with wild animals/dead bodies --> Ebola or Marburg viruses (spread via fomites/body fluids, No Rx)
4. South America with very high fevers, rash, bad myalgias (bone break fevers), bleeding, positive tourniquet test, thrombocytopenia --> Dengue hemorrhagic fever (aedes mosquito)
   1. Chikungunya: identical to dengue but without hemorrhage
   2. Zika: usually woman with child who has microcephaly and intracranial calcifications
   3. Yellow fever: jaundice, hepatomegaly (not present in dengue). Rehydrate + supportive care
5. RSM from Russia/China/Korea or Utah/NM/AZ/CO, contact with mice (deer mouse), high fever, myalgias, bilateral INTERSTITIAL pulmonary edema --> Hantavirus (No known treatment)
6. RSM from Hawaii or navy with conjunctival injection, jaundice --> Leptospirosis (Tx: Doxycycline)
   1. Weil's Disease: anemia, very high creatinine, jaundice — attacks liver and kidneys
   2. Use doxycycline for prevention
7. RSM with Hemoptysis, widened mediastinum +/- necrotic ulcer on neck --> Anthrax (poly-D-glutamate capsule)
   1. Can present as Hemorrhagic mediastinitis --> widened mediastinum on CXR
   2. Rx: doxycycline or fluoroquinolone. Vaccine available for service members

### Malaria PPX

1. Regions: Asia, Africa, ME
2. Options: atovaquone + proguanil, mefloquine, primaquine (for vivax and ovale — tertian fever, hypnozoite form), chloroquine (high resistance), doxycycline
3. P. malariae = quartan fever
4. P. falciparum = fever at odd intervals (most severe — cerebral malaria, pulmonary edema, renal failure)
5. Before primaquine for P. vivax or ovale --> check for G6PD deficiency (X-linked)

### Other RSM Scenarios

1. Exposure to animal/animal products/unpasteurized milk, undulant fevers and PROFUSE SWEATING --> Brucellosis
   1. Requires very low dose to get infected, tends to recur after treatment
   2. Rx: doxy and rifampin; If recurring: aminoglycosides
2. Cattle/sheep birth, pneumonia or chest pain (culture-negative endocarditis) --> Q fever (Coxiella Burnetii)
   1. Don't confuse with brucellosis — pneumonia is unique to Q fever
3. Contact with dogs/cats/pigs and bloody diarrhea --> Campylobacter jejuni
   1. Association with Guillain-Barre syndrome and Reiter's syndrome (Reactive arthritis)
4. Fever, neck rigidity, headache, myalgia, LP showing normal opening pressure/WBC/protein/glucose but + lymphocytes --> West Nile virus
5. Trouble swallowing (achalasia), chronic constipation (megacolon/Hirschsprung), S3 heart sound (dilated cardiomyopathy) --> Chagas disease
   1. Spread by reduviid bug, specific: periorbital edema (Romana sign)
   2. Rx: nifurtimox, benznidazole
6. Worked with turtles/pets/lots of eggs/poultry + bloody diarrhea + increased WBC (mostly neutrophils) --> Nontyphoidal salmonella --> DON'T TREAT IT
7. Chronic cough, fever, hemoptysis, night sweats, right upper lobe infiltrate/cavitary lesion --> TB
   1. Treatment = RIPE regimen for 2 months then continue with rifampin and isoniazid
   2. DON'T forget B6 vitamin
