# Cough

## Acute

### Acute Bronchitis

1. Preceding respiratory illness (Rhinovirus, Coronavirus, Influenza)
2. CF
   1. Cough >5 days to 3 weeks +/- purulent sputum (epithelial sloughing) +/- small amount of blood (epithelial damage and inflammation)
   2. Wheezing or rhonchi that clear with cough (easily mobilized vs. pneumonia), chest wall tenderness
   3. Absent systemic signs: Fever, chills
3. Dx: Clinical, CXR if pneumonia suspected
4. Tx: Symptomatic: NSAID +/- Bronchodilators (especially if wheezing in setting of preexisting pulmonary disease) Cough and airway sensitivity may persist for weeks
5. Antibiotics not recommended

## Chronic >8 weeks

1. Upper airway cough syndrome (UACS) = Post nasal drip
   1. Improvement with
      1. Chlorpheniramine, Diphenhydramine (H1-R blocker) due to decrease in nasal secretion (anticholinergic effect)
2. Asthma
   1. Chronic cough that occurs overnight and does not improve with antihistamine — Evaluate with PFTs to assess bronchodilator response
   2. Lack of bronchodilator response --> Methacholine challenge
3. GERD
4. ACE-I
   1. Onset within 1 week of drug initiation or dosage increase — tickling or scratching sensation in the throat
   2. Increase in circulating levels of inflammatory mediators (bradykinin, substance P, thromboxane, prostaglandins) that cause bronchoconstriction and bronchial irritation
5. Airway disease: Nonasthmatic eosinophilic bronchitis, Chronic bronchitis, Bronchiectasis, Malignancy
6. Pulmonary parenchymal disease: Lung abscess, Interstitial lung disease
