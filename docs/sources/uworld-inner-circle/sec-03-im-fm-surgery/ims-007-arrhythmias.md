# PREMATURE ARRHYTHMIA COMPLEX

## Premature Atrial Complex

1. Depolarization of atria originating outside SA node
2. Treatment only required when symptoms cause distress or when there is SVT — Low-dose BB
3. Management: reduction of precipitating factors (tobacco, alcohol, caffeine, stress)

# TACHYARRHYTHMIA

Wide Complex (>120msec) Tachycardia — Immediate Synchronized cardioversion due to risk of VF and Cardiac arrest

## Supraventricular

Rate Control Tx: Digoxin (esp if cannot tolerate BB due to hypotension or HF)

### Atrial Fibrillation

1. Atrial remodeling leads to disorganized atrial electrical activity, often originating in pulmonary vein ostia
2. Associations: Hyperthyroidism (seen in 5-15%; Propranolol for rate control and hyperadrenergic symptoms)
3. Management of thromboembolism risk (CHA2DS2VASc >=2)
   1. Valvular AFib — Warfarin
   2. Nonvalvular New onset AFib — CHA2DS2-VASc score
      1. Chronic anticoagulation: Men ≥2, Women ≥3
      2. Oral anticoagulants: Apixaban, Rivaroxaban, Dabigatran or less commonly Warfarin
      3. Generally low risk in patients <50yo with no significant PMH
4. Radiofrequency ablation
   1. Myocardial tissue surrounding pulmonary vein ostia can be disrupted by catheter-based RFA
   2. Electrically disconnects source of ectopic foci from the left atrium
5. Management of arrhythmia
   1. Acute and Chronic: BB or Nondihydro CCB — Diltiazem
      - If pt cannot tolerate BB due to hypotension or HF: Digoxin
   2. HemStable: Rate control — IV BB or Nondihydro CCB (caution in HF due to ↓CO)
   3. HemUnstable: Electrical cardioversion

### Atrial Flutter

1. MCC: reentrant circuit around tricuspid annulus (cavotricuspid isthmus of right atrium)
2. RF: Age, HTN, HF, post-cardiac surgery
3. Complication: thromboembolism risk similar to A.Fib
4. Management
   1. Chronic anticoagulation for multiple CHA2DS2-VASc risk factors
   2. Rate control: BB or Nonhydro CCB; Digoxin if intolerant
   3. Definitive: RFA or electric cardioversion (*require prior anticoagulation*)

### Multifocal Atrial Tachycardia

- Best indicator for improvement: Smoking cessation

### Paroxysmal SVT (Narrow Complex)

1. Most common — AVNRT
   1. Two distinct conduction pathways in AV node (slow and fast)
   2. Presentation: Young patients with structurally normal heart, palpitations
   3. PP: Atrial premature beat occurs when fast is refractory but slow is not → AVNRT initiated → retrograde atrial depolarization simultaneous with anterograde ventricular depolarization → buried P waves within QRS
4. Treatment
   1. HD Unstable: Cardioversion
   2. HD Stable:
      1. 1st: Vagal maneuvers (Carotid sinus massage, Cold-water immersion, Diving reflex, Valsalva)
         - Increased parasympathetic tone → temporary slowing through AV node → increase AV refractory period → termination
      2. 2nd: Adenosine
2. Less common — AVRT (accessory pathway outside AV node — WPW Syndrome)
3. Triggers: Alcohol, caffeine, nicotine, psychological stress

## Non-Supraventricular

### Wolff-Parkinson-White Syndrome

1. Preexcitation of ventricles via abnormal bypass tract (bundle of Kent)
2. Short PR (<120msec), Initial slow upstroke of QRS (delta wave), Widened QRS with ST and T-wave abnormalities
3. Mostly asx; Re-entrant SVT may occur if retrograde conduction present
4. Complications — Atrial Fibrillation
   1. 10-30% of WPW patients
   2. Bypasses rate-limiting AV node → rapid ventricular response → deteriorate into VFib
   3. Management: Unstable: Cardioversion | Stable: Procainamide or Ibutilide

### Ventricular Tachycardia (Wide, Bizarre Complex)

**Monomorphic — Sustained (SMVT)**
1. PP: Abnormal electrical activity around ischemic scar tissue or abnormal automaticity (ie. Dilated CM)
2. Increased Risk: CHD, LV Systolic Dysfunction
3. Dx: Fusion beats — hybrid of normal and wide QRS
4. Tx: Stable: IV Amiodarone | Unstable: Cardioversion | Pulseless VT: Defibrillation (unsynchronized)
5. Compare to PSVT: Narrow complex, treated with vagal maneuvers or Adenosine if stable

**Polymorphic — Torsades de Pointes**
1. Congenital or Acquired QT Prolongation
2. Tx: Stable: Magnesium Sulfate | Unstable: Defibrillation

### Ventricular Fibrillation

1. Most frequent underlying arrhythmia responsible for sudden cardiac arrest, 50% in 1st hour
2. Reentry is predominant mechanism
   1. Phase 1a "immediate" <10 min — acute ischemia causes heterogeneity of conduction → reentrant arrhythmia
   2. Phase 1b "delayed" 10-60min — abnormal automaticity
3. Presentation: Regain consciousness post CPR after brief unresponsiveness
4. Tx: Defibrillation (unsynchronized shock)
5. Increased Risk: CHD, LV Systolic Dysfunction

### Sudden Cardiac Arrest

1. Out-of-Hospital: MC due to VTach/VFib (pulseless, shockable rhythm)
2. Important factor for survival: Time to rhythm analysis and defibrillation

## Tachyarrhythmia Treatment Summary

- Pulseless VT, VF: Defibrillation = Unsynchronized Cardioversion
- Afib, Aflutter: Stable → Propranolol (Digoxin if hypotensive) | Unstable → Cardioversion

# BRADYARRHYTHMIA

## Sinus Bradycardia — <60bpm

## Sick Sinus Syndrome

1. PP: Impaired SA node automaticity due to degeneration/fibrosis
2. ECG: Alternating bradycardia and atrial tachyarrhythmia, sinus pause/arrest

## AV Block

Note: May be intermittent — normal HR does not exclude bradyarrhythmia

| Type | Presentation | ECG | Management |
|------|-------------|-----|------------|
| First | Asx | PR >200ms | Observation |
| Mobitz I | Usually Asx | Constant P-P, Increasing PR, Decreasing R-R, Dropped QRS, Group beating | Observation |
| Mobitz II | Fatigue, lightheaded, syncope | Constant PR, Randomly dropped QRS | Permanent pacemaker |
| Third | Fatigue, lightheaded, syncope | Complete P and QRS dissociation | Cardiac pacing → PPM |

### Differentiating Mobitz I vs II

| | Mobitz I | Mobitz II |
|---|---------|----------|
| Level of Block | AV Node | Below AV (His bundle) |
| Decreased vagal tone (Exercise, atropine) | Block improves | Block worsens |
| Increased vagal tone (carotid massage) | Worsens | Improves |
| Risk of complete block | Low | High (PPM indication) |
| Cause | Athletes, BB | Ischemia, Age related |

### Third Degree AV Block

1. P waves march at intrinsic SA node rate ~85/min
2. QRS complex at intrinsic His/ventricle rate ~50/min → Slow ventricular rate → reduced CO → dyspnea, fatigue, syncope or cardiac arrest
3. RF: Inferior wall MI (RCA affected)
4. Contraindications: Adenosine, BB, Digoxin (can worsen AV block)

### Highly Trained Athletes

1. First degree and Mobitz Type 1 common due to high resting vagal tone
2. Reassurance with no further intervention

## QT Prolongation

### Pharmacological Causes

- Antipsychotics: Haloperidol, ziprasidone, quetiapine, thioridazine, olanzapine, risperidone
- Antiarrhythmics: Amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide, procainamide, quinidine, flecainide
- Antibiotics: Macrolides, fluoroquinolones
- Antidepressants: Amitriptyline, imipramine, citalopram
- Others: Methadone, sumatriptan, ondansetron, cisapride

### Other Causes

- Long QT syndromes (Jervell and Lange-Neilson, Romano-Ward)
- Hypocalcemia, Hypokalemia, Hypomagnesemia
- Hypothyroidism, Hypothermia
- Note: May trigger Torsades de Pointes

# SYNCOPE

1. Reflex: Trigger or prodrome
2. Cardiac
   1. LV outflow obstruction
   2. VTach: At rest without warning symptoms
      - Young pt without heart disease: Ambulatory ECG
      - Heart disease patient: Hospital admission
   3. Conduction impairment
3. Orthostatic Syncope: Positional change
   1. Disruption of baroreceptor response (ie. medication interference)
      - A1 blockade — impaired arteriolar smooth muscle contractility
      - Upon standing → blood cannot mobilize from capacitance veins → CO and cerebral perfusion drop = syncope
