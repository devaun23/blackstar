# PLEURAL DISEASE

## Parapneumonic Effusion & Empyema

1. Pleural effusion
   1. Uncomplicated: sterile exudate, negative gram stain --> Abx only
   2. Complicated: bacterial invasion of space --> Abx and drainage with chest tube
      1. LOW PLEURAL FLUID GLUCOSE <60MG/DL, PH <7.2, WBC >50G, LDH>1G
   3. Empyema abscess of the pleural space
      1. MC due to aspiration pneumonia
         1. Movement of anaerobic oral flora into the intrapleural space
         2. Consolidation in lower lobe if empyema secondary to aspiration — dependent lung portions
      2. S/S:
         1. Weight loss and symptoms of uncomplicated pneumonia; Moderate pleural effusion
            1. Exudative effusion increased pleural fluid leukocytes, protein, lactate dehydrogenase; decreased glucose, pH
      3. Dx:
         1. Putrid or foul smelling pleural fluid is diagnostic
         2. Lateral decubitus XR: free flowing effusion
   4. Management: Evacuation via chest tube or surgery + Abx (Treated similar to abscess of skin with incision and drainage)

## Pleural Effusion (excess fluid in pleural space)

1. Decreased breath sounds in affected lung region
2. First step in management is Diagnostic Thoracentesis unless signs/symptoms of CHF (diuretics used)
   1. Pleural fluid analysis
   2. Decreased pH and glucose — increased cellular metabolism in fluid — may indicate malignancy, infection
   3. 75% malignant pleural effusions due to lung carcinoma, breast carcinoma and lymphoma
3. Transudative
   1. Serous fluid due to imbalanced hydrostatic or oncotic pressure
   2. Causes:
      1. Heart failure
         1. Initial management (HF exacerbation, peripheral edema): Furosemide
      2. Nephrotic Syndrome
      3. Cirrhosis
4. Exudative
   1. Complex fluid due to pneumonia, empyema, malignancy or inflammatory diseases
   2. Rheumatoid Arthritis: Neutrophil predominant
   3. TB: increased adenosine deaminase, lymphocyte predominant
   4. Pancreaticopleural fistula (PPF)
      1. Amylase-rich exudative pleural effusion due to acute/chronic pancreatitis
      2. "Large L-sided pleural effusion, hx of heavy alcohol use, recurrent epigastric abd pain"
         1. Vs. Empyema and Pulmonary TB (exudative, lower pH, very elevated amylase; TB effusions also have elevated adenosine deaminase levels)
      3. Management
         1. PPF recur after thoracentesis
         2. Bowel rest to avoid pancreatic stimulation
         3. ERCP with sphincterotomy +/- stent to promote drainage through ampulla of Vater instead of fistula
   5. Hepatic hydrothorax: pleural effusion not due to cardiac or pulmonary abnormality
      1. Seen in patients with cirrhosis and portal hypertension
      2. Small defects in the diaphragm allow peritoneal fluid to pass into pleural space on the right side due to less muscular hemidiaphragm --> transudative pleural effusion
      3. CF: Dyspnea, cough, pleuritic chest pain, hypoxemia
      4. Dx: Documentation of effusion (CXR) and testing to exclude other causes (Thoracentesis, Echo)
      5. Tx: Salt restriction and diuretic
         1. Severe symptoms: Therapeutic thoracentesis
         2. Definitive Tx: Liver transplant
         3. Chest tube placement avoided due to risk of large-volume protein, fluid and electrolyte losses and renal failure

## Pneumothorax

1. Occult Pneumothorax
   1. Seen on CT but not on CXR
   2. Positive-pressure ventilation patients more likely to require chest tube placement than those not on PPV
      1. Thus, monitor and provide supplemental oxygen only if pt is not in respiratory distress
2. Pneumothorax: CXR --> Chest tube placement
   1. Primary Spontaneous — No history of lung disease; tall, thin men in early 20s
   2. Secondary Spontaneous — Underlying lung disease
   3. Cause: Direct disruption of lung parenchyma or disruption of visceral pleura via rib penetration
3. Cause: Positive Pressure Ventilation (Mechanical ventilation)-caused barotrauma
   1. Pulmonary barotrauma leads to alveolar rupture and pneumothorax formation
   2. COPD patients at high risk due to preexisting pulmonary hyperinflation and presence of bullae or blebs that can rupture
   3. Affected lung receives stable volume of air from ventilators --> increased peak pressure (measure of increased resistance to air flow from the ventilator) and increased plateau pressure (measure of the lung's decreased ability to expand compliance)
4. Cause: Pulmonary barotrauma if diver fails to expel air from lungs during ascent
5. CF:
   1. Chest pain, dyspnea
   2. Hyperresonance to percussion
   3. Decreased breath sounds, decreased chest movement
   4. Soft tissue crepitance
   5. Large: Abrupt-onset Tachycardia, Tachypnea, Hypoxemia, Decreased or absent unilateral breath sounds
6. Imaging
   1. Visceral pleural line
   2. Absent lung markings beyond pleural edge
7. Small <2cm: O2 and monitor with repeat CXR
   1. Monitoring important in patients with thoracostomy because simple pneumothorax may progress to tension pneumothorax
8. Large: Thoracostomy (needle decompression), Thoracotomy (chest tube)
   1. Monitor for air leak and drainage volume
   2. Commonly can remove tube around 48hrs
   3. In case of persistent large air leak or failure of lung re-expansion --> strong possibility of tracheobronchial injury --> requires surgery
9. Spontaneous pneumomediastinum
   1. Severe coughing paroxysms increase intraalveolar pressure, cause leak into subcutaneous tissue (subcutaneous emphysema) and lead to spontaneous pneumomediastinum
      1. Hamman sign
   2. RF: Asthma exacerbation, Respiratory infection, Tall/thin adolescent boy
   3. 1st step: CXR to confirm mediastinal gas and rule out pneumothorax
   4. Tx: Rest, Analgesics; Avoid Valsalva

## Tension Pneumothorax: CXR --> Needle decompression + Chest tube

1. Cause
   1. Trauma
   2. Mechanical ventilation
      1. nCPAP: nasal CPAP in neonate
         1. Mechanical ventilation increases transpulmonary pressure
      2. Management: Immediate thoracostomy
      3. Meconium aspiration syndrome (common in post mature neonates)
         1. Meconium plugging of airways traps distal gas and promotes over distention and rupture
   3. Pleural bleb burst in smoker
2. PPV
   1. Worsens TP by increasing intrathoracic pressures and intensifying one-way valve effect
   2. As pleural cavity fills with air, increased pressure required to initiate inspiratory flow
3. High intrapleural pressure
   1. Large size pneumothorax
   2. High intrathoracic pressure compresses vena cava and impedes cardiac venous return
      1. Decreased venous return to heart and potential for hypotension and cardiac arrest — "distended neck veins, obtunded patient"
   3. Severe distress with hypotension and hypoxemia
4. Signs/Symptoms (Obstructive Shock)
   1. Hyperresonance
   2. Ipsi diminished breath sounds (could occur post intubation in blunt chest injury pt)
   3. Ipsi hemidiaphragm flattening
   4. Contralat Trachea deviation
   5. Hypotension
   6. Distention of neck veins due to SVC compression
5. Imaging
   1. CXR:
      1. Air (without lung markings) outside the pleural line
      2. Ipsilateral lung collapse
      3. Contralateral Mediastinal shift *Note: Also seen in foreign body aspiration (but with hyperinflation of lungs and wheezing)
      4. Increased brightness of the involved hemithorax or transillumination of chest
6. Management
   1. 1st: Urgent Needle decompression = thoracostomy done before intubation due to exacerbation of TP with positive-pressure ventilation
      1. Midclavicular 2nd ICS or Midaxillary 5th ICS
   2. 2nd: Emergency tube thoracostomy
   3. Note: Confirmation with X-ray not necessary as first step if patient is hemodynamically unstable
7. Complication: Persistent Pneumothorax/Pneumomediastinum despite tube thoracostomy
   1. Due to Bronchial Rupture (proximal)
      1. "Reaccumulation of air in pleural space, pneumomediastinum and subcutaneous emphysema"
      2. Rapid large air leak into chest-tube drainage and persistent pneumothorax/mediastinum
      3. Dx: Bronchoscopy
      4. Tx: Operative repair

## Mainstem Bronchus Intubation

1. CF: Hypoxemia, increased peak pressure, increased plateau pressure (due to limited volume of ventilated lung)
   1. Decreased breath sounds in contralateral/unventilated lung
