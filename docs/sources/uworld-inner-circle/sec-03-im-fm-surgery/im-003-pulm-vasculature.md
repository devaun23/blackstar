# PULMONARY VASCULATURE DISEASE

## Pulmonary Hypertension

1. PP
   1. Pulmonary arterial pressure >25mmHg at rest (N <20mmHg)
   2. Common cause of LV systolic or diastolic dysfunction
2. Causes
   1. Genetic: Mutation BMPR2
   2. Vasculature remodeling in Sickle Cell
   3. Systolic LV dysfunction
      1. Reduced LV ejection fraction
      2. Peripheral edema
      3. Dilated RV on Echo, tricuspid regurg (systolic murmur near lower sternal border, increases with inspiration)
      4. Initial management with Diuretic and ACE-I
   4. Cor Pulmonale (lung disease --> diffuse hypoxia --> pulmonary HTN --> RV failure)
3. CF
   1. Parasternal heave (RV faces increased afterload --> RV hypertrophy --> Stronger "heartbeat" since RV is most anterior heart structure)
   2. Loud S2 (increased afterload faced by RV)
   3. R-HF symptoms (JVD, LEE, Hepatomegaly) without L-HF symptoms (pulmonary edema)
4. Treatment
   1. LV Dysfunction: Diuretic, ACE-I (or ARBs)
   2. Idiopathic PH:
      1. PDE5-I (sildenafil)
      2. Endothelin-R antag (bosentan)
      3. Prostacyclin (epoprostenol)

## Cor Pulmonale

1. Right heart failure due to primary pulmonary disorder
   1. Diffuse lung hypoxia — diffuse vasoconstriction within the lung — pulmonary HTN --> increased afterload/resistance faced by RV --> RV failure
2. Cause
   1. Parenchymal disease/chronic hypoxia
      1. COPD Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction
      2. Cystic Fibrosis
      3. Interstitial lung disease — Pulmonary Fibrosis (Scleroderma)
      4. Obstructive Sleep Apnea Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction
   2. Pulmonary vascular disease: Pulmonary Embolism sudden increase in pulmonary artery pressure
   3. Others
      1. Myasthenia Gravis
      2. Poliomyelitis
3. CF
   1. Dyspnea and fatigue on exertion
   2. Exertional angina (due to increased cardiac demand)
   3. Exertional syncope (due to decreased cardiac output)
   4. Distant heart sounds (due to hyperinflated lungs in COPD)
   5. End-stage: presents with cardiogenic shock due to decreased stroke volume
      1. Hypotension, Tachycardia
4. PE
   1. Jugular venous distention
   2. Peripheral edema
   3. Palpable RV heave (hypertrophied RV is most anterior heart structure), loud P2, TR murmur
   4. Hepatomegaly with pulsatile liver
5. CXR: Enlarged pulmonary arteries (without vascular congestion), loss of retrosternal air space due to RVH
6. Diagnostic evaluation
   1. ECG: Incomplete/Complete RBBB, Right axis deviation, RV hypertrophy, RA enlargement
   2. Echo: Pulmonary HTN, RV dilation/dysfunction, Tricuspid regurgitation (TR)
   3. Cath — Gold standard —
      1. Elevated filling pressures — CVP + RV End diastolic volume + Mean pulmonary artery pressure (>=25mmHg at rest) without left heart disease
      2. Decreased CO
      3. Pulmonary HTN
7. Management
   1. Optimize RV dynamics (preload, afterload, contractility)
   2. Supplemental oxygen
   3. Diuretics
   4. IV inotropes for severe decompensation
8. Complication: Pulmonary HTN (RV dilation, RVH)

## Pulmonary Edema

1. Cardiogenic: SOB, S3, JVD, Peripheral edema, Respiratory distress
   1. Tx: Diuretics to lower hypervolemic state and cardiac filling pressure, pulmonary venous and capillary pressure
   2. Nitroglycerin: venodilator, lower preload
   3. PPV to reduce alveolar and interstitial edema and drive fluid back into circulation
2. Noncardiogenic causes
   1. ARDS
   2. Transfusion-related Acute Lung Injury
   3. Rickettsia rickettsi: Attacks vascular endothelial cells — life threatening noncardiogenic pulmonary edema = bilateral rales

## Sickle Cell — Pulmonary HTN

1. Right heart failure due to pulmonary remodeling causing Pulmonary HTN
2. Intravascular hemolysis causes chronic inflammation and endothelial dysfunction — hyperplasia and hypertrophy of small pulmonary vessels = vascular remodeling and increased pulmonary vascular resistance — decreased RV output and left ventricular filling = low CO
3. Initial presentation of pulmonary HTN as exertional dyspnea and fatigue

## Mitral Stenosis (Pulmonary Manifestations)

1. "Rheumatic Heart Disease" in those from Latin America, Africa, Asia
   1. LA dilation causes elevation of left main stem bronchus
   2. MS causes backflow — LA dilation disrupts electrical conduction — A.fib and possible systemic emboli
   3. A.fib can precipitate acute decompensated heart failure because loss of organized atrial contraction doesn't generate enough pressure to move fluid forward across stenotic valve
