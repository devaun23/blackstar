# Male Breast Cancer

1. RF
   1. Family History; BRCA 1/2
   2. Abnormal estrogen/androgen ratio: Klinefelter syndrome, Obesity, Cirrhosis, Marijuana use
2. CF
   1. Subareolar mass
   2. Skin & nipple dimpling, induration, ulceration
   3. Often detected at advanced stage
3. Dx
   1. Mammogram
   2. Biopsy: invasive ductal carcinoma (hormone receptor-positive) most common

# Gynecomastia

1. Pseudogynecomastia: Chronic, excessive deposition of fat in breast without distinct margin
   1. Tx: Weight loss
2. True: Margin of firm glandular tissue palpable and tender breast

# Cystoscopy Indications

1. Gross hematuria with no evidence of glomerular disease or infection
2. Microscopic Hematuria with no evidence of glomerular disease or infection but increased risk for malignancy
3. Recurrent UTIs
4. Obstructive symptoms with suspicion for stricture, stone
5. Irritative symptoms without urinary infection
6. Abnormal bladder imaging or urine cytology

# Androgenic Steroids

1. Complication: Infertility via suppression of GnRH, LH, FSH
   1. Normal testosterone assay levels (due to exogenous intake)
   2. Slightly elevated Creatinine due to elevated muscle mass
   3. Acne, gynecomastia (Androgen --> Estrone), decreased testicular size, aggressiveness

# Fournier Gangrene

1. Life-threatening necrotizing fasciitis that progresses to sepsis and possible death
2. Cutaneous breakdowns in perianal or genital region allow portal entry for polymicrobial colonic/urogenital organisms
3. Spreads along subq fat via fascial planes, results in micro thrombi of cutaneous vessels and leads to skin gangrene
4. Greater risk in poorly controlled diabetes mellitus and obesity
5. Emergent surgery; >20% patients die during hospitalization

# Genital Lesions

1. Chancre
   1. Painless, nonpruritic ulcer with induration
2. HSV
   1. Painful, ulcerating vesicles on an erythematous base
3. Lichen Planus
   1. CF: 5 "Ps"
      1. Pruritic, Purple/pink, Polygonal, Papules, Plaques
      2. Lacy, white network of lines (Wickham striae)
      3. Location: Skin (ankles, wrists), Oral mucosa (white papule and plaques, mucosal atrophy, ulcers), Genitalia
   2. Associations
      1. Hepatitis C
      2. Medications: ACEi, Thiazide
   3. Natural History
      1. Chronic symptoms
      2. Formation of lesions at site of trauma — Koebner phenomenon
      3. Spontaneous resolution within 2 years
   4. Tx
      1. Local: High-potency glucocorticoids — Betamethasone
      2. Widespread: Systemic glucocorticoids, Phototherapy

# TESTES

## Layers

Skin, Dartos m, External Spermatic fascia, Cremasteric m, Internal Spermatic fascia, Tunica Vaginalis (visceral and parietal) [peritoneal layers carried from abdomen]

## Epididymitis

1. Bacterial sequestration in the epididymis (purulent urethral discharge)
2. <35yo: Chlamydia, Gonorrhea
3. >35yo: Bladder outlet obstruction (coliform bacteria)
4. Presentation: Normal cremasteric reflex, relief with testicle elevation (Prehn sign), posterior testicular pain, unilateral
5. Dx: NAAT for Chlamydia/Gonorrhea, UA/Culture
6. Tx: Abx Ceftriaxone + Doxycycline or Levofloxacin (enteric)

## Hydrocele

1. Fluid collection between parietal and visceral layers of tunica vaginalis
2. "Persistent patent processus vaginalis"
3. Tx: Incision and drainage

## Orchitis

## Torsion

1. Presentation: Absent cremasteric reflex, horizontal testicular lie with elevated testicle, pain with testicle elevation
2. Referred pain to abdomen (due to descent during development)
3. Intermittent torsion: prior episodes that resolve without intervention
4. Imaging: No blood flow on scrotal ultrasound with Doppler
   1. Reactive hydrocele may be visible on US
   2. Heterogenous echotexture indicates testicular necrosis
5. 80% untreated torsions develop into nonviable testicle within 12 hours

## Varicocele

1. Tortuous dilation of the pampiniform plexus surrounding spermatic cord and testis
2. Primary (L-sided)
   1. Pubertal: Compression of L Renal vein between SMA and Aorta; Incompetent venous valves
   2. Soft, irregular mass that INCREASES IN SIZE WITH STANDING AND VALSALVA; "ropy mass" "irregular"
   3. Reassurance and observation
3. Secondary (R-sided)
   1. Prepubertal: Extrinsic compression of IVC; Venous thrombus
   2. Soft, coiled, scrotal mass that does not compress while supine
   3. Dx: Abdominal Ultrasound (Risk of Wilms tumor)
4. Complication: Increased risk for infertility

## Testicular Cancer

1. AFP, B-hCG, LDH
2. Metastasis: Retroperitoneal lymph nodes, Mediastinum
3. Types
   1. 95% Germ Cell:
      1. Seminoma: Retain features of spermatogenesis (Possible B-hCG elevation)
      2. Nonseminoma (B-hCG, +AFP): Partially differentiated
         1. Embryonal carcinoma
         2. Yolk sac (AFP)
         3. Choriocarcinoma (B-hCG)
         4. Teratoma
   2. Sex cord-Stromal:
      1. Leydig: Excessive Estrogen (gynecomastia) or Testosterone (Acne); Precocious puberty
         1. Estrogen/Testosterone lead to FSH and LH suppression
      2. Sertoli: Excessive Estrogen (gynecomastia)
4. Painless testicular mass, dull ache in lower abdomen
5. Workup: Bilateral scrotal US, Serum tumor markers, Radical Inguinal Orchiectomy

# PROSTATE

## BPH

1. Hyperplasia of prostatic stromal and epithelial cells
2. Urinary retention due bladder outflow obstruction
   1. Management: Acute decompression with urethral or suprapubic catheterization before any other intervention (including antibiotics for suspected infection)
      1. Post-void residual volume
         1. Normal: 50-100mL
         2. Intervention required: >200-250mL
   2. Complications of prolonged bladder outlet obstruction
      1. Bladder rupture
      2. Urothelial cell atrophy or necrosis
      3. Hydroureter, Hydronephrosis
      4. Postrenal azotemia +/- renal failure
3. Voiding (Obstructive symptoms)
   1. Weak urinary stream
   2. Intermittency
   3. Incomplete emptying
   4. Hesitancy
   5. Straining to void
4. Storage (Irritative, filling symptoms)
   1. Frequency
   2. Urgency
   3. Nocturia
   4. Incontinence
5. RF: Amitriptyline (anticholinergic properties — urinary retention)
6. Tx
   1. Alpha blockers — Terazosin, Tamsulosin
   2. 5-a reductase inhibitors — Finasteride
   3. Surgical — Transurethral resection of the prostate (TURP)
7. Complication: Recurrence post-TURP
   1. Leftover prostate tissue enlarges under influence of androgens
   2. Alpha-blockers, 5-alpha reductase-I, PDE5-I or repeat TURP
8. Complication: Acute Pyelonephritis
   1. Periurethral prostatic enlargement impinges urethra and prevents complete bladder evacuation --> High post-void residual volume
   2. This allows fecal flora that have contaminated urinary tract to proliferate rather than be removed in urine
   3. Bacterial ascent to kidney causes pyelonephritis: fever, costovertebral angle tenderness, leukocytosis

## Chronic Prostatitis/Chronic Pelvic Pain Syndrome

1. Unidentifiable cause but commonly due to noninfectious chronic prostate inflammation
2. CF
   1. Pain in pelvis, perineum, genitalia
   2. Irritative voiding symptoms (urgency, hesitancy)
   3. Hematospermia, pain with ejaculation
   4. "Increased anal sphincter tone"
3. Dx
   1. No or mild prostate tenderness
   2. Sterile urine culture and normal UA
4. Management
   1. Alpha blockers — Tamsulosin
   2. Antibiotics — Ciprofloxacin especially if history of UTI, Fluoroquinolones
   3. 5a-reductase inhibitor — Finasteride

## Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis

1. Resistant bacterial infection of the prostate (Hx of UTI, positive urine culture, prostatic tenderness)
2. PP
   1. Recurrent UTI, painful ejaculation, prostatic tenderness but normal prostate exam
   2. Prostatitis in a young man (<35yo): Chlamydia (sterile pyuria) or Neisseria gonorrhea
   3. Prostatitis in an older man (>35yo): Gram negative bacteria (such as E. coli)
3. CF
   1. Caused by coliform (E.coli) bacteria that contaminated the urethra and entered prostate via intraprostatic urinary reflux
   2. Fever, dysuria, swollen/tender prostate
4. Note: Sterile pyuria (positive WBCs in urine but negative urine culture) is classically seen in cervicitis (in women), chronic prostatitis (in men) and urethritis (both)
5. Transient improvement of symptoms with short courses of antibiotics
6. Tx: 6wks of Fluoroquinolones for eradication

## Prostate Cancer

1. RF: Advanced Age (>70)
2. Metastasis: Bone, Spine (via Batson plexus, valveless veins)
   1. Osteoblastic bone disease
      1. inc Alkaline phosphatase, lumbar sclerotic lesions (irregular and hyper dense areas of bony sclerosis)
      2. Osteoblastic lesions (vs. osteoclastic seen in most metastatic cancer): Normal or dec Calcium
      3. Low back pain
      4. Evaluation: Radionuclide bone scan, prostate-specific antigen test, prostate biopsy

# URETHRA

## Posterior Urethral injury

1. High-riding prostate; blood at the meatus; inability to void (urethral discontinuity)
2. Associated: Pelvic fracture (adducted and IR leg, perineal bruising)
   1. Abrupt upward shifting of bladder can lead to urethral tearing, commonly at bulbomembranous junction
3. Dx: Retrograde urethrogram, KUB xray
   1. Xray of urethral tract following radiopaque contrast injection through meatus
   2. Extravasation of contrast from urethra is diagnostic
   3. Urethrography preceded before foley insertion to prevent partial urethral tear becoming complete laceration
4. Tx: Supra-pubic catheter if necessary (avoid foley catheter)

## Posterior Urethral Valves

1. Bladder distention, decreased urine output, respiratory distress — due to OLIGOHYDRAMNIOS AND LUNG HYPOPLASIA
2. Dx: Renal and bladder ultrasound and then voiding cystourethrogram
3. Tx: Bladder drainage, ablation of PUV

## Bladder Cancer: Urothelial neoplasm (painless gross hematuria)

1. RF: Cigarette smoking, Occupational (Painters, Metal workers), Chronic cystitis, Cyclophosphamide, Pelvic radiation exposure
2. Dx: Cystoscopy

# PENIS

## Penile Fracture

1. If there is urethral injury (blood at the meatus, dysuria, urinary retention) --> Retrograde Urethrography prior to surgery. If urethral injury is confirmed, urethra repair also happens at time of surgery.

## Peyronie's Disease

1. Fibrosis of tunica albuginea (TGF-1 upregulation) restricts tissue expansion and flexibility during erections
2. Penile pain, curvature, dorsal nodules/plaques

## Priapism

1. Erection
   1. Is via parasympathetic-dilation of cavernous arteries, increased blood flow into corpora cavernosa; however this compresses emissary veins against tunica albuginea blocking blood outflow
   2. Subsequent sympathetic stimulation constricts arterial inflow, induces cavernosal smooth muscle contraction and blood exits corpora via emissary veins leading to detumescence
   3. Persistent inflow or obstructed outflow leads to priapism
2. Causes
   1. Autonomic dysfunction: Spinal cord injury
   2. Altered blood viscosity: Sickle Cell Disease, Blood Dyscrasias (CML)
      1. CML Hints: Hepatosplenomegaly suggests heme malignancy --> Order a Complete Blood Count
      2. Sickle Cell: Aspiration of blood from corpora cavernosa, then intracavernous phenylephrine injection
         1. Blood gas analysis reveals ischemic pattern with acidosis, hypoxemia and hypercarbia
   3. Meds that alter autonomic/vascular tone: PDE-5 inhibitors, Trazodone, Cocaine
3. Treatment
   1. Aspiration, irrigation
   2. Intracorporal a1 agonist: Phenylephrine
