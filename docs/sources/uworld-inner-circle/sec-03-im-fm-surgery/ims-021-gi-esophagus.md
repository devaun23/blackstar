# Diagnostic Tests

1. Stool fecal occult test: Stool guaiac test (most common type)
   1. Involves placing a fecal sample on guaiac paper and applying hydrogen peroxide which, in the presence of blood, yields a blue reaction product within seconds
   2. Sensitive, not specific
   3. If positive --> Colonoscopy

# Epiglottitis: H influenza type b or Strep pneumonia (adults)

1. Swollen epiglottis "thumb sign"
2. 3D's: Drooling, dysphagia, distress
3. High fever, stridor (high-pitched), life-threatening airway obstruction
4. Dx: Direct visualization, Imaging — Lateral neck X-ray
5. Tx:
   1. Emergency Laryngoscopy and endotracheal intubation
   2. Adults: IV Antibiotics: Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin

# Ludwig angina

1. Dental infection "teeth roots" --> Cellulitis of submandibular space, polymicrobial
2. S/S
   1. Bilateral edema of submandibular and sublingual space
   2. Airway compromise due to posterior displacement of the tongue — drooling, dysphagia, muffled voice
   3. Floor of mouth elevated, displacing the tongue "woody/brawny" neck
3. Dx: CT neck
4. Tx: IV Abx — Ampicillin-sulbactam + Clindamycin

# Foreign body ingestion

1. High risk of perforation and can be fatal
2. Immediate Endoscopic removal if an object is in esophagus, stomach or proximal duodenum

# Peritonsillar abscess (near uvula)

1. Tonsillitis "tender cervical lymph nodes" complication, deviation of uvula and unilateral LAD
2. Initial management: Peritonsillar aspiration and IV antibiotics

# Tracheoesophageal Fistula

1. TEF with esophageal atresia presents shortly after birth with copious oral secretions and choking, coughing and/or vomiting with feeding
   1. Defective division of foregut into esophagus and trachea
   2. Results in proximal esophageal pouch and fistula between distal trachea and esophagus
2. VACTERL association: Vertebral, Anal, Cardiac, TracheoEsophageal, Renal, Limb defects
   1. Screening: Echo, Renal US
3. Dx: NG tube insertion reveals resistance at proximal esophageal pouch

# Vascular Ring

1. Encircles trachea and esophagus
   1. Compression of trachea may cause Stridor
   2. Compression of esophagus may cause Dysphagia, Vomiting or Recurrent food impactions
2. Congenital vascular malformation

Note: Dysphagia to solids and liquids --> Motility issue (Achalasia, ALS, etc)
Dysphagia to solids and then progressive dysphagia to liquids --> Obstructive (Cancer, stricture, etc)

# Dysphagia: Motility

## Achalasia

1. CF
   1. Post-stricture dilatation
   2. Triad: Incomplete LES relaxation, Increased LES tone, Aperistalsis
   3. Primary Achalasia vs. Pseudoachalasia differentiated with Endoscopy
      1. Primary Achalasia
         1. Endoscopy: Normal appearing esophageal mucosa, dilated esophagus with possible residual material
         2. May pass endoscope through lower esophageal sphincter
      2. Pseudoachalasia
         1. Endoscopy: Unable to pass endoscope through lower esophageal sphincter
         2. Tobacco use (Adenocarcinoma)
         3. Alcohol use (Squamous cell carcinoma)
         4. Significant weight loss, rapid onset <6mo and presentation at age >60yo
         5. Tumor metastasis or local involvement may give radiologic appearance of "widened mediastinum"
2. Esophagography "shows small leak of contrast from distal esophagus into left chest, what should be done in addition to Abx"
   1. Operative repair of esophageal injury
3. Complication: Esophageal perforation is cause for emergent surgical repair
4. Tx
   1. Surgical: Laparoscopic myotomy and pneumatic balloon dilation
   2. Medical: Botulinum toxin injection, Nitrates, CCB

## Zenker's diverticulum: Pharyngoesophageal diverticulum

1. PP: Abnormal spasm or diminished relaxation of cricopharyngeal muscles during swallowing = cricopharyngeal motor dysfunction
   1. Increased intraluminal pressure above cricopharyngeus muscle causes herniation of the mucosa --> pseudodiverticulum (zone of muscle weakness between inferior pharyngeal constrictor fibers and cricopharyngeus muscle)
2. CF
   1. Coughing during meals
   2. "Gurgly" voice
   3. Dysphagia, halitosis, undigested food regurgitation
3. Dx: Contrast esophagography
4. Tx: Cricopharyngeal myotomy +/- diverticulectomy
5. Complication: Aspiration of regurgitated food --> Recurrent Aspiration Pneumonia

## Two main forms of dysphagia:

- Oropharyngeal dysphagia: Difficulty initiating swallowing associated with coughing, choking, aspiration or nasal regurgitation
  - Often secondary to stroke, dementia, oropharyngeal malignancy or neuromuscular disorder
  - Dysarthria (difficult articulation of speech that is normal linguistically) with normal on exam
  - Work-up with videofluoroscopic modified barium swallow (swallow study)
- Esophageal dysphagia: Sensation of food getting stuck in esophagus a few seconds after swallowing
  - Dysphagia to solids and liquids --> Likely motility issue --> Barium swallow or esophageal manometry
  - Dysphagia to solids progressing to liquids --> Likely esophageal obstruction (malignancy, stricture, esophageal web) --> Upper endoscopy >> Barium swallow

# Esophagitis

## Pill-induced (Abrupt onset)

1. CF: retrosternal chest pain, pain with swallowing, epigastric burning
2. Endoscopy: circumferential deep ulceration with relatively normal surrounding mucosa in middle third of the esophagus
   1. Occurs in middle third due to compression by the aortic arch or enlarged left atrium
3. Abx: Tetracyclines
4. Anti-inflam: ASA, NSAIDs
5. Bisphosphonates: Alendronate, Risedronate
6. Others: Potassium chloride, Iron

## Infectious

1. Candida: White plaques, oral thrush
2. CMV: Large linear ulcers
3. HSV: Vesicles and round/ovoid ulcers

## Eosinophilic

1. Food impaction: progressive esophageal inflammation from untreated EoE causes strictures; may lead to esophageal perforation and requires urgent upper endoscopy with food removal
2. Young M with other atopic disorders (atopic dermatitis, allergic rhinitis, asthma), inability to tolerate liquids (vomiting after drinking water), refractory heartburn despite PPI
3. Superficial Eosinophilic epithelial infiltration at sites far from GE junction
4. Dx: Esophageal biopsy shows eosinophilic mucosal infiltration
5. Tx: Eliminate diet, Glucocorticoid therapy, Stricture dilation

## GERD

1. Barrett Esophagus
   1. Direct precursor to cancer; more genetically similar to Adenocarcinoma than normal esophageal epithelium
   2. Defining feature of intestinal metaplasia is Goblet cells (mucous vacuoles)
   3. Dx: Endoscopy
   4. Tx: Esophagectomy (surgery), photodynamic therapy, radiofrequency ablation, endoscopic mucosectomy

# Esophageal perforation

1. Etiology
   1. Instrumentation — Endoscopy, Trauma
   2. Effort rupture — Boerhaave syndrome repeated vomiting
   3. Esophagitis — Infectious/Pills/Caustic
2. CF
   1. Constant chest/back pain +/- epigastric pain, fever
   2. Hamman sign: crunching upon palpation of thorax due to subcutaneous emphysema
   3. Pleural effusion with green fluid
      1. Particularly on the left due to intrinsic weakness in the left posterolateral aspect of the distal intrathoracic esophagus
3. Dx
   1. Esophagography with water-soluble contrast: leak from perforation — barium enema is not mediastinum-fluid friendly
   2. CXR or CT: widened mediastinum, pneumomediastinum, pneumothorax, pleural effusion
      1. Perforation allows GI contents to leak from esophagus into mediastinum (widened mediastinum)
      2. And/or into pleural space (pleural effusion)
      3. GI contents cause severe chest pain and inflammatory response that can quickly progress to septic shock
   3. CT: esophageal wall thickening, mediastinal fluid accumulation
4. Management
   1. NPO, IV Antibiotics, PPI
   2. Emergency surgical consultation
      1. Surgical exploration with debridement of mediastinum and perforation closure
      2. EP is life threatening that can progress to mediastinitis and septic shock
5. Complication: Boerhaave Syndrome
   1. Full thickness tear, alcoholics, left posterolateral aspect of distal esophagus
   2. Retrosternal chest pain, radiates to L shoulder
   3. Upper Endoscopy, Alcoholics
6. Complication: Mediastinitis

# Esophageal Cancer

## Adenocarcinoma — distal 1/3

1. RF: Barrett esophagus, GERD, Smoking, Obesity, Previous radiation
2. TP53 mutation early stages
3. May invade adjacent Gastric cardia

## Squamous Cell Carcinoma — middle 1/3

1. RF:
   1. Smoking, Alcohol, Poverty, Esophageal injury, Achalasia, Plummer-Vinson, Hot beverages, Previous radiation
   2. Non smoking areas: Polycyclic hydrocarbons, Nitrosamines, Fungus-contaminated foods
   3. HPV in high risk regions
   4. Tracheoesophageal Fistula-led aspiration of food
2. Strictures; protrude into lumen and cause obstruction; infiltrate wall and cause luminal narrowing
3. Invade local structures: Respiratory tree --> pneumonia, Aorta --> Exsanguination; Mediastinum or Pericardium

## Diagnosis

1. Endoscopy with biopsy
2. CT (PET/CT) is used for staging

## Lymph node metastasis

1. Upper 1/3: Cervical
2. Middle 1/3: Mediastinal, Paratracheal, Tracheobronchial
3. Lower 1/3: Gastric and Celiac
