# OBSTRUCTIVE LUNG DISEASE

## Asthma — Eosinophil-mediated inflammation

1. Differentiate Asthma/COPD — "Spirometry before and after inhaled bronchodilator" complete reversal of obstruction
2. Underlying cause — Mast cell activation
   1. Triggered by allergen often — worsening of symptoms at night, seasonal change, exercise or smoke exposure
   2. Mast cells produce histamine and leukotrienes in response to allergen that can cause bronchial hyperactivity
   3. End expiratory wheezes
   4. CXR: no abnormalities

### AERD: Aspirin-exacerbated Respiratory Disease

1. Due to Leukotriene overproduction
2. Classic triad: asthma, chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyposis and NSAID sensitivity (anaphylactoid rxn)
3. C4, D4, E4 cause bronchospasm and increased permeability
4. B4 causes chemotaxis
5. Tx:
   1. Replace ASA with Clopidogrel (P2Y12 inhibitor) if needed
   2. LTRA: especially effective due to central role of leukotrienes in AERD

### Intermittent Asthma

1. (i.e. cold/heat triggered, exercise, upper airway infection) due to — Leukocyte-induced bronchoconstriction
   1. Smooth muscle cells depolarize causing bronchoconstriction
   2. Goblet cells activate promoting mucus hyper-secretion
2. Exercise: Hyperventilation --> incomplete heating/humidification of air --> cooler, dry air triggers mast cells degranulation and bronchospasm
   1. Dx
      1. Empiric response to pre exercise bronchodilator
      2. Bronchoprovocation testing (spirometry before and after exercise) showing >=15% decline in FEV1
   2. Management
      1. Inhaled CST — Fluticasone and Beta agonist 10 minutes before exercise
      2. LTRA 2 hours prior (preferred in athletes with daily symptoms)

### Occupational Asthma

1. Dx: 2 step
   1. Confirm Asthma (reversible obstruction on spirometry or methacholine bronchoprovocation)
   2. Confirm workplace-specific worsening of airflow obstruction (serial peak expiratory flow rate [PEF] measurements via portable peak flow meter)

### Comorbid GERD

1. GERD can exacerbate asthma symptoms via micro aspiration of gastric contents --> increased Vagal tone and Bronchial reactivity
2. GERD sx: sore throat, morning hoarseness, worsening cough only at night, increased need for albuterol inhaler following meals
3. Tx: PPI trial — Esomeprazole

### Exacerbation

1. Acute exacerbation
   1. Acute worsening of symptoms (cough, wheezing, dyspnea) + significant decline in expiratory flow rate
   2. Occurs when sensitized bronchioles are exposed to a trigger (URI, Allergens) --> mast cell degranulation
2. Acute exacerbation management — Albuterol + Prednisone +/- Ipratropium
   1. Initial: Relax bronchoconstriction
      1. SABA: Inhaled Albuterol — bronchodilator
      2. Anticholinergic: Inhaled Ipratropium bromide — bronchodilator, ONLY WITH ALBUTEROL, ONLY ACUTE SETTING
      3. Glucocorticoid: Prednisone, Methylprednisolone, Dexamethasone (PO/IV) — multi dosing for late-phase inflammation control, delayed effect (6hr)
         1. **Note: CST given with bronchodilators in ER because they take several hours to provide anti-inflammatory effect
         2. CSTs target late phase reaction that occurs hours later (de novo synthesis of Th2 cytokines and further recruitment of leukocytes --> worsens ongoing bronchospasm and inflammation)
   2. Hours later: CST continued at home to prevent relapse
      1. Oral Prednisone
3. Severe Exacerbation indicator — "Normal PaCO2 on ABG"
   1. Asthma exacerbation — inc RR — Hyperventilation — dec PaCO2 = Resp alkalosis --> respiratory muscle fatigue = dec RR = respiratory collapse and normal/high PaCO2
4. Greatest risk factor for severe and life-threatening asthma — "Prior history of intubation" +/- ICU admission
   1. Signs of severe exacerbation = chest wall retractions
   2. RR >30bpm
   3. Hypoxia <90%
   4. Peak expiratory flow <50%

### Stepwise Management

1. SABA: Albuterol
2. LABA: Fluticasone/Salmeterol (CST/LABA combo), Formoterol
3. ICS: Beclomethasone, Fluticasone, Prednisone, Methylprednisolone, Dexamethasone (PO/IV)
   1. Chronic beclomethasone complication — Thrush
4. LTRA: Montelukast
5. Anticholinergic
   1. Short acting: Ipratropium
   2. Long acting: Tiotropium
6. Intermittent
   1. <=2 symptoms per week; <=2 night awakening per month
   2. SABA
7. Mild Persistent
   1. 3-6 symptoms per week; 3-4 night awakening per month (1 per week)
   2. SABA + Add low dose corticosteroid (Fluticasone)
8. Moderate Persistent
   1. Daily symptoms; 1-3 nighttime awakenings per week (4-12 per month)
   2. SABA + low dose corticosteroid + Add LABA (Salmeterol)
9. Severe Persistent Asthma (Adults)
   1. Criteria
      1. Nocturnal waking with asthma on any night
      2. Symptoms requiring reliever inhaler use on most days
      3. Reduced FEV1 (while on bronchodilator therapy)
   2. Tx: Scheduled Inhaled CST + LABA (formoterol) +/- Omalizumab (Anti-IgE antibody) or Oral corticosteroid

Note: ICS (ex: Fluticasone) is the most effective controller medication and is included in all regimens for persistent asthma

## Bronchiectasis

1. CF:
   1. PP: Airway insult with impaired bacterial clearance
   2. Chronic: daily production of voluminous, thick +/- blood-tinged +/- mucopurulent mucus
   3. Acute exacerbations: recurrent infections with mucopurulent sputum +/- frank hemoptysis
2. Etiologies:
   1. Airway obstruction (cancer, foreign body)
   2. Mucostasis
      1. Cystic Fibrosis
         1. MCC in young adults — often diagnosed after recurrent pulmonary infections
         2. Enhanced neutrophil recruitment and excessive release of elastase contributed to airway damage
         3. Pseudomonas aeruginosa classically found
         4. Upper lung lobe involvement classically due to CF
      2. Allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis
         1. Patient with asthma or cystic fibrosis who presents with recurrent fleeting infiltrates associated with eosinophilia due to hypersensitivity response to Aspergillus
         2. Tx: months of itraconazole and oral steroids
      3. Kartagener syndrome: impaired mucus clearance
         1. Autosomal Recessive
         2. Genetic ciliary disorder comprising the triad of situs inversus, chronic sinusitis, and bronchiectasis
   3. Immunodeficiency: Low Ig
   4. Autoinflammatory: Sjogren syndrome
   5. Chronic/past infection (Mycobacteria)
   6. Toxic inhalation
3. Chest Imaging may show any of the following
   1. Linear atelectasis or scarring
   2. Dilated airways with thickened walls
   3. Peripheral opacities related to multifocal mucous plugs
4. Evaluation:
   1. High-resolution CT of chest (diagnosis): airway dilation
   2. PFT: obstructive pattern
   3. Investigation of etiology (Ig levels, respiratory cultures, bronchial obstruction)
5. Tx:
   1. Address underlying disorder (ex: Ig replacement)
   2. Airway clearance (chest physiotherapy), mucolytics
   3. Antibiotics to suppress bacterial overgrowth and treat exacerbations

## COPD — Neutrophil-mediated inflammation

1. Higher work to get the next breath in due to difficulty getting the previous breath out (hyperinflation)
2. DLCO measures gas transfer between alveoli and pulmonary capillary blood
3. CF
   1. Markedly increased chest AP diameter
   2. Bilaterally decreased breath sounds with scattered expiratory wheezes
   3. Decreased Expiratory flow rate FEV1/FVC <0.7 — Air trapped during expiration — increased Residual Volume, Total Lung Volumes
   4. Increased lung distensibility (alveolar-capillary membrane destruction increases lung distensibility and compliance)
   5. Decreased Vital Capacity (in severe disease)
   6. Decreased Diaphragm force
   7. Chronic Cough, Erythrocytosis, Right-axis deviation
      1. Chronic productive cough should make you think of chronic bronchitis (form of COPD) vs bronchiectasis (chronic infections, cystic fibrosis, etc.)
      2. COPD --> Hypoxemia --> Decreased O2 sensed by kidneys --> Increased EPO production --> Erythrocytosis (Polycythemia)
      3. COPD --> Low oxygen tension throughout many lung fields --> Diffuse vasoconstriction in pulmonary vasculature (to try to shunt blood to better oxygenated areas of lung, unique aspect of pulmonary vasculature) --> Increased resistance faced by right ventricle (i.e. pulmonary hypertension) --> RV hypertrophy --> Right-axis deviation

### Emphysema — "Pink puffer" — Decreased DLCO, Decreased elasticity

1. PP: Terminal airway destruction and dilation
2. CF: Thin, wasted appearance with pursed lips, minimal cough
   1. Lung emptying slowed due to loss of elastic recoil and premature expiratory airway closure --> air trapping --> flattened diaphragm (already flattened diaphragm cannot contract further = decreased inspiratory flow)
   2. Decreased Diaphragmatic force
   3. Decreased Inspiratory flow
   4. Decreased Chest wall recoil
   5. Decreased preload
      1. Hyperinflation --> rise in intrathoracic pressure — limits venous return to the R heart
      2. Emphysema causes capillary bed destruction --> increased Pulmonary vascular resistance — decreased LV filling (preload) --> RV dysfunction = Cor Pulmonale --> Peripheral LE edema
   6. Increased lung compliance (elastance is the inverse of compliance, loss of alveolar elastin causes loose and baggy lungs)
   7. Late hypercarbia/hypoxia
3. Alpha-1-antitrypsin (AAT) deficiency
   1. PP: Insufficient inhibition of neutrophil elastase --> Loss of elastin in the lung matrix
   2. Presentation
      1. COPD at young age <45yo; COPD with minimal smoking
      2. CXR: BASILAR-PROMINENT COPD "INCREASED LUCENCY AT LUNG BASES" (Vs. Centriacinar that is in upper lung lobes)
      3. Unexplained liver disease
      4. "40yo nonsmoker with dyspnea, cough and sputum production"
   3. CXR: Hyperinflation
   4. CXR: Obliteration of alveolar septa and decreased lung tissue density (hyperlucency) and bullae
   5. Dx: Serum AAT levels
   6. Tx: IV supplementation pooled human AAT

### Chronic Bronchitis — "Blue bloater" — Normal DLCO

1. Productive cough >3 months for 2 years
2. Overweight, edematous
3. Early hypercarbia/hypoxia
4. CXR: Prominent thickened bronchovascular markings

### Acute Exacerbation (AECOPD)

1. CF:
   1. Diffuse bronchial obstruction due to increased production of purulent sputum
   2. Diffuse rhonchi and wheezing
2. Management
   1. Supp O2
   2. Combination inhaled bronchodilators: SABA (B2 agonist) + Anticholinergic
   3. Systemic glucocorticoid
      1. Glucocorticoids improve hypoxemia, dec inflammation, risk of relapse, treatment failure and length of stay
      2. "Methylprednisolone" Oral prednisone or IV methyl for 5 days
   4. Antibiotics
      1. Antibiotics indicated if increased sputum purulence (+ change in cough from baseline) and mechanical ventilation requirement
      2. Macrolides/Azithromycin, Fluoro/Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin, Penicillin/Amoxicillin-Clavulanate
      3. Azithromycin: Abx effect + inhibit neutrophil-mediated airway inflammation
      4. Culture only if P.aeruginosa suspected due to difficulty isolating single bacterial pathogen
3. Severe exacerbation (respiratory failure, severe hypoxemia or respiratory acidosis, AMS)
   1. Noninvasive positive-pressure ventilation: Decreases mortality, rate of intubation hospital length of stay and incidence of nosocomial infections
   2. NPPV fails --> Endotracheal intubation with mechanical ventilation

### Acute Exacerbation with Altered Mental Status

1. Raises suspicion for symptomatic hypercapnia --> evaluated with arterial blood gas analysis
2. COPD patients retain CO2 during exacerbation due to shallow breathing and high levels of ventilation-perfusion mismatch
3. Supplemental O2 may encourage hypercapnia due to worsening ventilation-perfusion mismatch and depression of respiratory drive
4. CF (CO2 >75-80mmHg): Delirium, confusion, lethargy

### Oxygen-induced CO2 Retention in COPD

1. PP: Advanced COPD causes destruction of terminal bronchioles and alveoli causing physiologic dead space development
   1. Affected regions have limited surface area available for gas exchange --> V/Q mismatch --> Local Hypoxia and Hypercapnia
   2. Hypoxia induces selective vasoconstriction in the affected lung area and redirects blood flow to better ventilated alveoli, reducing V/Q mismatch
2. Role of supplemental O2
   1. Supplemental oxygen improves hypoxia but causes CO2 retention by:
      1. Loss of compensatory vasoconstriction in areas of ineffective gas exchange worsens V/Q mismatch (increased dead space perfusion)
      2. Increase in oxyhemoglobin reduces uptake of CO2 from the tissues by Haldane effect (decreased affinity of oxyhemoglobin for CO2)
      3. Decrease in respiratory drive and slowed respiratory rate causes decreased minute ventilation (decreased alveolar ventilation)
   2. Acidosis caused by acute increase in CO2 increased brain GABA and Glutamine and decreased brain glutamate and aspartate --> change in consciousness
   3. Hypercapnia causes reflex cerebral vasodilation --> seizures
3. Oxygen cautiously used with a goal of SaO2 of 90-93% or PaO2 60-70mmHg
4. Patients with severe acidosis or severely reduced levels of consciousness require mechanical ventilation

### Chronic COPD Management

1. Management
   1. Prolonged Survival: Smoking cessation, Supplemental Oxygen (specifically SpO2 <88%), Lung reduction surgery (certain patients)
   2. Vaccines: Pneumococcal vaccine, Influenza vaccine
   3. Inhaled bronchodilators
      1. SABA: Albuterol
      2. LABA: Salmeterol
      3. Anticholinergic: Ipratropium (short acting), Tiotropium (long acting)
         1. Except for low risk/low severity patients, initial therapy for COPD includes daily LAMA (Tiotropium)
         2. LAMAs block airway M3 receptors to stabilize against bronchoconstriction and reduce mucus hypersecretion
      4. *Severe presentation/Refractory: LABA + LAMA + Inhaled CST
         1. Inhaled CST reserved for refractory because they increase risk of pneumonia by causing local pulmonary immunosuppression
      5. LAMAs >>> LABAs in COPD
2. Long-term Oxygen Therapy (LTOT) — maximal survival improvement
   1. Chronic hypoxemia causes secondary polycythemia (Elevated Hct 56%)
   2. Eligibility: PaO2<55mmHg or SaO2 <88%; PaO2<59 or SaO2<89% in Cor Pulmonale, RHF or Hct >55% (Polycythemia)
   3. *Note: Supplemental O2 can worsen hypercapnia by decreasing respiratory drive. Goal O2 saturation is 90-93%

### Theophylline Interactions

1. Decreased clearance with Ciprofloxacin, Cimetidine, Erythro/Clarithromycin, Verapamil
2. Toxicity: CNS stimulation (headache, insomnia, seizures), GI disturbance, Cardiac toxicity (arrhythmia)

### Complication: Pneumonia

1. Due to chronic inflammation and chronic use of CST
2. CF: Fever, productive cough, chest pain, breathlessness

### Complication: Secondary Spontaneous Pneumothorax

1. Cause: Rupture of alveolar blebs
2. CF: Rapid worsening — sudden onset dyspnea, unilateral chest pain, ipsilateral decreased tactile fremitus and breath sounds, hyperresonance
   1. Tracheal deviation more common with primary spontaneous pneumothorax
3. Dx: CXR — shows visceral pleural line beyond which no pulmonary markings are apparent
4. Management:
   1. Small: O2 and monitor with repeat CXR
      1. Monitoring important in patients with thoracostomy because simple pneumothorax may progress to tension pneumothorax
   2. Large: Thoracostomy

### Postoperative Complication Risk

1. Decreased with immediate smoking cessation
2. Significant improvement if cessation >4-8wk prior, otherwise not enough time for airway inflammation reduction
