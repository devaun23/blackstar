# VENOUS THROMBOEMBOLISM (DVT, PE)

## Causes: Thrombosis

1. Trauma, Traveling
2. Hospitalization
   1. Hormones: Exogenous Estrogen — OCP, Tamoxifen, Estrogen replacement therapy
3. Relative (family history) — F5 Leiden
4. Old age
5. Malignancy
6. Long bone fractures
7. Obstetrics, Obesity
8. Surgery, Smoking
9. Immobilization
10. Other sickness:
    1. Antiphospholipid Syndrome
    2. Nephrotic Syndrome
    3. Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria

## Modified Wells DVT Criteria (pretest probability of DVT)

1. Criteria (+1 for each)
   1. Calf swelling: increase in circumference >=3cm
   2. Swelling of entire leg
   3. Localized tenderness of affected leg
   4. Swollen unilateral superficial veins
   5. Unilateral pitting edema
   6. Paresis or recent casting of affected limb
   7. Hx of prior DVT
   8. Hx of paralysis
   9. Hx of being bedridden for 3+ days
   10. Active cancer
   11. (-2) if alternative diagnosis likely
2. Score:
   1. 0-1: low (Next step --> D-dimer)
      1. D-dimer (fibrin degradation product)
         1. High sensitivity, low specificity
         2. (-) D-dimer excludes DVT
   2. >=2: high (Next step --> Duplex Ultrasound)
      1. Initial: Duplex Ultrasound
      2. Gold standard: Venography

## Modified Wells PE Criteria

1. (Eat Chips)
   1. Edema in leg or any other symptom of DVT: 3
   2. Alternative diagnosis less likely: 3
   3. Tachycardia: 1.5
   4. Cancer: 1
   5. Hemoptysis: 1
   6. Immobilization: 1.5
   7. Previous history of DVT or PE: 1.5
   8. Major surgery in past month: 1.5
2. Score:
   1. <=4 unlikely
   2. >4 likely

## DVT

1. RF: Stasis — Long flight, Post-op immobility; Hypercoaguable state — Malignancy, OCP; Endothelial damage — Smoking, HTN, Atherosclerosis, Injury
2. CF
   1. Homan sign: calf pain on foot dorsiflexion
3. Tx
   1. 1st: Heparin — Enoxaparin
      1. Initially given heparin and then bridged to warfarin to reduce likelihood of warfarin-induced skin necrosis due to selective inhibition of proteins C and S (anti-thrombotic proteins) seen with initial warfarin administration
   2. Thrombolytics (alteplase)
      1. Only given in a patient with DVT and signs/symptoms concerning for a PE (chest pain, dyspnea, hypoxia, tachycardia)
   3. IVC filters are generally unfavored (become infected or become a nidus for clot formation)
      1. Indicated when anticoagulants are contraindicated — history of massive GI bleed, hemorrhagic stroke, etc
4. Complications: Postthrombotic Syndrome
   1. Most cases arise within 2 years of thrombus
   2. Epi: Chronic venous insufficiency following acute DVT
      1. Venous valve damage due to inflammatory mediators and venous lumen stenosis due to wall remodeling --> Venous HTN distal to the site of the previous thrombus
      2. Increases venous pressure causes clinical features
   3. CF:
      1. Leg edema, fatigue, pain, superficial venous dilation, venous stasis ulcers
      2. Worse at the end of the day
   4. Evaluation:
      1. Doppler US to rule out acute DVT
   5. Tx
      1. Exercise
      2. Compression

## Pulmonary Embolism

1. Clinical Features
   1. Classic features: Acute onset dyspnea with pleuritic chest pain
   2. Tachycardia, tachypnea, hypoxemia, unilateral lower extremity swelling (DVT)
      1. LUNGS CLEAR TO AUSCULTATION EARLY IN THE COURSE
   3. Bilateral wheezing (acute phase)
      1. Hypoxia and infarction --> cytokine-induced bronchoconstriction
   4. Pleuritic chest pain in setting of long flight — Pulmonary infarction
      1. Small peripheral thrombi cause pleuritic chest pain
   5. Hemoptysis
      1. Due to inflammation and irritation of lung parenchyma and adjacent visceral and parietal pleura
   6. Low grade fever
   7. Jugular venous distention
2. Severe PE cardiac output affected
   1. Right Ventricular Dysfunction
   2. Hypotension, Syncope and possibly Acute Right Heart failure
   3. Prepulmonary Obstructive Shock: increased CVP (increased jugular venous pressure), SVR, decreased PCWP, Cardiac index, SvO2
   4. Note: Right Ventricular MI also presents with RV dysfunction
      1. RV MI: Bradycardia, arrhythmia
      2. RV PE: Dyspnea, syncope
3. Complication: Pleural effusion
   1. Hemorrhage or inflammation --> exudative and grossly bloody
   2. Pain due to pleural irritation
   3. Leukocytosis +/- peripheral neutrophilia seen (as with any inflammatory process)
4. Complication: Atrial fibrillation
   1. Can be
      1. Due to PE (right atrium enlargement or increased sympathetic tone)
      2. Result in PE (right atrial appendage thrombus embolization into pulmonary circulation)
5. Complication: Hypocalcemia
   1. Pulmonary Emboli-caused hyperventilation --> Respiratory alkalosis --> increased extracellular pH
   2. H+ dissociates from Albumin and more Ca molecules bind Albumin --> decreased ionized Ca
   3. CF: Paresthesias, carpopedal spasm
   4. Also seen with DVT (hyperventilation induced)
6. V/Q Mismatch
   1. Leads to increased Alveolar (A)-Arterial (a) gradient due to impaired gas exchange (PaO2, partial arterial pressure of O2 significantly reduced)
7. Diagnosis
   1. CT Pulmonary angiography CTPA
      1. PE Unlikely --> D-dimer — <500 (PE excluded) or >500 (CT pulm angio)
      2. PE Likely -->
         1. Renal insufficiency: V/Q scan — Normal --> CT Scan (Still get CT because high PE probability although V/Q normal)
         2. Renal working: CT pulm angio — Negative (PE excluded) or Positive (PE confirmed)
   2. CXR is frequently abnormal but has poor sensitivity and specificity, and may be normal (due to dual pulmonary blood supply)
      1. Hampton hump:
         1. Peripheral, wedge-shaped opacification adjacent to the pleural surface
         2. Represents pulmonary infarction distal to a pulmonary embolus
      2. Westermark sign:
         1. Dilation of pulmonary arteries proximal to embolus
         2. Collapse of distal vasculature creates sharp cutoff
   3. ECG: aids in exclusion of acute coronary syndrome or acute pericarditis
      1. PE finding
         1. Sinus Tachycardia
         2. Non-specific ST Segment
         3. T-wave changes
         4. New RBBB
         5. S1Q3T3 (right heart strain)
8. Management
   1. Low pretest probability --> D-dimer
   2. High pretest probability --> Anticoagulation
      1. 1st: Initial treatment
         1. Renal dysfunction (GFR <30mL/min) --> Unfractionated Heparin
         2. Renal function intact --> LMWH/Enoxaparin, Injection 10a-I/Fondaparinux, Oral 10a-I/Rivaroxaban
      2. 2nd: CT Pulmonary Angiogram
         1. Avoid in Chronic kidney disease or contrast allergy --> V/Q scan
      3. 3rd: Establish Hemodynamic stability
         1. Hemodynamic unstable: Hypovolemic shock, Right heart strain on Echo
            1. Thrombolytic — TPA
               1. Risk of hemorrhage: assess with non contrast Head CT if patient experiences headache
            2. If anticoagulation contraindicated — Pulmonary embolectomy
         2. Hemodynamic stable
            1. Continue anticoagulation
            2. If anticoagulation contraindicated — IVC filter
   3. Maintenance therapy
      1. NOACs
      2. Cancer or Pregnant --> Heparin
   4. Early Pregnancy
      1. CTPA preferred over V/Q scan
9. Poor Prognosis
   1. Associated with Atrial fibrillation and hypoxemia
10. Postoperative anticoagulation or mechanical prophylaxis can reduce the incidence of DVT and PE
    1. Anticoagulation: Warfarin vs. Aspirin
    2. DVT: unilateral leg pain and swelling, positive Homan's sign (painful passive flexion at ankle), untreated leads to PE
    3. PE: tachypnea, hypoxemia, ACUTE RESPIRATORY ALKALOSIS
11. DVT vs PE
    1. DVT: Confirm diagnosis with Compression Ultrasonography 1st
       1. If patient noncompliant with Warfarin (missing INR appointments) — Switch to F10-I: Rivaroxaban, Apixaban
    2. PE: Begin empiric anticoagulation if high pretest probability
