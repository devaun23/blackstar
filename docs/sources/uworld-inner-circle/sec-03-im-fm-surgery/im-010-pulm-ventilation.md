# High Altitude Illness

1. "Increased urinary excretion of HCO3-"
   1. Low PiO2 — (+Peripheral chemo by low O2)
   2. Hyperventilation — decreased PaCO2 — increased pH — (+Central chemo by high pH) = Ventilation inhibited and increased PaO2 limited <-- accelerated by Acetazolamide and ventilation preserved

# Mechanical Ventilation = RR x VT

1. Respiratory acidosis treatment — Increase Respiratory Rate
   1. Hypercapnia correction involves: Increasing RR or Increasing VT
   2. Increasing RR preferred
   3. Excessive VT (>6-8mL/kg) increases risk of pulmonary barotrauma
2. Respiratory Alkalosis treatment — Decrease Respiratory Rate
   1. Ventilator delivers set TV (6ml/kg of ideal body weight)
   2. Respiratory alkalosis occurs due to hyperventilation therefore decreasing RR will adjust CO2 levels
3. Arterial oxygenation
   1. Affected by FiO2 (fraction of inspired oxygen) and PEEP (positive end-expiratory pressure)
4. Complication: Collapse of IVC
   1. Positive Pressure: Causes acute increase in intrathoracic pressure which could cause collapse of IVC
      1. In the setting of decreased CVP (hypovolemic shock), mechanical ventilation may cause acute loss of RV preload, loss of CO and Cardiac arrest
   2. Provide hemodynamic support (epinephrine) is provided
5. Avoid complications of mechanical ventilation by using
   1. Low tidal volume = low pulmonary pressure — less likely to over distend alveolar and improves mortality
   2. Prevention SpO2<88%
   3. Low TV, high PEEP, high FiO2 (<60%) **PEEP levels are elevated if FiO2 is already at 60%
   4. Note: Positive pressure ventilation (mechanical ventilation) decreases venous return to the heart — can cause CV collapse in hypotensive patient unless Respiratory Acidosis
6. Ventilator-associated pneumonia — ARDS requires Ventilation --> VA-Pneumonia --> May cause upper lobe Abscess
   1. Confirmation requires identification of organism in lower respiratory tract
   2. Reduce risk
      1. Elevating head of bed to 30-45 degrees
      2. Suctioning subglottic secretions
      3. Limiting endotracheal tube movement
      4. Avoiding gastric acid suppression (unless high risk of stress ulcers)

# Hypercapnia — Causes

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Decreased Respiratory Drive | Drugs (Opioids: Antidote — Naloxone; Benzo: Antidote — Flumazenil, toxicity worse with Alcohol), CNS trauma/stroke/encephalitis |
| Dec Respiratory Neuromuscular function | Spinal cord lesion, ALS (Noninvasive PPV used, home oxygen therapy avoided due to risk of respiratory drive suppression), Myasthenia Gravis |
| Dec Thoracic cage or pleural function | Obesity hypoventilation syndrome, Pneumothorax, Rib fracture/flail chest |
| Airway obstruction | OSA (upper airway), COPD (lower airway) |
| Impaired gas exchange | Cardiogenic pulmonary edema, Interstitial lung disease |

## Benzodiazepines

1. Bind GABA-A-R, inc inhibitory effect in CNS --> depresses Central respiratory drive in overdose, worse with Alcohol
2. Acute OD management: Protect airway, Flumazenil (compet antag) but can potentiate seizures if tolerant to Benzo
