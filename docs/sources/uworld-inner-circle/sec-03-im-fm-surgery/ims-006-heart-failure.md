# HEART FAILURE

## Systolic Heart Failure (HFrEF)

1. EF <55%
2. Laboratory
   1. Hyponatremia — parallels severity of heart failure, independent predictor of adverse outcomes
      1. CHF → low CO → decreased perfusion → neurohumoral activation → increased Renin, NE and ADH
      2. ADH binds V2-R → promotes water reabsorption → DILUTIONAL HYPONATREMIA
      3. Treatment: FLUID RESTRICTION, ACE-I, LOOP DIURETICS
   2. Elevated pro-BNP levels
   3. Renal insufficiency
3. Associated: Anemia, A fibrillation, Diabetes mellitus
4. Diagnosis
   1. ECG: QRS duration >120 msec, Left BBB pattern
   2. Echo: Severe LV dysfunction, concomitant diastolic dysfunction, reduced RV function, pulmonary HTN
5. Improved Survival
   1. BB: Carvedilol, Metoprolol succinate, Bisoprolol
   2. ACE-I
   3. ARB
   4. MRAs: Spironolactone, Eplerenone
      1. Indicated in persistent HF symptoms despite BB, ACE-I, ARB
      2. LV EF <40% with recent STEMI and symptomatic HF
   - Note: HCTZ, Metolazone and Furosemide used in symptomatic HF but do not improve LT survival

## Preserved EF Heart Failure (HFpEF)

1. EF >55%, Due to progressive remodeling
2. RF: Long-standing HTN + Tobacco use; Obesity and sedentary lifestyle with Chronic HTN
3. PP:
   1. Chronic HTN → elevated SVR → increased Afterload → stress on LV → Concentric hypertrophy
   2. Concentric hypertrophy worsened by systemic inflammation (obese ± sedentary)
   3. Systemic inflammation → microvascular ischemia → further sarcomere hypertrophy and collagen deposition
   4. Stiff LV = impaired diastolic filling (decreased compliance) = ↓preload → HF (reduced CO)
4. CF: Lung crackles, S3 or S4, cardiomegaly with Kerley B lines
5. ECG: LVH

## Acute Decompensated Left-sided Heart Failure

1. Mechanism
   1. Reduced LV function → reduced CO → arterial baroreceptors and JG apparatus activated
   2. Compensatory SNS and RAAS activation
      1. Na retention → increased Blood Volume → Increased Preload
      2. AT II → Vasoconstriction of AA and EA → Increased Afterload
      3. Increased Preload and Afterload increase strain → further reduce CO → clinical decompensation
2. RF
   1. Amphetamines: Cardiomyopathy (ischemic and non-ischemic mechanism)
      - Dyspnea, hypoxemia, diffuse pulmonary crackles, S3
      - Low CO state — hypotension, cool extremities, diminished pulses
      - Tx: Dobutamine
   2. "Recent chemoradiation" — LV systolic dysfunction due to cardiotoxic effect
3. Clinical Presentation
   1. Acute dyspnea, orthopnea, PND, accessory muscle use
   2. Cardiac asthma — diffuse crackles with possible wheezes
   3. S3, JVD, peripheral edema
   4. Moderate to severe MR
   5. Resting tachycardia, low BP <100/60
   6. Low maximal oxygen consumption (peak VO2)
   7. Note: Serum BNP <100 pg/mL useful in ruling out suspected HF exacerbation
4. Treatment
   1. Hypertension with adequate end-organ perfusion
      1. Supplemental O2 *Noninvasive ventilation preferred over mechanical*
      2. IV loop diuretic
      3. IV vasodilator (nitroglycerin)
   2. Hypotension or signs of shock
      1. Supplemental O2
      2. IV loop diuretic
      3. IV vasopressor (norepinephrine)
   3. Dobutamine (B1-R agonist)
      - Used for severe HF due to LV systolic dysfunction and cardiogenic shock
      - Improved contractility → ↓LV end-systolic volume → ↑CO

## CHF Exacerbation

1. Precipitated by: Infection (URI), NSAIDs (Increased Na retention)
2. CF: Bilateral pulmonary edema and pulmonary infiltrates that improve rapidly with diuretics
   - *Note: CHF exacerbation may mimic pneumonia; pneumonia infiltrates resolve in days-weeks, CHF infiltrates resolve immediately post-diuretics*
   - Note: Lung crackles seen in CHF and interstitial lung disease

## Chronic Heart Failure

1. Hepatojugular reflux differentiates cardiac from liver disease (ascites, splenomegaly, LE edema)
   1. Applying firm, sustained pressure to upper abdomen for 10-15 seconds
   2. Positive response: sustained elevation in JVP >3 cm
   3. Highly specific for elevated Right-sided cardiac pressures and RV failure
      - With RV failure, elevated hydrostatic pressure backs up through IVC, hepatic veins, portal sinusoids
      - Compression forces blood back → failing Right heart cannot handle → blood backs in jugular veins
   4. Right heart failure causes: Constrictive Pericarditis (latent TB activation)
2. Clear lungs help differentiate isolated RV failure from LV failure (pulmonary edema present)

## HIGH-OUTPUT HEART FAILURE

1. Mechanism: Increased O2 consumption and ↓SVR
2. Causes
   1. **Hyperthyroidism**
      1. Hints: Recent palpitations, poor sleep, unintentional weight loss
      2. PP: ↓SVR with ↑SV, CO and Venous return [Wide pulse pressure and prominent PMI]
         1. Thyroid hormone-led vasodilation → Peripheral vasodilation and ↓DBP
         2. Reflexive ↑ in myocardial contractility and HR
         3. ↑ Contractility → ↑SV (wide pulse pressure, ↑SBP) and ↑CO → hyperdynamic circulation ("prominent apical PMI and 2/6 pulmonic flow murmur")
         4. ↑CO against reduced SVR → blood returns quickly → ↑venous return
         5. LV eventually cannot sustain ↑venous return → decompensated HF (pulmonary edema, peripheral edema)
   2. **Severe anemia**
      1. Heart must increase CO through High HR and SV to deliver same O2 content
      2. SOB, fatigue, tachycardia, tachypnea, JVD, bilateral basilar crackles, S3 gallop, ejection murmur, LE edema → HF + dark stools and Hb of 5g/dL = high-output HF
   3. **Thiamine deficiency — Wet Beriberi**
