# VALVULAR HEART DISEASE

## Aortic Stenosis

### Supravalvular AS

1. Murmur: Loud mid systolic at right 1st ICS (higher than valvular AS)
2. Congenital LV outflow tract obstruction due to narrowing of ascending aorta
3. LV hypertrophy over time + coronary artery stenosis can be associated
4. Exercise: above factors + increased O2 demand → subendocardial or myocardial ischemia → "substernal chest pain during exercise"
5. Presentation: Unequal carotid pulse, differential BP in UE, palpable thrill in suprasternal notch

### Valvular AS

1. Bicuspid Aortic valve: Young patient, crescendo-decrescendo at RIGHT UPPER STERNAL BORDER, S4
2. Senile Calcific: Outflow obstruction → dyspnea on exertion, fatigue, exertional syncope
   - Fixed outflow obstruction → cannot increase CO during exercise-induced vasodilation → hypotension, transient cerebral hypoperfusion
3. Severe AS: Pulsus parvus and tardus, Single and soft S2, Mid-to-late peaking systolic murmur radiating to carotids

Note: Exertional syncope is due to underlying cause — ventricular arrhythmia (MI/infarct) or outflow obstruction (AS, HCM)

### Diagnosis (Severe)

1. Aortic jet velocity >4.0 m/sec
2. Mean transvalvular pressure gradient >40mmHg

### AVR Indications

1. Severe AS & symptomatic (high risk of SCD)
2. LVEF <50%
3. Undergoing other cardiac surgery (CABG)

*Note: patient may be asymptomatic due to sedentary lifestyle; symptoms appear upon exertion*

## Aortic Regurgitation

1. Diastolic + continuous murmurs due to underlying pathology
2. Eccentric hypertrophy increases LV compliance and contractility
   1. Initially in volume overload, LV stretches to increase SV and maintain CO
   2. Chronic: LV adapts with addition of muscle fibers in series → increases compliance and contractility
   3. Long term: maladaptive → increases LV stress → LV contractile dysfunction → decompensated HF
3. Valve abnormality: Left sternal border, 3-4th ICS, leaning forward, holding breath in full expiration

## Mitral Regurgitation

### Severe Chronic MR

1. Severe MR with mitral valve prolapse (primary defect due to myxomatous degeneration)
2. Surgical indications (Primary)
   1. Surgical repair before significant LV dysfunction
      - LV EF overestimated in MR since regurgitant flow accounts for large percentage of SV
   2. Durable valve repair preferred over replacement (lifelong anticoagulation and repeat replacement)
   3. Surgery if LVEF 30%-60% regardless of symptoms
   4. Consider if valve repair highly likely: Asx LVEF >60% — preemptive; Sx LVEF <30% — high risk
3. Secondary (MI, Dilated CM): Medical management

## Infective Endocarditis

### Acute: S. aureus or Strep

1. CF: Fever, Chills, Malaise, Signs of septic emboli (pneumonia, skin changes, septic arthritis)
2. Significant heart valve disruption → acute heart failure
3. Dx: Initial step: Blood Cultures >> Echo to begin prompt Abx

### IV Drug Users

1. S. aureus
2. Septic Pulmonary emboli — Cough, chest pain, hemoptysis, numerous round infiltrates on CXR
   - "Scattered round lesions in peripheral lung fields bilaterally"
3. Brain — Embolic stroke, Cerebral hemorrhage, Brain abscess, Acute encephalopathy or meningoencephalitis
4. Murmur: Tricuspid Regurgitation "Systolic murmur that increases with inspiration"
   - Augmentation with inspiration: 100% sensitivity, 88% specificity
