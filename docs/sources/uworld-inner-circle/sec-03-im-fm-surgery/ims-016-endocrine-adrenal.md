# Primary Adrenal Insufficiency (Adrenal gland, inc ADH)

1. Hormonal imbalance
   1. Loss of glucocorticoid: Hypotension
      1. Fatigue, anorexia can trigger hypoglycemia
      2. Eosinophilia
      3. Cortisol normally (-) ADH --> reduced Cortisol inc ADH --> further dec Na
   2. Loss of mineralocorticoid: Renal Na wasting and potassium retention
      1. inc K, significant hypotension/orthostasis and hyperchloremic acidosis
      2. Hypovolemia activates ADH --> dec Na
   3. Loss of adrenal androgen secretion
   4. High CRH --> High POMC = High ACTH = High MSH = Hyperpigmentation
2. Cause
   1. MCC is Autoimmune adrenalitis
      1. 50% patients have other auto-inflammatory conditions (primary hypothyroidism, vitiligo)
      2. May flare up in postpartum period
   2. Infection: TB
   3. Metastatic infiltration
      1. Breast, Lung, Kidney, Skin
   4. Etomidate (anesthetic)
3. CF: Hyperpigmentation from increased melanocytes, fatigue, hypotension, weight loss
4. Labs:
   1. inc K, dec Na (Renal wasting, Hypovolemia-led ADH release, Low cortisol-led ADH release)
   2. inc ACTH, dec Morning Cortisol
   3. Eosinophilia — reduced migration of eosinophils from bloodstream into tissue due to low cortisol
   4. Normocytic anemia and abdominal tenderness (due to unknown reasons)
5. Acute adrenal crisis
   1. Confusion, Hypotension, shock
6. Dx: Cosyntropin stimulation testing
   1. Synthetic form of ACTH — absence of expected increase in cortisol post cosyntropin administration reflects adrenocortical destruction
7. Tx
   1. Mineralocorticoid — Fludrocortisone necessary to restore Aldosterone
   2. Glucocorticoid — Hydrocortisone (preferred due to shorter duration of action that can mimic natural circadian rhythm of cortisol secretion), Prednisone, Dexamethasone

# Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency (Pituitary, dec ADH)

1. Hormonal imbalance
   1. Loss of glucocorticoid and adrenal androgen secretion — dec ACTH, dec Cortisol (Normal Aldosterone)
   2. Mineralocorticoid preserved/regulated by RAAS system — lack hyperkalemia, significant hypotension and hyperchloremic acidosis
   3. ACTH levels decreased (pituitary gland destroyed) = MSH not stimulated = hyperpigmentation not seen
2. (Postpartum) Sheehan Syndrome
   1. PP:
      1. Obstetric hemorrhage complicated by hypotension
      2. Postpartum pituitary infarction — pituitary gland enlarges during pregnancy and is vulnerable to ischemia from hemorrhagic shock
   2. CF:
      1. Lactation failure (Dec Prolactin)
      2. Amenorrhea, hot flashes, vaginal atrophy (Dec FSH, LH) Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism
      3. Fatigue, bradycardia (Dec TSH) Central Hypothyroidism
      4. Anorexia, weight loss, hypotension (Dec ACTH) Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency
      5. Decreased lean body mass (Dec GH)
      6. SIADH — dec Na
         1. Note: Cortisol normally inhibits ADH
   3. Labs: dec ACTH, dec Na, Normal K
   4. Tx: Replace deficient hormones; no effective treatment for restoring lactation
      1. Glucocorticoid
      2. *Don't require Mineralocorticoid due to aldosterone preservation
3. (Postpartum) Lymphocytic hypophysitis
   1. Lymphocytic infiltration of the pituitary gland
4. Termination of long term exogenous CST (long term therapy)
   1. Patient may exhibit signs of excess cortisol — facial plethora, supraclavicular fat pad
5. Tx: Glucocorticoids only

# Hyperaldosteronism

## Primary Hyperaldosteronism — Conn Syndrome

1. PP
   1. Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia
   2. Aldosterone-producing adrenal adenoma
2. CF
   1. Refractory HTN
   2. Lack peripheral edema due to spontaneous diuresis (aldosterone escape)
3. Labs
   1. Lack spontaneous hypokalemia but prone to diuretic-induced Hypokalemia
   2. Metabolic alkalosis
   3. Mild Hypernatremia (143-147 mEq/L)
4. Evaluation
   1. 1st: Early morning plasma aldosterone concentration (PAC) to plasma renin activity (PRA) ratio
      1. Withdraw drugs that alter ratio 4 weeks prior (spironolactone, eplerenone, amiloride, triamterene)
      2. PAC/PRA ratio >20 with plasma aldosterone >15 ng/dL suggests primary hyperaldosteronism
   2. 2nd: Adrenal suppression testing
      1. Salt loading and documenting inability to suppress serum aldosterone
   3. 3rd: Adrenal imaging
      1. CT scan
      2. If CT doesn't reveal discrete unilateral mass --> Adrenal vein sampling to differentiate bilateral adrenal hyperplasia from adrenal adenoma
5. Management
   1. Hyperplasia --> Medical therapy
      1. Aldosterone antagonists — Spironolactone, Eplerenone
      2. Potassium-sparing diuretics if aldosterone-antag not tolerated — Amiloride, Triamterene
   2. Adenoma --> Surgery
      1. Aldosterone antagonists (if surgery refused) — Spironolactone, Eplerenone

# Hypercortisolism — Cushing Syndrome

1. Etiology
   1. ACTH dependent
      1. ACTH secreting pituitary tumor (Cushing disease)
         1. Weight gain
      2. Ectopic ACTH production (Small cell lung cancer)
         1. Weight loss and thinning of extremities
   2. ACTH independent
      1. Exogenous Glucocorticoid intake
      2. Adrenal adenoma (rare, inc DHEA sulfate)
2. CF
   1. Weight gain — supraclavicular and dorsal neck fat pads
   2. Proximal muscle weakness — Catabolic effects of cortisol on skeletal muscles --> muscle atrophy without pain
   3. HTN: excessively elevated cortisol levels mimic mineralocorticoid activity
   4. Hypokalemia and alkalosis
      1. Partial mineralocorticoid effects of cortisol — seen in severe hypercortisolism
   5. Hyperglycemia
      1. Peripheral insulin resistance and hypercortisolism-induced gluconeogenesis
   6. Skin — catabolic effects of cortisol on connective tissue
      1. Easy bruisability — normal platelet function and coagulation proteins
      2. Dermal atrophy
      3. Wide purple striae
      4. Hyperpigmentation (ACTH-dependent)
      5. Cutaneous fungal infection (Tinea versicolor, onychomycosis)
   7. Females — hyperandrogenism (co-secretion of adrenal androgens with cortisol)
      1. Menstrual irregularities
      2. Acne
      3. Hirsutism
   8. Note: Hypercortisolism presents with excess aldosterone and androgen effects
   9. Note:
      1. Hirsutism (male-pattern hair distribution — upper lip, chest) seen in setting of mild androgen elevation --> PCOS
      2. Virilization (male pattern baldness, clitoromegaly, deep voice, increased muscle mass) seen with significantly elevated androgen levels --> ovarian mass, adrenal mass, exogenous steroids
3. Dx
   1. 1st: Lab confirmation of Hypercortisolism (1 of the following)
      1. Late-night salivary cortisol assay
      2. 24-hr urine free cortisol measurement
      3. Overnight low-dose dexamethasone suppression test
   2. 2nd: ACTH level evaluation
      1. inc ACTH --> ACTH dependent (high dose dexamethasone suppresses cortisol)
         1. ACTH secreting pituitary tumor (Cushing Disease), Ectopic ACTH (Small cell lung cancer)
   3. Note:
      1. DHEA (dihydroepiandrosterone) produced by both Adrenals and Ovaries
      2. DHEA Sulfate only produced by Adrenals --> high levels raise concern for adrenal hyperplasia or carcinoma

# Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia

1. Differentiating by enzyme names
   1. Is 2nd number a "1" = virilization
      1. Is 1st number a "1" = HTN
   2. 21-hydroxylase deficiency = virilization in females, peripheral precocious puberty in males
      1. 11b-hydroxylase deficiency = virilization in females, peripheral precocious puberty in males + HTN
         1. 11-deoxycorticosterone has partial mineralocorticoid receptor activity
      2. 17a-hydroxylase deficiency = HTN
2. 21-hydroxylase Deficiency
   1. Inhibited conversion of Progesterone to 11-Deoxycorticosterone: Aldosterone precursor
      1. Dec Mineralocorticoids: Dehydration, Salt wasting (Hypotension, Hyponatremia, Hyperkalemia)
   2. Inhibited conversion of 17-Hydroxyprogesterone to 11-Deoxycortisol: Cortisol precursor
      1. Dec Glucocorticoids: Fasting Hypoglycemia (Normally Cortisol stimulates gluconeogenesis in the liver)
   3. Increased conversion of 17-Hydroxyprogesterone to Androstenedione: Testosterone precursor
      1. Inc Androgens: Female — virilization, Male — normal or enlarged external genitalia
   4. CF
      1. First few days of life: Normal electrolytes (due to maternal adrenal hormones), virilized "underdeveloped phallus"
      2. 1-2wks: Salt abnormalities
   5. Dx: Elevated 17-Hydroxyprogesterone

# Pheochromocytoma

1. Extra-adrenal tumors are termed "paragangliomas"
2. S/S
   1. Triad: Episodic headache, Sweating, Tachycardia
   2. Resistant HTN or HTN with unexplained Elevated Glucose
   3. FH: MEN2, NF1, VHL
   4. 10% Bilateral, 10% Extraadrenal, 10% Malignant
3. Dx
   1. Urine or Plasma Metanephrine levels
   2. Confirmatory abdominal imaging for Elevated Metanephrine
4. Tx — All Pheochromocytomas should undergo surgical resection due to catecholamine surges risk
   1. Preop Alpha blockade (1-2wks before surgery) prior to Beta blockade
      1. Phenoxybenzamine (irreversible, nonselective alpha blocker)
      2. Terazosin (selective, alpha-1 blocker)
      3. Increased Na intake and hydration to avoid alpha-blockade resulting orthostasis and hypotension
   2. Laparoscopic or open surgical resection

Note — Medullary Thyroid Cancer: Type 2
- Consider Family History — 1/3 inherited in MEN Type 2
- Calcitonin producing tumor from neuroendocrine parafollicular C cells
- Pheochromocytoma associated with RET proto-oncogene mutations
- Might be asymptomatic and present with life-threatening hypertensive crisis during thyroidectomy surgery
- Most patients with Medullary Thyroid Cancer undergo RET mutation testing and be screened for Pheochromocytoma with plasma fractionated metanephrine assay

# Carcinoid Syndrome

1. PP
   1. Well-defined neuroendocrine tumors
   2. Slow growing tumors commonly found in distal small intestine, proximal colon, and lung
   3. Secrete Histamine, Serotonin and Vasoactive intestinal peptide that is metabolized by the liver
   4. GI carcinoid tumors metastasize to the liver — liver unable to metabolize hormones due to hormone release into systemic circulation --> Carcinoid Syndrome
   5. Pathognomonic plaque-like deposits of fibrous tissue occur on right heart endocardium --> Tricuspid regurgitation and Right-sided heart failure
2. Tryptophan
   1. Precursor for Serotonin and Niacin (nicotinic acid, B3)
   2. Excessive conversion to Serotonin and its metabolite 5-hydroxyindoleacetic acid leads to B3 deficiency
   3. B3 deficiency: Pellagra — dermatitis, dementia, diarrhea, glossitis, angular stomatitis
