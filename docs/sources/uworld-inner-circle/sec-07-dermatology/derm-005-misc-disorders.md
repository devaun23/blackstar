# MISCELLANEOUS SKIN DISORDERS

## ICHTHYOSIS VULGARIS

- "ichthys" = "fish"
- Inherited: filaggrin gene mutation
- Epidermal hyperplasia → Defective keratinocyte desquamation
- No erythema or papules/vesicles
- Sand-paper like texture
- Associated: Palmer hyperlinearity, Atopic (allergic rhinitis, atopic dermatitis)

## PITYRIASIS ROSEA

- Herald patch followed by "Christmas tree" distribution

## ACANTHOSIS NIGRICANS

1. Benign AN: younger, insulin-resistant states (DM, Obesity, PCOS). Increased Insulin or ILGF stimulate epidermal and dermal proliferation. Skin tags common.
2. Malignant AN: middle-aged/elderly with sudden appearance. GI and GU neoplasm. Lesions in uncommon areas (mucous membranes, palms, soles)

## ACTINIC KERATOSIS

- Solar damage: telangiectasias, deep wrinkles
- Tx: Isolated—cryotherapy; Diffuse—Topical fluorouracil, Imiquimod, Tirbanibulin

## LICHEN PLANUS

- Wickham striae
- Autoimmune, CD8+
- RF: Hx of illicit drug use, ↑aminotransferase
- RF Meds: ACE-I, Thiazide diuretics, BB, Hydroxychloroquine
- Med-related is diffuse
- Tx: Topical high-potency Glucocorticoids—Fluocinonide, Betamethasone
- Oral lesions: Koebner phenomenon (ulceration at trauma site)
  - Painful, lasts for months (vs. days for Canker sores)
- LP nails: Longitudinal ridges, fissuring, thinning

## ORAL LESIONS

### Aphthous Stomatitis (Canker Sores)
- Painful ulcers, resolve within few days (similar presentation but symptoms last months in oral lichen planus)

### Oral Herpes Simplex
- Primary infection—gingivostomatitis (children) or pharyngitis (adults) with fever and LAD
- Recurrence presents with ulcerating vesicles on lips and perioral skin

### Oral Candidiasis (Thrush)
- Loosely adherent, fluffy, white exudate on oral mucosa
- Immunosuppressed patients

## NAMED SKIN LESIONS

### Erythema Marginatum
- Acute Rheumatic Fever
- Numerous, widespread plaques with sharp annular or serpiginous border and central clearing
- Nonpruritic. Transient.

### Erythema Migrans
- Lyme Disease. Target-shaped.
- Systemic symptoms and history of exposure to ticks in endemic area.

### Ecthyma Gangrenosum
- Pseudomonas aeruginosa
- Hemorrhagic pustules with surrounding erythema that evolve into necrotic ulcers
- Setting of profound neutropenia and bacteremia
- Systemic infection signs (Fever)

### Pyoderma Gangrenosum
- Painful, rapidly expanding ulcer with purple/dusky margins
- Trunk or LE
- Systemic disease—Rheumatoid Arthritis, IBD, AML
- Skin biopsy: Neutrophil-predominant mixed cellular infiltrate with dermal and epidermal necrosis
- Tx: Local or systemic glucocorticoids

### Leukocytoclastic Vasculitis
- Cutaneous small-vessel vasculitis
- Triggered by Antibiotics or infection (HCV, HIV)
- Painful, raised, non blanching, petechial or purpuric lesions (palpable purpura)

### Discoid Lupus Erythematosus
- MC form of chronic cutaneous lupus erythematosus
- Sun-exposed regions. May occur independent of SLE.
- Chronic, scaly, irregular, erythematous plaques with ulceration and central hypopigmentation surrounded by hyperpigmentation
- Expand slowly and can lead to dermal atrophy and scarring
- Rash can extend into hair follicles causing scarring alopecia

### Mycosis Fungoides
- Progressive neoplastic proliferation of epidermotropic T cells
- Chronic and more common in men
- Early lesions: nonspecific, psoriatic-appearing plaques or patches, often pruritic with predilection for trunk and buttocks
- Later lesions: skin tumors with palpable lymph nodes
- Dermatopathic lymphadenopathy without tumor involvement of the node possible
- Internal organs can be involved: lymph nodes, liver, spleen
- Sezary syndrome: leukemic phase of cutaneous T-cell lymphoma—circulating Sezary cells, erythroderma, lymphadenopathy
- Dx: clinical features and histology, immunophenotypic characterization showing clonal T cells, electron microscopy showing Sezary or Lutzner cells (cerebriform lymphocytes)
- Early lesions clinically indistinguishable from dermatitis—histologic diagnosis indicated for any dermatitis chronic and resistant to treatment
- Tx: Phototherapy and skin-directed topical treatments (mainstay)
  - Early localized: total skin electron beam irradiation
  - Extensive/advanced: radiation therapy
  - Combined modalities: steroids, chemotherapy, retinoids, monoclonal antibodies, IFN-α

### Systemic Sclerosis (Scleroderma)
- Hands/fingers and face induration and skin hardening
- Ulcerations at fingertips
