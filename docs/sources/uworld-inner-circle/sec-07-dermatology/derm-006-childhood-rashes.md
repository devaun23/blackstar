# CHILDHOOD RASHES

## Vesicular Eruptions Differential (for NBME)
- Viral infections: HSV, VZV
- Dermatitis herpetiformis (Celiac disease)
- Eczematous rashes: Contact Dermatitis (memory T cells), Atopic Dermatitis

## 1. HAND-FOOT-AND-MOUTH DISEASE

1. Coxsackievirus
2. Any age—"Adult exhibited symptoms after playing with 2-yr niece in water park"
3. Transmission (communal setting): direct contact with respiratory, oral, vesicular secretions or fecal-oral spread
4. CF
   1. Palms, Soles, Buttocks
   2. Painful oral vesicles and ulcers
   3. Maculopapular or vesicular exanthem
5. Complications
   1. Myopericarditis
   2. Aseptic Meningitis
   3. Nail dystrophy
6. Diagnosis: Clinical
7. Management: Supportive (pain control, hydration), self-limiting

## 2. HHV6: ROSEOLA

- Fever first → Rash

## 3. MOLLUSCUM CONTAGIOSUM

1. Poxvirus
2. Benign condition
3. CF: Firm, round, pink/flesh colored papule with central umbilication
4. Tx:
   - Kids: reassurance and observation in healthy children, resolve in 6-12 months
   - Adults: therapy often painful and leads to scarring

| | Children | Adults |
|---|---|---|
| Location | Trunk, Intertriginous areas, Face | Lower abdomen, Genitals, Upper Thighs |
| Evaluation | Clinical Dx: Classic presentation | Clinical Dx: Classic presentation. Genital: STI testing. Extensive lesions: HIV testing |
| Treatment | Reassurance | Cryotherapy, Curettage, Topical therapy |

## 4. PARVOVIRUS B19: ERYTHEMA INFECTIOSUM

1. School-age children
2. CF
   1. Prodrome: Fever, rhinorrhea followed by "slapped cheek" rash and lacy or reticular rash on the body

## 5. RUBELLA: GERMAN MEASLES

- Mild symptoms, Cephalocaudal rash does not darken, prominent LAD
1. Maternal-fetal transmission: 1st TM most teratogenic
   - Classic Triad Congenital Rubella in unvaccinated child:
     1. Sensorineural hearing loss
     2. Cardiac defects (PDA) "L 2nd intercostal space harsh murmur heard during systole and diastole"
     3. Cataracts "absent red reflex"
2. Children: Fever, Cephalocaudal spread of maculopapular rash, Suboccipital/posterior auricular/posterior cervical LAD, Forchheimer Spots (Petechiae or Erythematous papules on Soft Palate)
   - *Postnatal infection much milder than prenatal (could lead to death)
3. Adolescents/Adults: Same as Children + Arthralgias/Arthritis
4. Dx: Serology
5. Prevention: Live attenuated vaccine to all nonimmune women before conception

## 6. RUBEOLA: MEASLES

- High Fever, Cephalocaudal rash that darkens, ILL APPEARING
1. Transmission: Airborne
2. Contagious: Airborne isolation and N95 respirator mask
3. CF
   1. Prodrome: Cough, Coryza, Conjunctivitis, Fever, Koplik spots
   2. 2-4 days later—Maculopapular exanthem: Cephalocaudal and centrifugal spread, Spares palms/soles "Morbilliform"
4. Dx: Serology
5. Prevention: Live-attenuated Measles vaccine
6. Tx: Supportive, Vitamin A for hospitalized—promotes antibody-producing cells and regeneration of epithelial cells (gut, lungs, retina)
7. Possible mild version of measles within 1-3 weeks of immunization
   - Self-limited, provide reassurance
   - Avoid contact with immunocompromised individuals until rash resolves
8. Complications: Subacute Sclerosing Panencephalitis, Measles Encephalitis, Giant Cell Pneumonia

## 8. VZV (VARICELLA-ZOSTER VIRUS)

### Neonatal
1. Fever, Vesicular eruption, Systemic involvement (Pneumonia, Hepatitis, Meningoencephalitis)
2. Tx: Acyclovir
3. Prevention:
   - Isolate infant from Varicella contact
   - Postexposure Prophylaxis extremely important for patient not immune (infants, lack 2 doses of VZV vaccine)
     - Immunocompetent: VZV vaccine
     - Non-immunocompetent: Passive immunization with VZIG to infant if maternal infection developed 5 days before or 2 days after delivery

### Immunocompromised
- Grouped, clear to yellow vesicles on an erythematous base in a dermatome distribution

### Varicella-like Rashes Post-vaccine
1. Vaccine strain: <10 lesions, maculopapular/vesicular, mildly contagious
   - Attenuated live vaccine replicates after immunization in 3% kids
   - Avoid contact with susceptible individuals: pregnant women, immunosuppressed
2. Wild-type: >100 lesions, vesicular in successive crops, highly contagious
   - If acquired before child's immunization results in protective antibodies

### Varicella Post-exposure Prophylaxis
1. Immunity (prior infection or 2 doses vaccine)?
   - Yes: Observe
   - No:
     - Immunocompetent: Varicella Vaccine
     - Immunocompromised: VZIG reserved for at-risk patients who cannot receive live vaccine

### Herpes Zoster
1. VZV reactivation from sensory nerve ganglion
   - VZV remains dormant in DRG following primary infection of chickenpox
   - Decreased cell-mediated immunity (older age, immunosuppressed meds, HIV) increase reactivation risk
2. CF:
   1. Prodromal phase: itching, tingling, burning in dermatomal distribution
   2. Rash: grouped vesicles and papules on erythematous base; ulceration & crusting, acute neuritic pain (itching, burning, allodynia)
   3. Postherpetic neuralgia: persistent neuritic pain >4 months after rash onset
3. Tx:
   1. Antiviral: Acyclovir, Famciclovir, Valacyclovir
   2. *Oral glucocorticoids (prednisone) suggested as adjuvant to antiviral but don't reduce risk of postherpetic neuralgia
   3. Postherpetic neuralgia: TCA (Amitriptyline, Amoxapine, Doxepin, Imipramine), Pregabalin, Gabapentin
