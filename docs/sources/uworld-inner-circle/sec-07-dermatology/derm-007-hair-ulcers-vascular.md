# HAIR LOSS

## ALOPECIA AREATA

- Autoimmune
- Patchy, painless hair loss
- Hair shafts show narrowing close to the surface and may be broken off

## ANDROGENIC ALOPECIA

- Uneven hair loss
- Men: thinning in frontotemporal hairline and vertex
- Women: thinning at vertex and sides
- Minoxidil: direct vasodilator that increases blood flow to the scalp

## TELOGEN EFFLUVIUM

- One of the MCC of hair loss in adults
- Acute, diffuse, noninflammatory hair loss
- Hair follicles pass through 3 phases:
  1. Growth phase (Anagen—90% follicles)
  2. Transformative phase (Catagen—<1%)
  3. Rest/shedding (Telogen—10%)

## TINEA CAPITIS

- Superficial dermatophyte
- Pruritic, scaly, erythematous patches with hair loss
- Proximal hair shafts may be visualized as small dots in the area of hair loss (broken shafts)

## TRICHORRHEXIS NODOSA

- Fragility of hair with breaking of strands and splitting of fibers
- Congenital or acquired (excessive heat, hair dyes, salt water)

## TRICHOTILLOMANIA

- Behavioral disorder of compulsive hair pulling
- Irregular patches of hair loss and broken fibers
- Affects front and sides of the scalp, eyebrows and eyelashes

# LE ULCERS

## VENOUS ULCERS
- Most common cause of lower extremity ulcers
- Medial malleolus area
- Associated with venous insufficiency

## ARTERIAL ULCERS
- Lateral malleolus, distal toes
- Painful, punched-out appearance
- Associated with peripheral arterial disease

## PRESSURE ULCERS
- Staging system (I-IV) based on tissue depth involvement

# VASCULAR TUMORS OF SKIN

## PYOGENIC GRANULOMA

- Vascular tumor
- Rapidly growing, friable, red papule that bleeds easily

## ANGIOSARCOMA

- Malignant vascular tumor

## KAPOSI SARCOMA

- Vascular proliferative disease attributed to herpesvirus (HHV-8), also called Kaposi sarcoma–associated herpesvirus (KSHV)
- Presents with multiple red to violaceous macules, papules, or nodules that can progress to plaques on the lower limbs, back, face, mouth, and genitalia
- Plaques can also be found in the GI tract and lung
- HIV-associated (epidemic) KS is an aggressive form; although less common since HAART, it remains the most common HIV-associated malignancy
- Diagnosed by history, clinical impression, and histology
