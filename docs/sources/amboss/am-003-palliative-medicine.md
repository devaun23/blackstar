# Overview of Palliative Medicine

Palliative medicine is a comprehensive, interdisciplinary approach to medical care that aims to relieve suffering and provide optimal quality of life in patients with serious or life-threatening illnesses. It is often provided in conjunction with life-prolonging therapy.

## Basics

### Key Elements of Palliative Care
- Symptom relief, particularly sufficient analgesia
- Assistance in the organization of adequate, needs-based care
- Support regarding social services
- Psychological support of patients and their families

### Members of a Palliative Care Team
- Physicians (including palliative care specialists)
- Nurses
- Social workers
- Psychologists
- Chaplains
- Pharmacists

Palliative medicine is not synonymous with end-of-life care. It is often used to improve a patient's quality of life even as life-prolonging therapy continues.

## Symptom Management

### General Approach
- Consider enrolling patients with cancer or other high-morbidity illness in a palliative care program
- Frequently reassess the patient's symptoms
- If new symptoms arise or current symptoms change, consider investigating the etiology
- Discuss the risk-benefit profile and confirm goals of care with patients and caregivers
- Adjust approach to eliminate diagnostic and therapeutic procedures that are too invasive, painful, or have side effects not aligned with the patient's preferences
- Involve associated healthcare professionals (e.g., dietitians, physical therapists, occupational therapists)

### Overview of Symptom Management

#### Pain
- Optimize nonpharmacological therapy for all patients
- Consider early escalation to opioids for sufficient pain relief and titrate to effect
- Combine fixed-schedule slow-release opioids with short-acting opioids as needed
- Consider specific therapy for neuropathic pain and bone pain

#### Gastrointestinal
- Nausea and vomiting: antiemetics (e.g., metoclopramide), fluid therapy
- Constipation: optimize fluid and fiber intake; consider stimulant laxatives with or without osmotic laxatives
- Loss of appetite: dexamethasone or prednisolone
- Thirst: oral care
- Diarrhea: fluid and electrolyte replacement

#### Pulmonary
- Dyspnea: consider treatment of reversible causes (e.g., pleural effusion); low-dose opioids (e.g., morphine); breathing and relaxation techniques
- Cough: consider expectorant or antitussive drugs depending on type

#### CNS
- Anxiety and depression: benzodiazepines, antidepressants, psychotherapy
- Delirium: antipsychotics, benzodiazepines; optimize nonpharmacological and preventative measures

#### Terminal Phase
- Discontinue unnecessary investigations, monitoring, and treatments not required for comfort care
- Optimize mouth care and patient positioning
- Optimize route of medication delivery for symptomatic treatment
- Manage noisy terminal respiratory secretions: repositioning, consider anticholinergic drugs (glycopyrrolate, atropine)
- Consider palliative sedation in select patients for refractory distress or agitation

## Pain

Pain is an unpleasant experience with sensory and emotional components that may occur with or without actual or potential tissue damage.

### Pain Types
- **Nociceptive pain**: caused by damage or potential damage to tissue (excluding neural tissue)
- **Neuropathic pain**: caused by damage to a somatosensory nerve
- **Nociplastic pain**: caused by altered nociception despite no underlying tissue damage
- **Mixed pain**: simultaneous presence of nociceptive, neuropathic, and/or nociplastic pain
- **Emotional pain**: mental suffering and the impact of emotions on pain experience
- **Total pain**: the combination of physical, social, spiritual, and psychological/emotional pain

### WHO Analgesic Ladder
Use the WHO analgesic ladder to structure treatment:
- Step 1: Non-opioid analgesics (acetaminophen, NSAIDs) +/- adjuvants
- Step 2: Weak opioids + non-opioid analgesics +/- adjuvants
- Step 3: Strong opioids + non-opioid analgesics +/- adjuvants

### Nonpharmacological Therapy
- Spiritual and social services: chaplains, spiritual advisors, social workers
- Physical interventions: physical therapy, acupuncture, massage, transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation
- Mental health interventions: depression and anxiety treatment, relaxation therapy, cognitive behavioral therapy

### Nonopioid Pharmacotherapy
- First-line agents: acetaminophen and NSAIDs
- Neuropathic pain: consider adding gabapentinoids and antidepressants
- Metastatic bone pain: consider bisphosphonates, denosumab, consult radiation oncology for external beam radiotherapy

### Opioid Therapy
- Most patients require a combination of scheduled slow-release opioids and fast-acting doses for breakthrough pain
- Treat severe pain first with parenteral therapy, then transition to oral
- Consider preemptive doses before painful procedures (e.g., dressing changes, bed baths)
- Consider continuous opioid infusions in terminally ill patients
- Consider extended-release formulations for patients not controlled with as-needed dosing or requiring >= 4 as-needed doses per day

## Breathlessness (Dyspnea)

- Common in palliative care patients
- Often not easily attributable to an organic disease process
- Responds poorly to standard medical therapies (e.g., bronchodilators, diuretics)
- Approach: identify and treat reversible causes, optimize nonpharmacological therapy, offer palliative pharmacotherapy
- Palliative pharmacotherapy: low-dose opioids (first line), benzodiazepines (second line)

## Nausea and Vomiting

- If related to cancer treatment: consult oncology, consider CINV treatment
- Begin immediate symptomatic treatment while working up underlying causes
- Pharmacological therapy:
  - Start with dopamine receptor antagonist (e.g., metoclopramide), titrate to max tolerance
  - Add serotonin (5HT3) antagonist (e.g., ondansetron) for postoperative N/V
  - Add antihistamine (e.g., meclizine) or anticholinergic (e.g., scopolamine patch) for vestibular N/V
  - Add corticosteroid (e.g., dexamethasone) for meningeal irritation, increased ICP, or malignant bowel obstruction
  - Consider benzodiazepines for anxiety-triggered N/V

## Constipation

- Occurs in up to 90% of palliative care patients
- Consequences: pain, nausea and vomiting, bowel obstruction, urinary retention, delirium
- Management:
  - Reduce or replace offending medication (e.g., anticholinergics, opioids)
  - Manage bowel obstruction
  - Address electrolyte abnormalities (e.g., hypercalcemia, hypokalemia)
  - Optimize diet and fluid intake
  - Provide symptomatic treatment with laxatives

## Delirium

- Occurs in up to 90% of patients receiving palliative care
- Etiologies: medications (opioids, steroids, antiemetics, benzodiazepines), pain, distended bowel or bladder, metabolic causes, disruption of sleep-wake cycle, CNS pathology
- Management:
  - Address underlying causes, especially pain and constipation
  - Initiate nonpharmacological supportive measures
  - Perform medication reconciliation and minimize polypharmacy
  - Discontinue offending agents: glucocorticoids, anticholinergic medications, benzodiazepines
  - Mild symptoms: oral antipsychotics
  - Severe symptoms: parenteral antipsychotics
  - Refractory symptoms: add benzodiazepine to antipsychotics

## Anxiety

- Very common in palliative care; inadequate treatment can worsen nausea, pain, and depression
- Consider early treatment with antidepressants or anxiolytics
- Palliative anxiolysis: benzodiazepines are first-line
  - Short-acting: lorazepam, alprazolam
  - Long-acting: clonazepam, diazepam

## Management of Imminently Dying Patients

### General Principles
- Patient comfort and psychosocial support for caregivers are the primary concerns
- Educate the family members and patient about the dying process
- Offer consultation for spiritual support
- Discuss end-of-life care with patient and family
- CPR is only appropriate if the cause of death is reversible. CPR will be ineffective for a patient who is in the imminent phase of death.

### Recognizing Imminent Death
- Early symptoms: increased time sleeping, loss of interest in food and drink, delirium, difficulty mobilizing (bedbound)
- Late symptoms: mottled extremities, noisy terminal respiratory secretions, irregular breathing with periods of apnea, coma
- **Terminal agitation**: cognitive decline, restlessness, altered affect, and/or hallucinations. May be part of unrecognized acute delirium. Often distressing to both patient and family.

### Nonpharmacological Management
- Discontinue diagnostic testing and unnecessary monitoring
- Provide positioning support; use pressure-relieving mattress
- Provide adequate mouth care: regular moistening of oral mucosa, artificial saliva, assisted oral care
- Consider deactivation of implanted cardiac devices
- Stop routine medications (statins) and treatments (chemotherapy, dialysis) not contributing to symptom relief

### Pharmacotherapy
- Continue existing symptom control medications
- Change medications to nonoral routes in patients unable to swallow
- Patients may require continuous infusions as symptoms worsen

### Management of Terminal Respiratory Secretions
- Consider anticholinergic medications: glycopyrrolate, atropine, hyoscyamine, scopolamine

### Palliative Sedation
- Treats patient discomfort by reducing level of consciousness
- Controversial: requires sedating drugs in doses that can cause respiratory depression, potentially hastening death
- Most major palliative care societies support it as an exceptional option after all other attempts at controlling refractory symptoms have failed
- Decision should only be made after consultation with patient or advocates and treating team under guidance of palliative care physician and/or medical ethicist
- Indications: refractory symptoms, terminal agitation, perceived patient discomfort
- Consider only after identifying and treating reversible causes of agitation
- Discuss plan in detail with patient and family; clarify it will induce unconsciousness and has potential to hasten death; obtain explicit permission
- Medications: first line midazolam; alternatives lorazepam, propofol

Adequate management of end-of-life symptoms may involve large enough doses of medication to cause respiratory depression; while this may hasten death, it is considered acceptable according to the principle of double effect.

## Hospice

### Definition
Type of palliative care specifically given to patients at the end of life.

### Principles
- Preserve the dignity of patients during final stages of life
- Provide maximum comfort and ensure pain relief
- Prioritize positive effects over potential negative effects, according to the principle of double effect

### Eligibility
- Estimated life expectancy < 6 months
- Patient (and family) has decided to stop curative or life-preserving treatment to maximize quality of life
- Not all treatment should be withdrawn. Antibiotics can still be given if the patient develops an infection.

### Facilities
- Patients can receive hospice care at home, in a skilled nursing facility, or at a hospital
- Home hospice: regular nursing visits, assistance with ADLs, support for home medical equipment
- Hospital/nursing facility hospice: indicated if pain or symptoms require more specialized care
- Services available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week

## Deprescribing Medications

### General Considerations
- To improve quality of life in end-of-life patients, medications should be evaluated and nonessential or inappropriate drugs discontinued
- High-risk medications (sedatives, opioids, anticholinergics) may be continued because short-term benefits outweigh long-term risks

### Considerations for Deprescribing
- Drug interactions
- Potential adverse events
- Treatment burden

### Drugs Considered for Deprescribing
Medications indicated for primary or secondary prevention and/or with no short-term benefits:
- Antihypertensives
- Statins
- Aspirin
- Chronic proton pump inhibitors
- Short-acting insulins and oral hypoglycemics
- Antiresorptive medications for osteoporosis

## Pediatric Palliative Care

### Communication
- Full information for parents/legal guardians regarding diagnosis, treatment options, goals, and prognosis
- Disclosure to child adapted by age and developmental level:
  - Preschoolers (< 6 years): often cannot conceptualize death; provide clear explanation with parental presence
  - Elementary schoolers: often begin understanding death; allow participation in medical decisions when appropriate
  - Adolescents: often have full understanding of death; allow participation in decision-making, respect privacy and autonomy
- Consider values and preferences of the family

### Decision Making and Ethics
- Parents/legal guardians are the legal medical decision-makers
- Parental consent must be obtained
- Assent from the minor with appropriate developmental level should also be obtained
- Consider previously agreed upon DNR orders or advance directives
- Encourage exchange with parents in similar situations and facilitate contact with support groups
- Ethics committees can aid parental decision making

### Psychosocial Support
- Facilitate psychosocial and/or spiritual support for child and family
- Address implications of the condition (disability and death)
- Consider personal wishes of the child (e.g., make-a-wish)
- Facilitate contact to grief and bereavement support for family

### Symptom Management
- Pharmacological management of symptoms
- Nonpharmacological measures: massages, physical therapy, acupuncture, play therapy, music therapy, art therapy, guided meditations
