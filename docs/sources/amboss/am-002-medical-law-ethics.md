# Principles of Medical Law and Ethics

Best medical practice is founded upon the ethical principles that guide health care providers who care for patients or perform research. The core ethical principles of medicine are autonomy, beneficence, nonmaleficence, and justice.

## Medical Ethics

### Core Ethical Principles

Medical ethics is founded on a set of core principles that are based on respect to patients as individuals. Ethical dilemmas arise when respecting one of these principles becomes impossible without compromising another. Ethical responsibilities usually align with legal precedence, but the two systems remain distinct.

#### Autonomy
- Provide sufficient information for the patient to be able to make their own decisions regarding their care (i.e., informed consent)
- Honor the patient's choices to accept or decline care

#### Beneficence
- Advocate for the patient and act in their best interest (fiduciary relationship)
- May conflict with autonomy

#### Nonmaleficence
- Avoid causing injury or suffering to the patient
- May conflict with beneficence: the balance of risks and benefits must be favorable to the patient
- Frequently discussed in reference to drugs and surgical procedures

#### Justice
- Treat patients fairly and equitably
- Equity is not the same as equality

### Obligation to Treat
- A clinician is legally obligated to treat a patient when failing to provide treatment would immediately endanger the patient's life
- This law was established in the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA)
- Any hospital with an emergency department is required to screen for emergency medical conditions if requested and, if such a condition exists, provide treatment until that condition is stabilized
- Clinicians are not obliged to treat a patient longitudinally and may end a clinician-patient relationship if they wish
  - The patient or their surrogate must be notified and have the ability (e.g., time, money) to establish care with another clinician
  - The clinician is also obligated to facilitate the transfer of care

### Principle of Proportionality
- **Definition**: A bioethical principle stating that an intervention's potential benefits should be proportionately greater than its potential harm or burden
- Benefit, harm, and burden should be considered holistically: costs for the patient, costs for the health care system, and safety of providers and other patients
- Aim: to minimize patient harm proportionately to maximizing treatment efficiency
- Examples:
  - Using physical restraints if a patient poses a threat to themselves or others
  - Reassessment of resource allocation during a health crisis when providing maximum benefit to select patients would overburden the system as a whole

## Research Ethics

### Role of Institutional Review Boards
- **Definition**: An institutional review board (IRB) is a committee that reviews designs of studies involving humans to ensure participant safety, participant confidentiality, and compliance with medical ethics
- **IRB composition**: at least five members with at least one scientist and one non-scientist. IRBs reviewing studies on vulnerable populations should include representatives of those populations.
- **IRB approval**: For most forms of primary research involving human subjects, an IRB should review and approve the study proposal and informed consent protocols before the research activity is started
- Any amendment to an approved research protocol must be reevaluated and reapproved by the IRB
- Studies that do NOT require IRB review: surveys or interviews about products or policies, educational tests, secondary research involving publicly available nonidentifiable information

### Informed Consent for Research

Researchers performing a study involving an intervention on or interaction with human subjects should obtain informed consent unless an informed consent waiver has been obtained from the IRB.

The process of obtaining informed consent requires:
1. Provide sufficient information verbally and in writing:
   - All aspects of the experimental protocol (trial design, purpose, duration)
   - Any foreseeable hazards to the participant
   - The likelihood of direct benefit to the participant
   - All alternative treatment options, if applicable
   - Details about funding and financial incentives for researchers
   - A statement about confidentiality and record maintenance
   - For research involving more than minimal risk: information on compensation or medical treatments available in case of injury
   - Information on whom to contact regarding questions
   - A statement that participation is voluntary and the participant can withdraw at any time without penalty
2. Facilitate understanding and clarify misunderstandings
3. Provide adequate time and opportunity to decide
4. Minimize the possibility of coercion or undue influence

Key concepts:
- **Therapeutic misconception**: an individual participating in research believes they are receiving individualized medical treatment rather than participating in research
- **Therapeutic misestimation**: an individual misjudges the risk or benefit of participating in research
- **Coercion**: an overt or implicit threat of harm made to obtain compliance
- **Undue influence**: the offer of an inappropriate reward to obtain compliance

The process of obtaining informed consent extends for the entire duration of the trial. Participants have the right to withdraw consent at any time.

### Research Funding
- Researchers should contractually ensure the sponsor cannot influence, delay, or obstruct publication of findings
- Clinicians should not accept any form of compensation for directing patients to participate in research studies
- The source of funding should be disclosed as part of informed consent and when publishing findings
- Researchers should refuse financial compensation that surpasses the value of their input

### Compensation for Enrolled Participants
- Type and amount should be determined prior to study enrollment and should be just and fair
- **Prorated compensation**: participants receive compensation for each trial activity completed
- **Completion-contingent compensation**: participants receive compensation for completing all trial activities
- Compensation should be adequate but not excessive or tied to conditions that affect voluntary consent
- Offering excessive or completion-contingent compensation may constitute coercion or undue influence
- Participants can withdraw at any time without loss of benefits they are otherwise entitled to
- If adverse events occur from participation, participant is eligible for financial support for medical treatment

### Research in Vulnerable Populations

**Vulnerable population**: a population with insufficient ability to protect or advocate for their interests. The policy on protection is regulated by the Office for Human Research Protection (OHRP).

#### Pregnant Individuals and Fetuses
- Preclinical studies on pregnant animals and clinical studies on nonpregnant individuals must have been conducted prior
- Adequate data on potential risks must be available
- The trial must have potential to directly benefit the pregnant individual and/or fetus
- No other means of answering the research question, and minimal risk
- Investigators may not have any role in determining viability of the fetus
- No incentive may be offered to terminate a pregnancy
- If research only benefits the fetus, paternal consent must also be obtained (unless father is unavailable, incapacitated, or pregnancy resulted from rape or incest)

#### Neonates
- Research question must be crucial and cannot be answered by other means
- Research must provide direct benefit to the neonate at no additional risk
- For neonates of uncertain viability: research must increase probability of survival
- Nonviable neonates: vital functions must not be artificially maintained; heartbeat and respiration must not be terminated by the research

#### Children
- Clinical trials that pose no greater risk of harm to children than adults
- If greater risk: trial only if the research question cannot be answered otherwise and research has potential to directly benefit subjects
- Investigators must obtain assent from the child AND informed consent from the parents

#### Prison Inmates
- Research must investigate health issues directly related to the prison inmate population
- Risks may not be greater than for nonprison volunteers, may not pose more than minimal risk
- At least one IRB member should be a prison inmate or representative
- None of the nonprisoner IRB members may have any affiliation with the prison
- Advantages offered through participation may not be so great as to affect the inmate's ability to weigh risks vs. benefits

### Participant Data Protection
- The HIPAA Privacy Rule establishes guidelines for protecting participant data
- A covered entity may always use or disclose deidentified health information for research
- For individually identifiable health information, at least one of the following is required: participant authorization, waiver from IRB or Privacy Board, or only health information of deceased individuals is used

### Participant Withdrawal from Research
- A participant can withdraw at any time without penalty, loss of benefits, or obligation to explain
- Researchers may ask if the participant is willing to share reasons (with no obligation)
- Researchers may offer participants to opt-in to select components only
- Withdrawal must be documented
- Already collected data may be kept and analyzed
- Data collected under FDA regulations may not be destroyed or omitted from analysis

## Medical Laws

### Involuntary Commitment
- **Definition**: a legal intervention through which an individual experiencing symptoms of a severe mental disorder can be detained in a mental health facility for involuntary treatment
- Criteria differ state-by-state but generally include:
  - Mental illness
  - Grave disability (e.g., inability to self-feed or shelter)
  - Danger to self or others
  - Need for treatment
- The commitment settings should be the least restrictive option possible
- Proceedings usually initiated by a family member or health care provider
- Individuals can typically be held for a period specified by state law without a court order given the admission is medically certified

### Abortion and Stillbirth Laws
- **Stillbirth**: Offering an autopsy of the fetus and placenta is recommended after a confirmed unexplained stillbirth
- **Abortion**: Laws vary greatly state-by-state
  - Most states require parental notification and/or consent for minors
  - Patient counseling prior to abortion is mandatory in some states
  - Most states allow clinicians to refuse to perform abortions under the condition patients are referred to another willing clinician
  - Many states only permit abortions under certain conditions: the pregnant individual's life or health is at risk, the procedure is performed by a licensed physician, the fetus is below a certain gestational age
