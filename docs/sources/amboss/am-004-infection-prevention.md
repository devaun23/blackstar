# Infection Prevention and Control

Health care-based infection prevention and control programs aim to reduce the spread of infections between patients and health care personnel (HCP). The most commonly used methods include standard precautions (universal for all patients) and isolation precautions (designed for specific infectious diseases).

## Standard Precautions

**Definition**: standard practices that should be used for all patients to minimize the spread of infectious material, whether an infection is suspected or not.

Includes:
- Hand and respiratory hygiene
- Personal protective equipment (PPE): use of gloves, gowns, masks, and eye protection as needed
- Aseptic and safe injection techniques
- Routine cleaning, disinfection, and/or sterilization of devices, instruments, and surfaces

## Hand Hygiene

**Definition**: practices used to minimize pathogens on the hands of HCP.

### Options
- **Antiseptic (alcohol-based) hand rub**: preferred method for unsoiled hands (most effective)
- **Handwashing with soap and water**: preferred method for soiled hands

### Basic Precautions
- Nails: cut short, no artificial nails
- No jewelry on the hands or forearms
- Avoid touching the face

### Indications for Alcohol-Based Hand Rub
- Before and after work shifts and breaks
- Before and after contact with each patient and/or their immediate environment
- Moving from contaminated to clean body sites on the same patient
- Handling medication, syringes, invasive equipment, and infusions
- Before putting on and after removing gloves

### Indications for Handwashing with Soap and Water
- Hands are visibly soiled
- Following exposure to spore-forming bacteria (e.g., C. difficile)
- Before eating and after using the restroom
- If hand rub is not available

### Barriers to Hand Hygiene Compliance
- Work overload and time constraints
- Poor situational awareness
- Intolerance or aversion to certain disinfectant formulations
- Lack of a safety culture at the workplace

### Measures to Increase Compliance
- Implement real-time feedback loops (designated observers or electronic systems)
- Repeat training sessions frequently
- Increase number of hand hygiene stations and visual reminders
- Provide alcohol-based hand rubs at stations

### Hand Care
- Use alcohol-based rubs when possible to minimize handwashing
- Avoid using hot water
- Use skin care products (moisturizers, emollients) regularly

Alcohol-based hand rubs are preferred unless hands are visibly soiled or if there has been contact with spore-forming pathogens. Hand rubs are quicker to use, more effective, and less irritating to the skin than handwashing.

## Respiratory Hygiene

Practices to control transmission of respiratory infections:
- Place a mask on coughing patients
- Cover mouth when coughing or sneezing
- Maintain distance of 3-6 ft from others while coughing or sneezing
- Dispose of tissues after use
- Perform hand hygiene after coughing or sneezing

## Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

**Definition**: a collection of physical barriers to protect against and/or prevent the transmission of microbiological, chemical, and/or radiological hazards.

### General Principles
- Work from clean to dirty areas
- Change gloves and gowns when heavily soiled, before using shared equipment, and upon leaving patient's room
- Perform hand hygiene before and after PPE use

### Types of PPE and Indications

#### Gloves
- Anticipated exposure to body fluids, infectious material, mucous membranes, nonintact skin, chemicals
- Aerosol-generating procedures
- Contact precautions

#### Gowns
- Anticipated clothing/body exposure to blood or body fluids
- Aerosol-generating procedures
- Contact precautions

#### Masks
- Anticipated exposure to respiratory secretions, sprays or splashes of blood or body fluids
- Should be worn by coughing patients
- Aerosol-generating procedures
- Procedures requiring sterile technique
- Droplet precautions

#### Respirators (N95, elastomeric, PAPR)
- Aerosol-generating procedures with certain pathogens
- Airborne precautions

#### Face Protection (face shield, goggles)
- Anticipated exposure to respiratory secretions, sprays or splashes

### Donning PPE (putting on)
Order: hand hygiene -> gown -> mask or respirator -> eye protection -> gloves

1. Tie back hair, remove jewelry, clean eyeglasses, check PPE for damage
2. Perform hand hygiene
3. Put on isolation gown and tie all closures
4. Put on mask or respirator and mold nosepiece; for respirator, perform user seal check
5. Place goggles or face shield
6. Put on gloves covering cuffs of gown

Do not touch the front of the mask after entering the patient's room.

### Doffing PPE (removing)
- Do not touch any contaminated part with ungloved hands
- Remove all PPE (except respirator) before leaving contaminated space
- Wash hands between steps any time they are contaminated

Order (Example 1): gloves -> gown -> goggles/face shield -> mask/respirator -> hand hygiene
Order (Example 2): gown+gloves together (inside out) -> goggles/face shield -> mask/respirator -> hand hygiene

## Disinfectants, Antiseptics, and Sterilization

### Common Agents

- **Alcohol-based disinfectants** (isopropyl, ethyl alcohol): membrane damage and protein denaturation. Active against bacteria, enveloped viruses, fungi. NOT sporicidal.
- **Chlorhexidine** (bisbiguanide): cell membrane disruption at low concentrations; precipitates proteins/nucleic acids at high concentrations. NOT sporicidal.
- **Iodine/iodophors** (e.g., povidone-iodine): halogenation of RNA, DNA, proteins. Active against bacteria, enveloped and nonenveloped viruses, fungi. Sporicidal with prolonged contact.
- **Chlorine-releasing agents** (sodium hypochlorite): oxidizing agents that denature proteins and nucleic acids. Sporicidal (effective against Clostridium spores).
- **Hydrogen peroxide**: produces hydroxyl free radicals damaging lipids, proteins, DNA. Sporicidal at higher concentrations.
- **Aldehydes** (glutaraldehyde): alkylation of protein, DNA, RNA. Sporicidal.
- **Quaternary ammonium compounds** (benzalkonium chloride): enzyme inactivation, protein denaturation, membrane disruption. Active against bacteria (not mycobacteria), enveloped viruses, fungi. NOT sporicidal.

### Sterilization
**Definition**: the process of destroying all microbial life, including spores, on a surface or in a fluid.

#### Steam Sterilization (Autoclave)
- Irreversible coagulation and denaturation of enzymes and structural proteins
- Active against bacteria, fungi, viruses, and spores
- Typically 134 degrees C for 3 minutes or 121 degrees C for 15 minutes
- Prions are NOT destroyed by standard autoclaving; must be sterilized at 121-132 degrees C for 60 minutes

#### Dry Air Sterilization
- Denatures and oxidizes proteins and cell components
- 170 degrees C for 60 min, 160 degrees C for 120 min, or 150 degrees C for 150 min

#### Ethylene Oxide Gas Sterilization
- Alkylation of protein, DNA, and RNA
- For heat- and moisture-sensitive equipment
- Disadvantages: lengthy cycle time, costly, hazardous

#### Hydrogen Peroxide Gas Plasma
- Free radical formation damaging enzymes, nucleic acids, cellular metabolism
- Active against bacteria (including mycobacteria), yeasts, fungi, viruses, bacterial spores

### Pasteurization
- Pathogen destruction through brief heating (< 100 degrees C)
- Destroys broad spectrum of bacteria but NOT heat-resistant spores

## Isolation Precautions

Isolation precautions provide additional protection against highly contagious infections, used IN ADDITION to standard precautions.

### General Principles
- Minimize interactions with the patient
- Coordinate tasks to minimize patient encounters
- Perform tasks inside the patient's room if possible
- Place patients in single-patient rooms if possible
- Utilize patient cohorts (grouping by condition) and provider cohorts (single provider per condition)

### Contact Precautions
- **Transmission**: direct contact with body fluids or fomites
- **Used for**: drug-resistant pathogens (MRSA, VRE), enteric infections (C. difficile, E. coli O157:H7), skin conditions (scabies, impetigo, varicella, draining abscesses)
- **Required PPE**: gloves and gowns, even without direct contact expected
- Dedicate medical equipment to single patient; disinfect before reuse if not possible

### Droplet Precautions
- **Transmission**: large respiratory droplets (> 5 micrometers) via coughing, sneezing, talking
- **Used for**: N. meningitidis, B. pertussis, Influenza, Parainfluenza, Adenovirus, H. influenzae type B, M. pneumoniae, Rubella
- **Required PPE**: mask when within 6 ft of patient; patient should also wear mask during transport

### Airborne Precautions
- **Transmission**: aerosolized particles (< 5 micrometers) that remain suspended in air
- **Used for**: tuberculosis, varicella
- **Goal**: prevent contaminated air from escaping patient's room
- **Airborne infection isolation rooms (AIIRs)**: constant negative air pressure, 6-12 air cycles/hour, HEPA filtration of outgoing air
- **Required PPE**: fitted N95 respirator or PAPR when entering patient's room
- Patient should wear surgical mask during transport

### Protective Environment for Immunosuppressed Patients
- **Indication**: allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) recipients
- **Goal**: keep potentially infectious air OUT of patient's room (reverse isolation)
- Positive pressure rooms with HEPA filtration of incoming air
- No carpet, upholstery, flowers, or potted plants
- Daily surface disinfection
- Patients should only leave room for diagnostic and therapeutic procedures
- During hospital construction: patients wear N95 respirators outside room

## Prevention of Community Spread

### General Measures
- **Isolation**: separation of persons with confirmed or suspected highly infectious pathogen
- **Quarantine**: separation of asymptomatic individuals who have been exposed
- **Contact tracing**: identifying source of infection and preventing further spread
- **Postexposure prophylaxis (PEP)**: treatment following exposure to a highly infectious pathogen (immunizations, antibiotics, antivirals, immunoglobulins)

### Epidemic Control
- Social distancing: maintain 6 ft distance, avoid congregate settings and mass gatherings
- Lockdown: governments mandate individuals not to leave homes unless necessary

## Prevention of Healthcare-Associated Infections (HAIs)

### General Infection Control
- Follow standard precautions
- Screen patients for common HAI causes (e.g., MRSA) and isolate appropriately
- Use antibiotics judiciously to prevent antimicrobial resistance
- Ensure HCP do not work when ill and are up to date on vaccinations

### General Precautions for Indwelling Devices
- Only insert when clearly indicated; consider less invasive alternatives
- Inspect devices daily and provide proper care
- Assess daily whether device is still needed; remove as soon as no longer required
- Consider hospital bundles to automate prevention steps
- Do not routinely use systemic prophylactic antibiotics

### Prevention of CAUTIs
- Consider whether catheterization is necessary; consider alternatives (CIC, condom catheters)
- Select smallest catheter necessary
- Use sterile technique during insertion
- Antimicrobial catheters are not routinely recommended
- Perform daily maintenance; clean genital and meatal area with soap and water

### Prevention of CLABSIs/CRBSIs
- Consider PICC; avoid femoral lines in adults if possible
- Skin preparation with chlorhexidine and alcohol
- Hand hygiene and strict aseptic technique
- Place line under ultrasound guidance
- Replace emergently inserted lines within 2 days
- Perform regular maintenance

### Prevention of Ventilator-Associated Infections
- Consider alternatives (NIPPV); oral rather than nasal intubation if possible
- Oral care with sterile water
- Ventilator bundle: lowest sedation, early exercise and mobilization, minimize secretion pooling, elevate head of bed 30-45 degrees, maintain ventilator circuit

### Prevention of Surgical Site Infections
- Alcohol-based surgical skin preparation
- Follow guidelines for IV antimicrobial prophylaxis
- Prior to elective operations: advise smoking cessation 1 month prior, treat all infections, advise bathing the night before
- Perioperatively: maintain blood glucose < 200 mg/dL, normothermia, increased FiO2 under general anesthesia
- Postprocedural: do not apply antimicrobial agents to incision, inspect dressings regularly

## Health Care Personnel Exposures

### Types of Exposure
- Percutaneous injuries (needlestick, sharps): highest risk for bloodborne virus transmission
- Bites
- Through nonintact skin
- Splashes onto mucous membranes

### Prevention
- Follow standard and isolation precautions and use PPE
- Avoid using needles if possible (needle-free IV systems)
- Use safety devices (blunt-tip suture needles, safety syringes, safety scalpels)
- Do not remove contaminated needle from syringe
- Do not recap or bend needles
- Use neutral zone rather than directly handing sharps
- Dispose in appropriate sharps containers
- Consider double gloving

### Approach After Exposure
1. **First aid**: wash with soap and water (percutaneous); rinse under running water (nonintact skin); flush with water or saline (mucous membranes/eyes)
2. Notify supervisor and occupational health department
3. Immediately seek medical care
4. Obtain history about the exposure
5. Order laboratory studies for both exposed HCP and source patient
6. Arrange follow-up and provide PEP if indicated

### Baseline Laboratory Studies
- **Source patient**: HIV Ag/Ab (+ rapid test), HBsAg (if HCP nonimmune), HCV RNA or anti-HCV
- **Exposed HCP**: HIV Ag/Ab, anti-HBs (if nonimmune), anti-HCV

### Postexposure Prophylaxis
- HIV PEP: antiretroviral therapy
- Hepatitis B PEP: HBIG and/or hepatitis B vaccine (only in HCP without documented immunity)
- Tetanus prophylaxis: tetanus booster and/or immunoglobulin
- There is NO postexposure prophylaxis for hepatitis C

### Additional Precautions During Follow-up
- Any bloodborne pathogen: avoid donations of blood, plasma, organs, tissue, semen
- HIV: use barrier contraception, avoid breastfeeding and becoming pregnant
