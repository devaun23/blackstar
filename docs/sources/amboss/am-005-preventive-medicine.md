# Preventive Medicine

Preventive medicine aims to prevent disease occurrence as well as disease progression and complications after disease onset. The five levels of preventive medicine target all stages of disease.

## Public Health Interventions

Public health interventions are actions and policies introduced by public health authorities to protect and improve the health of a population. Population-based practice focuses on the health concerns of entire populations and intervenes at the individual, community, and systemic levels.

### Case Finding and Epidemiology
- **Surveillance (public health)**: collection, analysis, and interpretation of data required for planning and evaluation of public health interventions
- **Outreach**: identifying at-risk populations and informing them about prevention, mitigation, and treatment
- **Case finding**: identifying individuals and families with risk factors and providing educational resources and medical referrals
- **Health event and disease investigation**: gathering data on reported threats to population health to develop control measures
- **Screening**: identification of asymptomatic or at-risk individuals in a population

### Diagnosis and Management
- **Referral and follow-up**: directing individuals to health care resources and assessing outcomes
- **Case management**: collaborating with individuals and providers to plan, coordinate, and assess care
- **Delegated functions**: entrusting health care tasks to other authorized personnel

### Information, Education, Empowerment
- **Counseling**: developing interpersonal rapport to facilitate coping and self-care
- **Consultation**: deliberation between health care workers and/or experts
- **Health teaching**: educating the public about health-related conditions and behaviors

### Partnerships and Networks
- **Collaboration**: coordinating cooperation between institutions, groups, individuals
- **Community organizing**: uniting individuals to identify and solve public health problems
- **Coalition building**: developing alliances among organizations to solve public health problems

### Policy Development, Promotion, and Enforcement
- **Health advocacy**: promoting public health through collaboration with stakeholders and engagement with policymakers
- **Social marketing**: deploying marketing techniques to positively influence health behaviors
- **Policy development and enforcement**: working with decision-makers to develop laws and regulations

## Primordial Prevention

- **Definition**: actions that aim to prevent the development of risk factors for disease
- **Target**: entire population
- **Goal**: encourage positive and discourage negative lifestyle habits
- **Strategies**: health promotion, mass education, legislation
- **Examples**:
  - Programs on food safety and nutrition guidelines
  - Campaigns discouraging tobacco and drug use (e.g., smokefree air laws)
  - Building bicycle lanes and sidewalks to promote physical activity

Primordial prevention aims to prevent risk factors from developing, whereas primary prevention targets existing risk factors to prevent the onset of a disease.

## Primary Prevention

- **Definition**: actions targeted at preventing specific diseases from occurring to decrease incidence and, subsequently, prevalence
- **Target**: entire population or selected groups without disease who are at increased risk
- **Goal**: decrease incidence and prevalence of a specific disease
- **Strategies**: health promotion, environmental modifications, specific protection interventions (immunizations, chemoprophylaxis)
- **Examples**:
  - Immunization
  - Lifestyle modification (smoking cessation, exercise, dental care)
  - Fortification of salt with iodine
  - Fluoridation of toothpaste, water, and salt
  - Fortification of food with folic acid to reduce neural tube defects
  - Health legislation (seat belt laws, food safety standards)
  - Routine neonatal ophthalmic antibiotic prophylaxis
  - Postexposure prophylaxis

Primary prevention helps to Prevent disease.

## Secondary Prevention

- **Definition**: actions targeted at early detection of disease in asymptomatic individuals to promote timely intervention
- **Target**: individuals at risk for disease
- **Goal**: detect disease and initiate treatment early to improve chance of treatment success
- **Strategy**: two-step process: screening test to identify disease, then follow-up for disease management
- **Examples**: cancer screening, metabolic screening, prenatal screening

Screening complements diagnostics, but it is not a substitute.

Secondary prevention helps Screen.

## Tertiary Prevention

- **Definition**: actions taken to optimize the care of patients with an existing disease to improve well-being and prevent complications
- **Target**: individuals with an established disease
- **Goal**: decrease morbidity and mortality by preventing disease progression and reducing risk of relapse
- **Strategies**: chronic disease management, strategies to improve treatment adherence
- **Examples**:
  - Adjuvant therapy to reduce risk of cancer recurrence (e.g., tamoxifen in breast cancer)
  - Blood pressure management to reduce risk of cardiovascular events
  - Diabetes management to reduce risk of chronic kidney disease and/or cardiovascular events
  - Low-dose aspirin to prevent recurrent MI

Tertiary prevention helps Treat.

## Quaternary Prevention

- **Definition**: actions taken to avoid medical interventions in which the harms may outweigh the benefits
- **Target**: individuals with potential to undergo unnecessary or inappropriate interventions
- **Goal**: prevent medical overuse (overdiagnosis and overtreatment) and avoidable iatrogenesis
- **Strategies**:
  - Practicing evidence-based medicine
  - Using shared decision-making before initiating interventions
  - Educating providers and patients on potential harms (e.g., Choosing Wisely campaign)
  - Avoidance of exploitation of medical conditions to sell products/procedures
- **Examples of overdiagnosis**:
  - Screening individuals outside recommended age range (e.g., cervical cancer screening in individuals > 65 without risk factors)
  - MRI for lower back pain < 6 weeks without red flags
  - Routine pelvic examination before prescribing contraception
- **Examples of overtreatment**:
  - Antibiotics for viral infections
  - Opioids in chronic noncancer pain
  - Antibiotics for acute mild to moderate sinusitis
- **Examples of avoidable iatrogenesis**:
  - Combined hormonal contraception in patients with absolute contraindications (e.g., migraine with aura)
  - Continuing sulfonylureas despite multiple hypoglycemic events

Quaternary prevention helps squeeze out unnecessary interventions.

## Prevention Paradox

**Definition**: A preventive measure that benefits a population as a whole will offer little benefit to each individual member (population approach). A preventive measure that benefits a group of individuals susceptible to a disease will offer little benefit to the population as a whole (high-risk approach).

Key points:
- The high-risk approach and the population approach are complementary
- Preventive medicine should prioritize preventing underlying causes of disease (primordial and primary) over reducing impact after disease occurs (secondary and tertiary)
- The paradox may lead to misconceptions: that a measure providing no immediate individual benefit provides no population benefit, or that a small risk of a measure (e.g., vaccination) outweighs the benefits
- Misconceptions may negatively affect epidemiological policy and adherence
- Effective primordial and primary prevention require consistent, long-term education programs

**Examples**:
- While heavy drinking carries greater individual risk than moderate drinking, moderate drinking has greater negative population impact because moderate drinkers are far more numerous
- Seatbelt laws have prevented many severe injuries, yet the overall risk of dying without a seatbelt is still low

## Disease Outbreak Investigation

### Definitions
- **Disease cluster**: an unusual aggregation of cases grouped in time and space
- **Disease outbreak**: the sudden occurrence of more cases than expected in a given area, population, and/or season

### Identifying Outbreaks
- Identified through reports from hospitals, laboratories, providers, and the general population
- Health authorities decide whether to investigate further

### Steps of Field Investigation
1. Preparing for the investigation (gather information, organize team, gather supplies)
2. Confirming the outbreak
3. Establishing a case definition
4. Finding cases and documenting details of each case
5. Developing a hypothesis about the cause
6. Analyzing gathered data and evaluating the hypothesis
7. Comparing and reconciling with laboratory and/or environmental studies
8. Developing and implementing control and prevention measures
9. Initiating or maintaining surveillance
10. Communicating findings to the public
