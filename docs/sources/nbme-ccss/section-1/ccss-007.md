# Item 7 — Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia / Pulmonary Hypoplasia

## Clinical Scenario
3402g (7lb 8oz) male newborn with severe respiratory distress immediately after delivery. Born at term following uncomplicated pregnancy and delivery. Gasping for air. Trachea deviated to the right. Breath sounds markedly decreased over the left hemithorax compared to the right. Heart sounds shifted to the right. Abdomen is markedly scaphoid.

## Decision Point
This patient is at greatest risk for death from which of the following?

## Options
- A) Congenital cardiac disease with low output
- B) Increased alveolar-arterial oxygen gradient
- C) Intrapulmonary shunting
- **D) Pulmonary hypoplasia** ✓
- E) Ventilation-perfusion mismatch

## Correct Answer: D
This newborn has congenital diaphragmatic hernia — herniation of abdominal organs into the thorax compresses the developing lungs in utero. Pulmonary hypoplasia results from direct compression with reduced branching and vascular architecture. The scaphoid abdomen (contents have herniated into the chest), absent left-sided breath sounds, and trachea/heart shifted to the right are classic. Newborns with pulmonary hypoplasia develop persistent pulmonary hypertension due to increased pulmonary vascular resistance, resulting in potentially severe heart failure. The greatest risk of death is from the overall respiratory insufficiency from pulmonary hypoplasia.

## Educational Objective
Pulmonary hypoplasia occurs as a sequela of congenital diaphragmatic hernia and results in serious and potentially life-threatening consequences to pulmonary and cardiac function. Pulmonary hypoplasia is caused by compression of the lungs by herniated abdominal organs with secondary changes in bronchial and vascular development.

## Common Errors
- **A (Low cardiac output)**: Can occur secondary to pulmonary hypoplasia but is not the primary cause of death.
- **B (Increased A-a gradient)**: Will occur due to thickened alveolar septa but death results from the overall respiratory insufficiency rather than the gradient alone.
- **C (Intrapulmonary shunting)**: Likely to occur but is less likely to be the sole cause of death compared to the overall pulmonary hypoplasia.
- **E (V/Q mismatch)**: Common in many pulmonary pathologies but less likely in this case where the primary issue is complete hypoplasia of lung tissue.
