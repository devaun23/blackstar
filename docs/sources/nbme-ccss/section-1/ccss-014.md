# Item 14 — Caudal Regression Syndrome / Stillbirth

## Clinical Scenario
35yo primigravid woman brought to ED by paramedics after a neighbor called emergency services because the patient was being disruptive and combative. En route, fingerstick blood glucose 45 mg/dL, IV 50% dextrose administered, mental status improved. 10-year history of T2DM treated with insulin. Fully oriented on arrival. Smoked two packs of cigarettes daily for 23 years. Not yet initiated prenatal care. Does not drink alcohol or use illicit drugs. Vital signs within normal limits. On exam, uterus is nontender, fundal height is 2cm below the umbilicus. Fetal heart rate 146/min. Pelvic US shows a single intrauterine fetus consistent with 17-week gestation; thighs and legs are fused, and there are two femoral and two tibial bones within a single soft-tissue and muscular compartment. Amniotic fluid index is 2cm (N=10-20).

## Decision Point
Most likely outcome of this patient's pregnancy?

## Options
- A) Cerebral palsy
- B) Macrosomia
- C) Microcephaly
- D) Neonatal hypoglycemia
- **E) Stillbirth** ✓

## Correct Answer: E
Caudal regression syndrome is a congenital condition characterized by partial or complete absence of the sacrum, and in some cases the lower lumbar spine. It falls under the umbrella of diabetic embryopathies — conditions caused by poor glycemic control and hyperglycemia prior to and during early pregnancy. Abnormal methionine/folate uptake leads to aberrant neural crest migration in the caudal and presagittal pathway. This fetus has minimal differentiation of the bilateral lower extremities (fused thighs and legs) and an amniotic fluid index of 2cm (20% of normal) — indicating significant developmental abnormalities incompatible with survival.

## Educational Objective
Caudal regression syndrome is a congenital condition characterized by the partial or complete absence of the sacrum, and in some cases the lower lumbar spine. This is most commonly caused by poorly controlled diabetes prior to and during gestation. The clinical presentation and prognosis are dependent on the severity of the lesion and the level of neurologic compromise.

## Common Errors
- **A (Cerebral palsy)**: Not generally associated with diabetic embryopathy or caudal regression syndrome. CNS defects in caudal regression typically stem from neural tube defects.
- **B (Macrosomia)**: Generally associated with poorly controlled diabetes during pregnancy, but the significant caudal regression and gross malformation make stillbirth more likely.
- **C (Microcephaly)**: Associated with oligohydramnios, but given the significant lack of amniotic fluid, the extent of caudal abnormalities makes stillbirth more likely.
- **D (Neonatal hypoglycemia)**: A known complication of gestational diabetes, but this fetus is not expected to survive to delivery given the severity of abnormalities.
