# Item 11 — Hepatitis A Prognosis

## Clinical Scenario
37yo man, 2-week history of fever, progressive fatigue, abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting. Dark urine, clay-colored stools. Traveled to Central America 6 weeks ago and ate local food including raw shellfish and vegetables. Temp 38C, pulse 90, resp 20, BP 110/85. Scleral icterus. Liver tender, palpated 6cm below right costal margin. Labs: Hgb 15, MCV 100, Total bilirubin 7.1, Alk phos 105, AST 2000, ALT 1500.

## Decision Point
Without treatment, most likely outcome of this patient's condition?

## Options
- A) Chronic carrier state
- B) Chronic hepatitis
- C) Fulminant hepatitis
- D) Hepatocellular carcinoma
- **E) Complete resolution of symptoms** ✓

## Correct Answer: E
This patient has hepatitis A virus (HAV) infection — nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, icterus, hepatomegaly, and markedly elevated transaminases following travel to Central America and raw shellfish consumption. HAV is transmitted fecal-oral, is a nonenveloped RNA virus, and has an incubation period of several weeks. Diagnosis confirmed by anti-HAV IgM antibodies. HAV infection is typically self-limited and does not progress to chronic infection. Complete resolution of symptoms is the expected outcome.

## Educational Objective
Hepatitis A virus is a common cause of food poisoning and traveler's diarrhea among travelers in the developing world. The disease course of hepatitis A is generally benign with fever, nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea, and further complications are rare. Vaccination against hepatitis A virus is recommended to all travelers who visit developing countries.

## Common Errors
- **A (Chronic carrier state)**: Not associated with HAV. Once infected, HAV is self-limiting and confers immunity.
- **B (Chronic hepatitis)** and **D (HCC)**: Not associated with HAV. Hepatitis B and C are more commonly associated with chronic infection, leading to cirrhosis and potentially hepatocellular carcinoma.
- **C (Fulminant hepatitis)**: Not commonly seen with HAV. In rare cases HAV can become fulminant, but it is significantly more likely to continue its course as a self-limited illness with no long-term effects.
