# Item 15 — Osteomalacia / Vitamin D Deficiency / Elevated PTH

## Clinical Scenario
92yo woman brought to ED with 1-week history of generalized fatigue and weakness. Resident in a skilled nursing facility for 30 years and is nonambulatory. She became agitated when medical staff tried to lift her arms and legs to move her on the bed. Has intestinal malabsorption syndrome, pulmonary fibrosis, dementia, and global deconditioning. Receives chronic parenteral nutrition. Appears pale, oriented to person but not place or time. Follows simple commands. Pulsation of upper extremities and thighs produces pain. Can flex and extend elbows and dorsiflex and plantarflex ankles and extend toes bilaterally. Sensation is intact over extremities. Skeletal survey shows pseudofractures of the femur and proximal ulna bilaterally.

## Decision Point
Which of the following serum concentrations is most likely to be increased in this patient?

## Options
- A) Calcium
- B) 1,25-Hydroxyvitamin D
- C) Magnesium
- **D) Parathyroid hormone** ✓
- E) Phosphorus

## Correct Answer: D
This patient has osteomalacia secondary to vitamin D deficiency. Vitamin D plays a role in serum calcium and phosphate homeostasis by promoting intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphorus, increasing bone mineralization at low concentrations, and bone resorption at higher concentrations. Elderly patients are at particularly increased risk for vitamin D deficiency due to decreased vitamin D synthesis with aging, malabsorption, insufficient dietary intake, and decreased sun exposure. Decreased vitamin D leads to decreased intestinal calcium absorption and hypocalcemia, sensed by the parathyroid gland via calcium-sensing receptors — leading to an increase in secretion of parathyroid hormone (PTH) to normalize serum calcium levels. Increased PTH stimulates osteoclastic bone reabsorption and distal convoluted tubular calcium reabsorption and phosphate excretion by the kidney.

## Educational Objective
Vitamin D deficiency can be caused by intestinal malabsorption, malnutrition, insufficient dietary intake, and decreased sun exposure. Osteomalacia is an adult form of vitamin D deficiency characterized by loss of bone mineral density and abnormal osteoid mineralization. Pseudofractures (Looser zones), also known as insufficiency fractures, are a characteristic radiographic finding. Patients present with nonspecific symptoms including muscular weakness, myalgia, bone pain, and in later stages may have bone deformity, gait alteration, and falls. Treatment is with oral supplementation of both vitamin D and calcium.

## Common Errors
- **A (Calcium)**: Usually decreased in patients with osteomalacia, though may be normal. Serum calcium may be encountered in primary or secondary hyperparathyroidism, but this patient's pseudofractures and advanced age suggest vitamin D deficiency and osteomalacia, not primary hyperparathyroidism.
- **B (1,25-Hydroxyvitamin D)**: Decreased in osteomalacia. Increased concentrations of 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D can be encountered in hypervitaminosis D, which is caused by excessive intake of vitamin D supplements.
- **C (Magnesium)**: Typically normal in patients with osteomalacia. Hypermagnesemia is rare, found in end-stage kidney disease or excessive magnesium supplementation.
- **E (Phosphorus)**: Often decreased in patients with osteomalacia. PTH increases urinary excretion of phosphorus. Hyperphosphatemia is commonly encountered in secondary hyperparathyroidism caused by end-stage kidney disease.
