# Item 12 — SLE Pericarditis

## Clinical Scenario
35yo woman with SLE presents to ED with 3-day history of sharp midchest pain that worsens with inspiration and lying on her back. Only medication is hydroxychloroquine. Appears mildly uncomfortable. Temp 38.1C, pulse 96, resp 14, BP 135/87. Malar rash. Lungs clear. Scratchy, high-pitched sound heard over the lower left sternal border on cardiac exam. Erythema, swelling, and warmth of the wrists. ECG shown (diffuse ST elevation with PR depression).

## Decision Point
Most appropriate initial pharmacotherapy?

## Options
- A) Clopidogrel
- B) Cyclophosphamide
- C) Enoxaparin
- **D) Ibuprofen** ✓
- E) Rituximab

## Correct Answer: D
This patient with lupus presents with pleuritic chest pain (worse with inspiration and supine, improved with leaning forward) and a friction rub (scratchy, high-pitched sound at lower left sternal border) — classic pericarditis. ECG shows diffuse ST elevation with PR depression, further supporting the diagnosis. Pericarditis can occur in inflammatory, infectious, or malignant conditions, and can present as a complication of myocardial infarction. Treatment requires anti-inflammatory medications such as ibuprofen, glucocorticoids, and/or colchicine.

## Educational Objective
Pericarditis classically presents with central chest pain, which is often pleuritic, worse when supine, and improves with leaning forward. On cardiac examination, pericarditis discloses a diastolic friction rub. ECG may show diffuse ST elevation with PR depressions. Treatment requires anti-inflammatory medications such as ibuprofen, glucocorticoids, and/or colchicine.

## Common Errors
- **A (Clopidogrel)**: Antiplatelet for ACS. Although lupus patients are at increased risk for coronary artery disease, this patient's pleuritic pain pattern and ECG are more consistent with pericarditis than ACS.
- **B (Cyclophosphamide)** and **E (Rituximab)**: Both are immunosuppressants used for severe manifestations of lupus, especially lupus nephritis. Pericarditis is treated with anti-inflammatory medications first.
- **C (Enoxaparin)**: Anticoagulant used for DVT/PE or ACS. This patient's presentation is consistent with pericarditis, not thromboembolic disease.
