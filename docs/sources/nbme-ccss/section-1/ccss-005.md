# Item 5 — End-of-Life Goals of Care

## Clinical Scenario
56yo woman with recurrent ovarian cancer, admitted for inoperable small-bowel obstruction. Four courses of chemotherapy over 5 years, cancer continues to progress. No current medications. NG tube placed. Temp 37C, pulse 80, resp 12, BP 120/70. Abdomen distended with decreased bowel sounds.

## Decision Point
Most appropriate next step in management?

## Options
- A) Discharge to hospice care
- **B) Family meeting to determine goals of care** ✓
- C) Ileostomy
- D) Intravenous total parenteral nutrition
- E) Placement of a percutaneous gastrostomy tube

## Correct Answer: B
As patients near end of life, medical complications that decrease quality of life commonly accumulate. It is critical to weigh benefits and drawbacks of further interventions. Family meetings to determine goals of care help clarify the patient's wishes. The patient may opt to forgo invasive interventions that prolong life but decrease quality of life, and may elect hospice care. If incapacitated, close family, durable powers of attorney, and advance directives should guide decisions.

## Educational Objective
As patients near the end of their life, physicians should facilitate discussions with the patient and their family to clarify their wishes for ongoing care. Some patients may favor life-prolonging interventions while others may opt to prioritize quality of life. Hospice care prioritizes quality of life over prolongation of life.

## Common Errors
- **A (Discharge to hospice)**: Premature — must first clarify the patient's wishes through a family meeting. She may still want life-prolonging interventions.
- **C (Ileostomy)**, **D (TPN)**, **E (Gastrostomy tube)**: All are invasive procedures. Her small-bowel obstruction has been deemed inoperable. Before initiating any invasive intervention, the physician must first facilitate discussion about goals of care.
