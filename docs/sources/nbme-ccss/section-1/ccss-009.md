# Item 9 — Infectious Mononucleosis

## Clinical Scenario
15yo boy, 3-day history of fever, sore throat, and fatigue. Has hemophilia A and requires recombinant factor VIII therapy as needed. Temp 38.2C (100.8F), pulse 90, resp 24, BP 100/70. Exudative tonsillopharyngitis and bilateral lymphadenopathy. Tenderness to palpation of the right upper quadrant. Liver span 14cm, spleen tip palpated at left costal margin.

## Decision Point
Most appropriate next step in management?

## Options
- A) CT scan of the liver
- **B) Measurement of serum AST and ALT activities** ✓
- C) Measurement of serum ceruloplasmin concentration
- D) Measurement of serum electrolyte and glucose concentrations
- E) Ultrasonography of the liver

## Correct Answer: B
This presentation is classic for infectious mononucleosis caused by Epstein-Barr virus — fever, pharyngitis, lymphadenopathy, and hepatosplenomegaly in an adolescent. EBV is transmitted through respiratory secretions and saliva. Atypical lymphocytes (CD8+ T cells reacting to the viral infection) are seen on peripheral smear. Patients commonly present with cholestatic hepatitis. Liver function testing (AST/ALT) typically shows mildly increased serum concentrations of transaminases, alkaline phosphatase, and bilirubin. Severely increased transaminase concentrations should prompt evaluation for other causes (CMV, viral hepatitis). Assessment of hepatic status is particularly important in this patient with hemophilia.

## Educational Objective
Infectious mononucleosis presents with fatigue, malaise, fever, chills, and sore throat, most commonly caused by EBV. Physical examination may disclose pharyngitis with erythema and exudates, posterior cervical lymphadenopathy, and hepatosplenomegaly. Patients may present with cholestatic hepatitis. Liver function testing typically shows mildly increased serum concentrations of transaminases, alkaline phosphatase, and bilirubin. Treatment is supportive.

## Common Errors
- **A (CT liver)**: Not necessary for hepatosplenomegaly in the setting of infectious mononucleosis.
- **C (Ceruloplasmin)**: Tests for Wilson disease (copper transport disorder). Wilson disease presents with neurologic findings (dysarthria, parkinsonism, choreoathetosis) and this patient lacks those features.
- **D (Electrolytes/glucose)**: May be needed in mononucleosis patients with signs of volume depletion or poor oral intake, but is not the most appropriate next step.
- **E (US of liver)**: Not initially necessary. US may provide increased ability to detect hepatosplenomegaly but is equivocal when physical exam is readily identified.
