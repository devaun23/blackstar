# Item 13 — Progressive Supranuclear Palsy

## Clinical Scenario
70yo man brought to office by his niece because of 6-month history of frequent falls. He falls backward suddenly while walking approximately once monthly. Tends to look down at his feet while walking. Speech has become slurred, difficulty reading, choking episodes while eating, difficulty with constipation, changes in mood, and hallucinations. No light-headedness, difficulty with urination, or tremor. Impaired visual gaze that worsens when he attempts to look down. Decreased stride length in gait and impaired balance while turning. Speech is soft with poor enunciation and impaired letter fluency. Mild nuchal rigidity and symmetric rigidity of all extremities.

## Decision Point
Most likely diagnosis?

## Options
- A) Cerebellar astrocytoma
- B) Cervical myelopathy
- C) Lewy body dementia
- D) Parkinson disease
- **E) Progressive supranuclear palsy** ✓

## Correct Answer: E
Progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP) is an atypical parkinsonian disorder with tau-positive neurofibrillary tangles in the basal ganglia, cortex, and brainstem. Patients typically present around age 65 with gait instability, oculomotor deficits (classically vertical gaze palsy), frontal lobe deficits, bradykinesia, and muscle rigidity. The gait pattern varies in step length and base width, though patients typically walk and pivot too quickly — resulting in frequent backward falls. Definitive diagnosis occurs by neuropathologic examination. Death typically occurs within 6-9 years after disease onset. Management is supportive and includes physical and occupational therapy along with advanced care planning.

## Common Errors
- **A (Cerebellar astrocytoma)**: Would present with ataxia, intention tremor, and nystagmus. This patient's gait is narrow-based rather than wide-based, and muscle rigidity would not be expected.
- **B (Cervical myelopathy)**: Refers to spinal cord dysfunction at the level of the cervical spine. Presents with upper motor neuron findings, weakness, hyperreflexia, and atrophy. This patient does not exhibit bladder/bowel dysfunction, sensory signs, or upper/lower motor neuron signs.
- **C (Lewy body dementia)**: Characterized by fluctuating alterations in cognitive function, parkinsonism, visual hallucinations, and REM sleep behavior disorder. This patient has vertical gaze palsy, which is atypical of Lewy body dementia.
- **D (Parkinson disease)**: Presents with bradykinesia, resting tremor, rigidity, and postural instability. Some patients with Parkinson disease exhibit pseudobulbar palsy. This patient also has vertical gaze palsy, which is atypical of Parkinson disease.
