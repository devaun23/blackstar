# Item 1 — Latent TB Management

## Clinical Scenario
27yo woman, Haitian immigrant, routine exam. T2DM on metformin. All childhood vaccinations including BCG 20 years ago. Asymptomatic, normal exam. Fingerstick glucose 168. PPD produces 12mm induration at 48hrs. CXR normal.

## Decision Point
Most appropriate next step in management?

## Options
- A) Repeat PPD skin test in 6 months
- B) Repeat x-ray of the chest in 3 months
- **C) Begin isoniazid therapy** ✓
- D) Begin isoniazid therapy only if the patient becomes symptomatic
- E) Begin isoniazid, rifampin, and pyrazinamide therapy

## Correct Answer: C
This patient has latent TB infection — positive PPD (>=10mm for high-risk groups), negative CXR, and risk factors (recent immigration from high-prevalence country). BCG vaccination in childhood can cause false-positive PPD, but CDC recommends interpreting PPD the same for BCG-vaccinated and non-BCG individuals. Treatment for latent TB should be initiated for patients with >10mm induration and risk factors. Isoniazid monotherapy (or rifamycin-based regimens) is appropriate for latent TB with favorable completion rates and low hepatotoxicity.

## Educational Objective
Latent TB is a clinical diagnosis based on positive immune response to TB antigens and patient risk factors. Treatment with isoniazid or rifamycin should be initiated for patients with a positive PPD or IGRA, negative CXR, and positive risk factors.

## Common Errors
- **A (Repeat PPD in 6 months)** and **B (Repeat CXR in 3 months)**: Unnecessary — patient already has positive PPD, negative CXR, and risk factors. Treatment should be initiated now.
- **D (Wait for symptoms)**: Inappropriate — latent TB should be treated before progression to active disease. By the time symptoms appear, TB could be active and transmissible.
- **E (Triple therapy)**: This regimen is for active TB, not latent TB infection.

## Key Teaching Points
- PPD >=10mm is positive for high-risk groups (immigrants, IV drug users, healthcare workers, homeless shelter residents)
- BCG history does NOT change PPD interpretation per CDC guidelines
- Latent TB = positive PPD/IGRA + negative CXR + no symptoms
- Treat latent TB with isoniazid monotherapy or rifamycin-based regimen
- Active TB requires multi-drug therapy (isoniazid + rifampin + pyrazinamide +/- ethambutol)
