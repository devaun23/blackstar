# Item 10 — Anaphylaxis / Type I Hypersensitivity

## Clinical Scenario
12-month-old girl brought to physician 6 hours after onset of swelling of the lips, itchy rash over face and hands, drooling, vomited twice. Daughter had a similar rash once before that resolved spontaneously within 1 hour. Breast-fed, eats some table foods. Prior to today's symptoms, she ate ice cream for the first time. Alert and active. Multiple erythematous macular lesions over face, neck, hands, and wrists. Bilateral expiratory wheezing heard on auscultation.

## Decision Point
Most likely to confirm the diagnosis?

## Options
- A) Measurement of serum IgA concentration
- B) Rapid streptococcal testing
- **C) Radioallergosorbent testing** ✓
- D) X-ray of the chest
- E) X-ray of the lateral neck

## Correct Answer: C
This is anaphylaxis — a severe type I (immediate) hypersensitivity reaction presenting with urticaria, angioedema (lip swelling), wheezing, and GI symptoms after exposure to a new food allergen (ice cream — likely cow's milk protein). The prior similar rash suggests previous sensitization. Radioallergosorbent testing (RAST) identifies specific IgE antibodies against particular allergens, and can be used to identify the specific trigger. Milk protein allergy can occur in both breast-fed and formula-fed infants. The ice cream may have contained traces of another allergen as well.

## Educational Objective
Anaphylaxis is an IgE-mediated hypersensitivity reaction that leads to extensive degranulation of mast cells and basophils, which results in acute multisystem inflammation and vasodilation. Symptoms include bronchospasm with associated dyspnea or wheezing, urticaria, flushing, nausea and emesis, edematous lips and tongue, and hypotension with tachycardia. Treatment begins with urgent administration of epinephrine, followed by antihistamines, bronchodilators, glucocorticoids, and fluids. Following acute treatment, radioallergosorbent testing can be used to identify triggers.

## Common Errors
- **A (Serum IgA)**: Useful for diagnosing IgA deficiency, which can cause anaphylactic reactions to blood transfusions. Selective IgA deficiency is not associated with anaphylactic reactions to food allergens.
- **B (Rapid strep)**: Diagnoses group A streptococcal pharyngitis. This patient's symptoms (urticaria, angioedema, wheezing after new food) are consistent with type I hypersensitivity, not pharyngitis.
- **D (CXR)**: Useful for diagnosing intrathoracic pathologies. Bilateral expiratory wheezing with urticaria, angioedema, and vomiting is more consistent with anaphylaxis.
- **E (Lateral neck X-ray)**: Diagnoses neck pathologies such as epiglottitis and croup. These conditions can present with drooling and stridor but vomiting and urticarial rash following new food consumption is more consistent with type I hypersensitivity.
