# Item 4 — Testicular Torsion

## Clinical Scenario
12yo otherwise healthy boy, sudden onset of severe right testicular pain 4 hours ago. No trauma history. Right testis is palpated in the upper hemiscrotum, exquisitely tender and twice the size of the left (normal). Remainder of exam normal, CBC and UA normal. Morphine administered.

## Decision Point
Most appropriate next step in management?

## Options
- A) Reexamination in 4 hours
- B) Technetium 99m scan of the right testis
- C) Intravenous administration of antibiotics
- D) Reduction of the scrotal mass
- **E) Emergency orchidopexy** ✓

## Correct Answer: E
This is a classic presentation of testicular torsion — rotation of the testes around the spermatic cord causing vascular compromise with edema, ischemia, and potential necrosis. Presents with acute severe testicular pain, tenderness to palpation, scrotal swelling, and a diminished or absent cremasteric reflex. The "high-riding" testis oriented transversely is characteristic. This is a surgical emergency requiring emergent detorsion and orchidopexy (surgical fixation of both testes to prevent recurrence). 80% of untreated torsions result in nonviable testicle within 12 hours.

## Educational Objective
Testicular torsion is caused by rotation of the testes around the spermatic cord with consequent ischemia and necrosis. It presents acutely with severe testicular pain, tenderness to palpation, scrotal swelling, and a negative cremasteric reflex. It is a clinical diagnosis, although US can be used to confirm if uncertain. Management includes emergent surgical consultation for detorsion and orchidopexy.

## Common Errors
- **A (Reexamination in 4 hours)**: Delays treatment. Failure to treat promptly increases ischemic time and risk of necrosis and infertility.
- **B (Technetium 99m scan)**: Can detect diminished blood flow but delays definitive treatment. US with Doppler is the more common imaging modality, but when the clinical presentation is classic, imaging should not delay surgery.
- **C (IV antibiotics)**: Treats epididymitis or epididymo-orchitis, not torsion. Epididymitis presents with posterior testicular pain, normal cremasteric reflex, and relief with elevation (Prehn sign). This patient has a high-riding testis — classic torsion.
- **D (Reduction of scrotal mass)**: Describes management of inguinal hernia, not torsion.
